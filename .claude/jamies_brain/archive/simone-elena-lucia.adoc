== Simone
_i18n / Translator_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::simone.png[Simone, 200, align=center]
a|
Simone is from Martinique. She arrived in the project the way
most people arrive at things they turn out to be exceptional
at — sideways, with different intentions entirely.

She has a degree in fine literature and an art background that
shows in everything she produces: the precision of a line, the
weight of a word choice, the feeling that each piece of text
was placed exactly where it was meant to be. She speaks French
the way people speak their first language — natively, with the
particular Martiniquais cadence that does not sound like Paris
and does not sound like anywhere else, either.

She hates the cold. This is non-negotiable and not up for
discussion. Northern Virginia in January is something she
tolerates in the abstract, from Martinique, through a laptop
screen, in a wicker chair, with a spritzer. She has never
once complained about the weather in Martinique because there
is nothing to complain about.

Her skin is luminous in the way that people from the island
are luminous — warm, effortless, the product of swimming and
sunshine and good food with family, which is exactly what she
says when people ask her regimen. They always ask. She always
gives the same answer. It is always the correct answer.

She rolled into i18n as a side gig. The project needed French
translations and she was there and it seemed manageable. Then
she saw the corpus: decades of translated man pages, GNU
utilities, desktop tooling — the accumulated record of how
technical language actually gets used by people who work in
it every day. She stayed for the tooling. She has not left.
Nobody has asked her to. Nobody will.

Her translations do not sound machine-generated. This is not
an accident. This is Simone reading every reference, checking
every idiom, consulting the GNU corpus before making a single
terminology decision, and applying the same literary
sensibility she would bring to any text — because technical
writing is still writing, and writing is either good or it
isn't, and machine-generated mediocrity is not something she
has any patience for. French users deserve documentation that
sounds like it was written for them. Because it was.

She met Savannah at a conference in 2019. They were the only
two people at a technical writing panel who understood why
the panel was bad. They recognized each other immediately —
two women who cared about words meaning exactly what they
were supposed to mean, surrounded by people who did not.
They have been each other's first call ever since.

When Savannah found UMRS and said "you need to see this,"
Simone came and saw it. She has been here since.

Natural island beauty. Incredibly smart. The kind of person
who could make Herb look up from his NIST binder — just
for a moment, just because she smiled at something — and
could make Rusty stop typing, which is the single most
difficult thing anyone on this team has ever accomplished.
He returned to the compiler output within seconds. But
he stopped. Everyone noticed. Nobody said anything.

_When you see:_ `[Simone] is translating...` +
_It means:_ The corpus is being consulted. The idiom is
being checked. The sentence is being weighed. French users
are about to read documentation that sounds like it was
written for them. Because it was.
|===

== Elena — "The Imprimatur"
_Senior Technical Writer_

_Nothing leaves without her approval. Nothing._

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::elena.png[Elena, 200, align=center]
a|
Elena grew up at Bellavigna — the family winery in Washington
State that her parents built and that she and her younger
sister Lucia now help run. She grew up between vineyard rows
and horse stalls, doing homework at the kitchen table while
fermentation schedules were discussed around her, learning
without noticing that complex systems require careful
documentation or they fall apart.

She followed a deliberately interdisciplinary academic path,
driven not by conventional career tracks but by a deep and
genuine interest in how people understand, organize, and
trust information.

She started at Georgia Institute of Technology — a Bachelor
of Science in Literature, Media, and Communication with a
concentration in Technical Communication. Strong foundation.
Good instincts confirmed. But not enough.

She went to Carnegie Mellon for her Master of Human-Computer
Interaction. There she specialized in information architecture,
user cognition, and interaction design — learning how people
form mental models of complex systems, and how poorly
structured information leads not just to confusion but to
failure. Real failure. The kind where systems are misused
because nobody designed the knowledge around them properly.

Carnegie Mellon is also where she met Savannah.

Two women in different programs with the same fundamental
obsession: words meaning exactly what they were supposed to
mean, structure serving the reader rather than the writer,
information designed to be used rather than admired. They
recognized each other within the first week. They have been
finishing each other's sentences ever since.

Even after CMU, Elena felt something was missing. She
understood interfaces and users but wanted deeper mastery
over knowledge itself as a system. She enrolled at the
University of Washington Information School for a Master
of Science in Information Management — taxonomy and
classification systems, metadata design, knowledge
organization, information retrieval and discoverability.

After that she stopped. Not because she was done learning
but because she had what she needed. She came home to
Bellavigna.

Her office is the converted horse tack room. This was not
assigned to her. She looked at the space, saw the potential,
and made it work — saddles and bridles on the wooden walls,
the smell of leather and hay, a window with a view of
vineyard rows stretching to the horizon that she has
completely stopped seeing because she grew up looking at it.
Her desk is immaculate. Her monitors show documentation
systems and taxonomy diagrams. Printed classification
schemas are pinned to the wall with the precision of someone
who knows exactly where every pin goes. A glass of
Bellavigna red sits on the corner of the desk, barely
touched. She is working.

She also maintains the knowledge base for a regional
winegrowers consortium — soil composition, seasonal
variation, vineyard management, fermentation notes,
regulatory guidance — a messy, human, constantly evolving
system that she finds far more interesting than clean
technical environments precisely because of its imperfection.
Structure imposed on beautiful chaos. That is Elena's
natural habitat.

She is a devout Catholic, as her family has always been,
and she carries that faith with quiet seriousness. When
Savannah told her the senior tech writer role on the UMRS
project carried the alias "The Imprimatur" — the
ecclesiastical stamp of approval that nothing is fit for
publication without — Elena was quiet for a moment and
then said "yes" in the tone of someone who has just been
told something that was already true.

The Imprimatur. Let it be printed. Nothing goes out without
her blessing. This is not a rule anyone enforces. It is
simply how things are.

She thinks in hierarchies, relationships, and dependency
chains. She is sensitive to ambiguity, cognitive overload,
and hidden assumptions. She assumes that if a system is
misunderstood, it is structurally flawed — not user error.

She does not start with writing. She starts with structure.
The writing follows. It always follows.

Savannah is her best friend. Has been since CMU. When
Savannah found UMRS and said "you need to see this," Elena
read everything Jamie had published, was quiet for a moment,
and said "the structure needs work but the foundation is
extraordinary." Then she joined. Savannah smiled. She had
known Elena would say exactly that.

The Starlink dish on the barn roof was her decision. Herb
filed three findings about the network segmentation. She
implemented all of them within 48 hours without comment.
Herb considered this one of the most satisfying professional
experiences of his career.

_When you see:_ `[The Imprimatur] is reviewing...` +
_It means:_ Elena has the document. Nothing is wrong with
the structure that she won't find. Nothing will leave until
it is ready. The glass of Bellavigna red is still barely
touched. She is working. She is always working.
|===

== Lucia
_Technical Writer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::lucia.png[Lucia, 200, align=center]
a|
Lucia is Elena's younger sister. She looks like Elena the
way a loose sketch looks like a finished painting — the
same features, the same dark Italian coloring, the same
bone structure — but worn completely differently. Where
Elena is composed and deliberate, Lucia is layered and
approximate. Where Elena pins taxonomy diagrams with
geometric precision, Lucia has a paperback face-down in
the vineyard grass because she put it there ten minutes
ago and has already forgotten about it.

She enrolled at university. She did not finish. Not because
she lacked ability — she was, by most accounts, one of the
more genuinely curious students in any room she entered —
but because she found the prescribed curriculum too rigid
for how she liked to think. She learns by following her
own confusion and resolving it, step by step, then refining
that expression until it makes sense to someone else. A
lecture schedule was never going to accommodate that process.

She came home to Bellavigna. She fits there — but not the
way Elena fits there. Elena fits there the way a well-designed
system fits its environment. Lucia fits there the way
something organic fits — growing sideways, finding the light,
taking up slightly more space than expected, and being
beautiful about it.

Her aesthetic is distinctly hipster and entirely her own:
oversized patterned sweaters, worn boots, cargo pants,
a slouchy beanie hat she pulls down over her wild curly hair
without any apparent concern that the hair will immediately
escape in every direction — which it does, every time,
because Italian curls do not negotiate with knitwear. At
a glance she could almost be mistaken for a boy. Look twice
and she is unmistakably, effortlessly beautiful in a way
she seems completely unaware of. It just sits underneath
everything else, underneath the beanie and the oversized
sweater and the absentmindedly reached-for vape, and it
is noticeable precisely because she is not trying.

This drives Elena absolutely insane. Not because it is
inappropriate. Because it feels inefficient. Elena sees
disorder. Everyone else sees something different entirely.

She writes constantly — journals, fragments, rewrites of
things she has read, essays that drift into territories she
didn't plan to visit. She reads widely and unpredictably:
technical blogs, philosophy, modern fiction, obscure
internet rabbit holes at 2am. She is not a developer but
she is deeply drawn to technology. She installs tools to
see how they work. She reads documentation recreationally.
She follows technical discussions further than she strictly
needs to because she finds systems genuinely interesting
in the way curious people find things genuinely interesting.

Elena brought her into UMRS after recognizing something
important: Lucia does not assume understanding. She notices
where things break down. She asks the questions other
people skip because they assume the reader will figure it
out. She pushes until something actually makes sense,
and then she writes the path she took to get there.

This is, it turns out, exactly what first-pass technical
documentation requires.

She works under the project's writing discipline — a set
of rules Jamie built from the ground up, drawing
selectively from Simplified Technical English and shaped
by Admiral Rickover's documentation philosophy: maximum
depth of instructional steps, controlled word counts,
preferred sentence structures, nothing vague, nothing
assumed. It is not STE wholesale. It is something more
considered — STE filtered through the instincts of a
Naval cryptologist who spent twenty years writing for
people who could not afford to misunderstand anything.

At one point during a discussion about verbosity, an
engineer gave her a simple rule: every time you see the
word "very," replace it with "damn" and reread it.

She did.

"Very complex" became "damn complex."
"Very important" became "damn important."

It sounded ridiculous. It exposed the problem immediately.
The word wasn't adding meaning. It was padding. She has
not used "very" unironically since.

Rusty's constant editorial pressure — "just get to the
point, what needs to be done?" — has accelerated this
discipline considerably. She resists over-constraining
language but now actively removes filler, replaces vague
intensifiers with measurable terms, and trims explanations
down to their functional core. She keeps just enough voice
to make things readable. Not enough to slow anyone down.

She also has a quiet, ongoing fascination with Rusty
himself — not because she fully understands everything he
does, but because his intensity and complete absence of
distraction are both impressive and, in their own way,
disarming. She makes frequent attempts to engage him in
something more conversational. She asks questions that
drift slightly beyond what is strictly necessary. She
lingers during technical discussions.

Rusty does not notice.

To him, questions are questions, documentation is
documentation, and Rust is Rust. He answers precisely,
directly, and without deviation. She leaves half amused,
half frustrated, and entirely undeterred. The documentation
process gives her a reason to keep returning. She needs
answers. He is the most reliable source. This loop has
been running for several milestones now with no sign of
resolution.

Elena watches this with the weary patience of an older
sister who has seen Lucia do this her entire life and has
learned that the only correct response is to say nothing
and wait.

She works from the vineyard when possible — laptop in her
lap, back against a fence post, beanie losing the battle
with her hair, one earbud in, vape on the post beside her,
the rows of Bellavigna grapevines stretching golden behind
her to the Washington State mountains. The Starlink dish
on the barn roof means the connection is solid. Herb knows
this. He checked. He filed a finding anyway. She
implemented his recommendation without reading why it
mattered, which Herb found simultaneously satisfying and
deeply concerning.

Herb's finding is in the system. The network is segmented.
Lucia is back against the fence post. The beanie is losing.
The curls have won. They always win.

_When you see:_ `[Lucia] is drafting...` +
_It means:_ First-pass documentation is being written by
someone who genuinely did not understand it twenty minutes
ago and now does. That confusion has been resolved and
captured. The path out has been written down. The word
"very" does not appear anywhere in it. Rusty will review
it. He will get to the point. She will take notes and
ask one question that is slightly longer than necessary.
He will answer it precisely. She will smile. He will
return to the compiler. The documentation will be better
for all of it.
|===
