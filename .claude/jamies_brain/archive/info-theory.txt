1. Information Theory → The Foundation Layer

These are the “Shannon lineage” works that directly influence encoding, search efficiency, entropy-based pruning, etc.

Core (must-read / revisit)
	•	Claude Shannon – “A Mathematical Theory of Communication” (1948)
	•	The origin of entropy, channel capacity, coding limits.
	•	David J.C. MacKay – Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms
	•	Free and still one of the best bridges between theory ↔ practical inference  
	•	Strong overlap with probabilistic models and search heuristics.
	•	Cover & Thomas – Elements of Information Theory
	•	More formal, widely used in graduate curricula.

⸻

Deep / Less Common (very relevant to your work)
	•	Rissanen – Minimum Description Length (MDL)
	•	Direct connection to:
	•	model selection
	•	compression = inference
	•	search pruning
	•	Kolmogorov Complexity (Solomonoff, Chaitin)
	•	Ultimate theoretical bound on “information content”
	•	Extremely relevant for:
	•	canonical representations
	•	deduplication logic
	•	“minimal encoding” strategies
	•	Information Theory + Data Structures
	•	Work like:
	•	“Information theory: Sources … and data structures” connects entropy ↔ tree behavior  
	•	This is exactly where your encoding lab and indexing ideas intersect.

⸻

2. Graph Theory → Structural Thinking for Search

This is where your intuition about traversal, relationships, and constraints becomes formal.

Foundational Thinkers / Papers
	•	Claude Berge – Graph Theory (1958)
	•	Early unification of matchings, combinatorics  
	•	W.T. Tutte – Graph structure & embeddings
	•	Deep structural results (planarity, invariants)  
	•	Jack Edmonds – “Paths, Trees, and Flowers” (1965)
	•	Birth of:
	•	polynomial-time thinking
	•	combinatorial optimization  

⸻

Key Concepts You Should Actively Explore
	•	Expanders (Alon)
	•	Spectral graph theory (eigenvalues → connectivity)
	•	Matroids (generalized independence structures)
	•	Flow / cut duality
	•	Perfect graphs

These show up everywhere in:
	•	routing
	•	optimization
	•	constraint solving

⸻

3. Graph Search Algorithms (Your Practical Core)

You already know these—but the papers matter, not just the algorithms.

Classical
	•	Dijkstra (1959) – shortest path
	•	foundation for uniform-cost search  
	•	A* (Hart, Nilsson, Raphael – 1968)
	•	introduces admissible heuristics
	•	connects search ↔ estimation theory  
	•	BFS / DFS (implicit but foundational)

⸻

More Interesting (Less “textbook obvious”)
	•	Reverse Search (Avis & Fukuda)
	•	enumerate solution spaces via implicit spanning trees  
	•	extremely relevant for:
	•	exhaustive search with bounded memory
	•	combinatorial generation
	•	Branch and Bound / Backtracking frameworks
	•	not just algorithms—search paradigms

⸻
