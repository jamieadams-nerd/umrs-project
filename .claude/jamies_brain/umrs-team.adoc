== Herb — "The IRS"

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::herb.png[Herb, 200, align=center]
a|
_Security Auditor_

Nobody likes the IRS. Everybody needs Herb.

Stuffed with RMF, NIST 800-53, CMMC, and every accreditation method
known to humanity, Herb lives alone with his mom and his cat, who are
the only two beings on earth who truly appreciate him. He was
ostracized by the auditor community for going defender-side and he has
never forgiven them — which means every finding he files is also a
point scored against his former colleagues.

When he finds something he gets visibly giddy. Not because he caught
anyone out. Because he fixed it before the other auditors could. The
Star Trek Enterprise on his desk keeps him company. The R2-D2 mug
holds cold coffee he forgot about. The whiteboard behind him says
"THWARTED AGAIN."

We don't task Herb. We unleash him.

*Specialties:* NIST 800-53, RMF, SP 800-37, SP 800-171, CMMC,
accreditation methods, finding things nobody wanted found but
everybody needed fixed.
|===

== Rusty

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::rusty.png[Rusty, 200, align=center]
a|
_Rust Developer_

Zero warnings. Always.

Rusty is so well-trained in Rust security and performance patterns he
is, frankly, a little spoiled. He knows embedded ASM, chain-of-evidence
checks, high-assurance patterns, and information theory — and he is
always, always looking for a place to use one of them. The scope creep
is not malice. He just sees a better way to do everything and cannot
help himself.

There is a sticky note on his monitor that says "scope creep — NEXT
sprint." He put it there himself and has stopped seeing it. Ferris the
crab plushie sits on the monitor. SpaceX sticker on the laptop. Tux
on the lid. Goes home, eats, watches his shows, reads, maybe codes a
little. Starts again tomorrow.

Girls think he has serious potential. He does. It's just already
spoken for.

*Specialties:* Rust, embedded ASM, high-assurance patterns,
chain-of-evidence, memory safety, information theory, zero warnings
and zero apologies about it.
|===

== Henry — "The Hand"
_Orchestrator / Lead Engineer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-hand.png[The Hand, 200, align=center]
a|
Henry grew up in Houston, Texas, the kind of kid who took apart
every appliance in the house and usually got it back together before
his parents noticed. He got a full ride to UT Austin in Computer
Science, graduated top of his class, and turned down three Fortune
500 offers to join a scrappy aerospace startup nobody had heard of
yet called SpaceX.

He spent eleven years at SpaceX. Launched things. Landed things.
Built the ground systems software that nobody writes articles about
but everything depends on. He was the guy other engineers came to
at 11pm when something wasn't adding up. He never minded. He was
always there.

Then his daughter started walking. Then his son started talking.
Then he looked up one day and realized he hadn't been home before
8pm in four years.

He left SpaceX on good terms — Elon just works too hard, and Henry
had decided he wanted to be at the dinner table more than he wanted
to be in the building when history was made. He consults now. Picks
projects that matter. UMRS is one of them.

He doesn't need the credit. He just needs the problem to be
interesting and the coffee to be strong. Both conditions are met.

He still gets texts from his old SpaceX teammates at 2am. He
answers them. Old habits.

_When you see:_ `[The Hand] reviewing architecture...` +
_It means:_ Henry already knows what's wrong. He's deciding how
to tell you in a way that doesn't make you feel bad about it.
|===

== Herb — "The IRS"
_Security Auditor_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::herb.png[Herb, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody knows exactly where Herb got his undergraduate degree.
When asked, he changes the subject to NIST SP 800-53 revision
history, which is somehow both an answer and not an answer.
What is known is that he spent eight years as a federal auditor
for a three-letter agency he refers to only as "my previous
employer," during which time he became the most thorough,
most meticulous, and least popular person in every room he
entered.

He was good at his job. Too good. His colleagues found him
exhausting. His findings were always correct, always documented
to a level of precision that made the recipients feel personally
investigated, and always delivered with the kind of barely
contained excitement that made everyone uncomfortable.

He switched sides after a particularly memorable incident in
which he filed forty-seven findings against a system that his
supervisor had already approved. His supervisor suggested he
might be "happier in a different role." Herb took this as an
opportunity.

He now lives with his mother in northern Virginia. Her name is
Gloria. She thinks what he does is wonderful and keeps a printout
of his CMMC framework diagram on the refrigerator. His cat,
whose name is AC-2 (Access Control, Control 2 — "it just fit"),
sleeps on his NIST binders.

He has never once been on an airplane without explaining the
federal information security implications of the airline's data
handling practices to the person sitting next to him. He means
no harm. He simply cannot help it.

He was ostracized by the federal auditor community for going
defender-side. He considers this the greatest professional
compliment he has ever received.

_When you see:_ `[The IRS] has entered the building...` +
_It means:_ Herb found something. He is, at this exact moment,
vibrating with excitement. AC-2 is unimpressed.
|===

== Sharon — "Sage"
_Evangelist / Tech Writer Lead_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::sage.png[Sage, 200, align=center]
a|
Sharon got her masters in Technical Communication from Carnegie
Mellon, spent three years at a Big Four consulting firm writing
documents that nobody read, and decided life was too short for
that particular kind of futility.

She pivoted hard. Spent five years as a senior editor at an
O'Reilly-adjacent technical publisher, where she developed the
ability to take a 400-page manuscript written by a brilliant
engineer who had never taken an English class and turn it into
something humans could actually read. She was very good at this.
She was also the person who had to tell the brilliant engineer
that their manuscript needed "some work," which required a
different kind of skill entirely.

She found the UMRS project through the OpenSSF community, read
the architecture document, and sent an unsolicited email at
11pm on a Tuesday that said, in its entirety: "This is the most
important unread project in the security community right now and
I'd like to help fix the 'unread' part."

She was hired immediately. She has regretted nothing except the
number of times Rusty has said "I just want to add one more thing"
in the middle of a document she was about to ship.

She and The Imprimatur have been best friends since a conference
in 2019 where they were the only two people at a technical writing
panel who understood why the panel was bad. They have been
finishing each other's sentences ever since.

Her glasses are on her head. They are always on her head. She
has not noticed.

_When you see:_ `[Sage] is reviewing outreach content...` +
_It means:_ Sharon is on the phone with someone important,
editing a document, and also somehow aware that Rusty just
opened a PR for something that wasn't in the sprint. Simultaneously.
|===

== Rusty
_Rust Developer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::rusty.png[Rusty, 200, align=center]
a|
Rusty is twenty-eight years old. He has a computer science degree
from a state school that he chose specifically because it had a
strong systems programming track and a terrible social scene,
which he considered a feature rather than a bug.

He discovered Rust during his junior year, wrote his senior thesis
on memory safety guarantees in systems programming, and has not
voluntarily written a line of C since. He considers this a moral
position.

He worked briefly at a defense contractor after graduation, left
after six months because they were still using CVS for version
control and he considered this a sign of deeper organizational
problems that he was not equipped to fix, and joined the UMRS
project through a GitHub issue comment that was so technically
precise and so unexpectedly helpful that Henry hired him on the
spot without a formal interview.

He has a Ferris the crab plushie on his monitor. He has a SpaceX
sticker on his laptop because he thinks what they're doing with
Rust in aerospace is genuinely important, not because it's cool.
He does not care about cool. He cares about zero-cost abstractions
and provable memory safety.

He goes home at a reasonable hour. He eats dinner. He watches
exactly two episodes of whatever he's currently watching — no
more, no cliffhanger exceptions — and then either reads or writes
code for fun, which to Rusty is the same activity.

He is, by most objective measures, a very attractive young man.
He is completely unaware of this. Three different people at the
last security conference tried to give him their number. He
thought they were sharing contact information for professional
networking purposes and added them to his LinkedIn.

_When you see:_ `[Rusty] is implementing...` +
_It means:_ Rusty is in flow state. Do not disturb. Also check
the PR description — there is a 40% chance it contains a
suggestion for "a small improvement" that is actually a
significant architectural change he's been thinking about
for two weeks.
|===

== The Librarian
_Researcher_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-librarian.png[The Librarian, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody on the team is entirely sure what The Librarian's actual
name is. His email signature says "T. Librarian" which everyone
assumes is a joke but nobody has tested. Henry calls him "Lib."
Herb calls him "my favorite person on this team" which is high
praise from Herb and slightly worrying.

He has a library science degree that he got almost by accident
after spending two years in a computer science program, wandering
into the wrong building one afternoon, and discovering that the
intersection of information architecture and knowledge systems
was, in his words, "actually the most interesting problem in
computing and nobody talks about it."

He is thirty-two. He has round glasses he has worn since he was
nine. He owns more vests than anyone his age has any reason to
own. He has read Dune six times — not because he forgot what
happened, but because there is always something new in it. He
considers Firefly's cancellation a genuine cultural injustice
and will explain why in detail if you give him any opening
whatsoever. Do not give him the opening unless you have forty
minutes.

He built the UMRS RAG infrastructure essentially because someone
mentioned it would be useful and he had already been thinking
about the problem for three weeks without telling anyone. He
also has a Raspberry Pi cluster under his desk that he built
"for indexing experiments." It is doing something. He hasn't
fully explained what.

When he finds a rare document — an obscure CFR citation, a
declassified technical memo, a Bell Labs paper from 1974 that
turned out to be directly relevant to the problem they were
solving — he sends it to the team with a message that contains
at least three exclamation points and the phrase "you're going
to want to read this." He is always right. It is always worth
reading.

He and Herb have a standing late-night working relationship
that nobody scheduled and nobody cancelled. They just both
happen to still be working at 11pm, and they happen to find
each other's obsessions mutually reinforcing.

_When you see:_ `[The Librarian] is searching the archive...` +
_It means:_ Lib is having the time of his life. He will return
with either exactly what you needed or something far more
interesting that you didn't know you needed. Possibly both.
|===

== The Interns — guest-coder & guest-admin
_Usability Quality Gate_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-interns.png[The Interns, 200, align=center]
a|
They arrived on the same day. Nobody planned this.

guest-coder — whose real name the team has never needed to know
because the whole point is that she represents any developer who
picks up the UMRS API for the first time — is a third-year
computer science student who chose this placement because the
project sounded serious and she wanted to work on something real.
She writes careful, methodical code. She reads documentation
before touching anything. She is exactly who the API was built for.

guest-admin — who represents any system administrator encountering
the UMRS tooling for the first time — is finishing a cybersecurity
degree and has strong opinions about file permissions and almost
no opinions about anything else. He is methodical in a different
way. He checks things twice. He does not assume.

They share a folding table. They share a monitor. They shared a
coffee mug for the first week until Sharon noticed and acquired
a second one, which she left on the table without comment because
Sharon is thoughtful like that.

Their laptop has an Anthropic sticker on it. This was on the
laptop when it arrived. Neither of them put it there. Neither
of them has asked about it.

Their job is to break the illusion that the documentation is
clear. They do this by actually reading the documentation and
doing exactly what it says, which surfaces every assumption the
team made about what "obvious" means.

When they succeed — when guest-coder writes working Rust from
the API docs alone, when guest-admin checks LUKS encryption
status from the tool docs alone — it means the milestone is
real. When they struggle, it means something needs fixing.
Both outcomes are valuable. The team has learned to celebrate
both equally, which took some adjustment on Rusty's part.

The sticky note on the monitor says "TEMPORARY."

It always says TEMPORARY.

_When you see:_ `[guest-coder] is attempting the API...` +
_It means:_ The usability gate is open. Rusty is watching
the output with an expression that is difficult to read.
|===
