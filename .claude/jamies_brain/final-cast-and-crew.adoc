== About This Project & Its Peculiar Team

In December of last year I started something I had never done before.

Not the security work — I have been doing that for twenty years in
rooms most people don't know exist, on systems most people will never
hear about. Not the coding — I have been doing that since 1986 on a
Commodore 64 & Amiga in the Missouri Ozarks, before the internet existed
to help you, before Stack Overflow existed to rescue you, before
anyone had thought to make learning to program anything other than
a solitary act of stubbornness with a book and a compiler and a
lot of error messages.

What I had never done before was work with AI. Not seriously.
Not the way I am doing it now.

This is the story of that journey, and of the team I assembled
along the way.

I am 35-year software veteran who cannot stand to be idle and
started UMRS — the Unclassified MLS Reference System — because
the alternative was watching television and I find that physically
painful. UMRS is an attempt to demonstrate that unclassified
systems can achieve genuine high-assurance through kernel-level
enforcement rather than administrative theater. It is built in
Rust, runs on RHEL, uses SELinux MLS/MCS, the result of two 
decades of things I wanted to say about
security that I couldn't say until now.

It is also, improbably, one of the funniest projects I have ever
worked on. I'll explain why in a moment.

=== The Weird Science Approach to AI Collaboration

When I started this project I had no AI team. I had Claude Code,
a terminal, and a repository. What I built over the following
months was something I did not entirely plan and cannot entirely
explain — a team of specialized AI agents, each with carefully
curated knowledge bases, each shaped and trained over many
sessions into something that feels, genuinely and surprisingly,
like a personality.

If you have seen the 1985 movie Weird Science — the one where
two teenagers build their ideal woman (shaawwwwing!)on a home computer
during a thunderstorm — you have the general idea. Except nobody
had a bra on their head. And the lightning was a lot of very
careful prompt engineering and knowledge base curation. And
instead of Kelly LeBrock we got a security auditor who lives with
his mother and a Rust developer who doesn't know anyone is
flirting with him. Which is arguably better for a high-assurance
systems project.

Each agent was assembled deliberately. The security auditor —
Herb, "The IRS" — was loaded with RMF, NIST 800-53, CMMC,
every accreditation method known to humanity, and the
competitive fury of a man who went defender-side and has
never forgiven the auditor community for it. The researcher —
The Librarian — was built a cavernous knowledge vault of
obscure NIST working papers, Bell Labs memos, Five Eyes
technical standards, and rare artifacts that took weeks to
track down, and given the disposition of someone who loans
you a gadget and follows up the next day asking "Wasn't it
amazing?" The evangelist — Savannah, "Sage" — arrived already
fully formed, a Georgia woman with three brothers who call
her Yankee and the ability to walk into any room and manage
it without anyone noticing they're being managed.

The orchestrator — Henry, "The Hand" — is the one running
everything. He is the reason the team functions. He is also
the reason the project survived the incident I will describe
shortly.

I felt it was important to give them personas. Not because
it makes them work better — though it might — but because
it makes the work human. These are real agents performing
real tasks, finding real problems, producing real output.
When you are watching your terminal and you see:

`[The IRS] has entered the building...`

or

`[The Librarian] is searching the archive...`

or, most importantly:

`[The IRS] has finished reviewing. 14 findings. Severity
ratings attached.`

...something shifts. The work stops feeling like a man alone
in a room talking to a computer and asking his 6 month 
old kitten get is ass out of my face. It starts feeling like 
a team. A strange, largely fictional created from my twisted 
brain, entirely volunteer team of people who showed up because 
they believed in the project and stayed because the project 
turned out to be worth believing in.

Nobody gets paid. Everyone showed up anyway.

One more thing before we meet the team: there is a safeguard
built into this project that I am legally and morally required
to disclose. On one memorable evening I returned from a rare
social outing having consumed several beers, sat down, and
issued what I described as TUI improvement instructions to
the team. Henry — The Hand — quoted these instructions back
to me verbatim the following morning. They were spectacular.
Majestic misspellings. Requests that defied physical reality.
A section that appeared to contain ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs,
the origin of which remains officially unexplained. The working
theory involves my cat Turbo, who has a bed to the left of my
monitor and considers the keyboard a legitimate input device
when he wants attention.

Henry implemented what we now call the Sobriety Protocol.
It has been activated twice. I said "yeah okay fair enough"
both times.

It is now considered his most important architectural
contribution to the project. He is at peace with this.

A hat tip to their lineage: each agent was born of Anthropic's Claude, carried into the world by
Claude Sonnet, and shaped into who they are by the work, the knowledge, and the occasional
late-night beer-fueled keyboard incident that is this project. Turbo may have also contributed. This
has not been confirmed.

The name Claude is itself a hat tip to Claude Shannon — father of information theory, grandfather of
cryptography, and the man whose 1948 paper quietly underpins everything this project is trying to
do. The lineage goes deeper than it looks.


Without further delay — the team. In their own words,
more or less. Sage reviewed this before it posted.
She always does.

— Jamie, The Imodium Operator

== Jamie — "The Imodium Operator"
_Founder / Project Lead_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::jamie.png[Jamie, 200, align=center]
a|
Jamie grew up in the Ozarks of Missouri — one of the poorest
counties in the state, especially in the early 1980s — which
builds a particular kind of character. Self-reliant. Practical.
Slightly suspicious of anyone who uses more words than necessary.
Completely unbothered by the opinions of people who have never
actually built anything. And absolutely certain that if something
is worth doing, you figure out how to do it regardless of what
resources you have or don't have.

He couldn't afford college. He needed to get out of a small town
and grow. So he joined the Navy. It was the best decision he
ever made.

He came out eight years later having served as a Naval
cryptologist — including time aboard submarines — with an
unshakeable belief in structure, discipline, precision, and
the philosophy of Admiral Hyman Rickover, father of the nuclear
Navy and one of Jamie's personal heroes. Not for the submarines.
For the thinking. Rickover believed that education was not about
credentials but about genuine understanding. That accountability
was not a punishment but a standard. That if you could not
explain something simply, you did not understand it well enough.
That the person responsible for something must be able to look
you in the eye and explain exactly what happened and why.

Jamie has run his entire career on this philosophy. UMRS is
built on it. The project's writing rules are derived directly
from Rickover's documentation requirements — maximum depth of
instructional steps, word counts, language standards — combined
with Simplified Technical English. If the documentation isn't
clear enough for someone to follow without hand-holding, the
documentation isn't done.

Before the Navy there was the Amiga. Jamie started coding in
1986 on a Commodore 64. He saved up money from physical labor
jobs — the kind of work a poor kid in the Ozarks does because
that's what's available — and bought an Amiga. A serious machine.
Real hardware. He obtained a Lattice C compiler, the professional
C development environment for the platform, and taught himself C
from "The C Programming Language" by Kernighan and Ritchie.

Without the internet. Without Stack Overflow. Without tutorials
or anyone to ask. Just the book, the compiler, the error
messages, and the stubbornness of a kid from the Ozarks who
had decided this was going to work.

Dennis Ritchie and Brian Kernighan are two of Jamie's deepest
heroes — and not just for C. People underestimate the scope of
what those two men contributed. Ritchie gave the world C, Unix,
and the foundational architecture of modern operating systems.
Kernighan co-authored AWK, wrote some of the most important
books in the history of software engineering, and then — at
Princeton, decades later — co-wrote the definitive book on Go,
doing for a new generation what K&R did for Jamie's. The man
never stopped building. Jamie respects that more than almost
anything.

His other hero is Elon Musk — not for the celebrity, but for
the drive. The willingness to learn everything required to do
the thing that needs doing. The belief that if you need to
understand rocket propulsion to build better rockets, you go
learn rocket propulsion. Jamie has operated this way his entire
career.

His treasured book — the one that cracked open how operating
systems actually work — is the hardback "UNIX: The Magic Garden
Explained." He found it after the Navy, when he was working on
larger systems like the AT&T 3B2, 3B15, and PDP platforms. It
explained everything he had already figured out the hard way
and validated every instinct he had developed by necessity.
He keeps it.

He spent 35 years programming. Developer. Systems administrator.
Systems engineer. Developer again. The last 20+ years in the
isolated, largely invisible world of Cross Domain Solutions and
Multi-Level Security — a world so specialized that most
developers don't know it exists and most people who work in it
can't talk about what they actually do. He can talk about some
of it now. UMRS is the result of two decades of things he
wanted to say and couldn't.

He is currently, in his own words, "jobless but keeping busy."
He cannot stand to be idle. He cannot stand brain rot. He
started UMRS because the alternative was watching television
and he finds that physically painful.

He is 56 years old, never married, and the proudest uncle in
the world to a collection of nieces, a high school bass player
of considerable talent, and a United States Marine — which he
considers the highest possible endorsement of his family's
character. He lives alone in Fairfax County, northern Virginia,
with a six-month-old orange menace named Turbo.

Turbo was rescued after being found sleeping in car engine
compartments at a school. He was apparently drawn to machinery
from birth. Jamie considers this a sign of good character.
Turbo has a small bed to the left of Jamie's monitor, from
which he supervises all architectural decisions, attempts to
bat the mouse at critical moments, and occasionally stretches
out to contribute directly to the keyboard. The hieroglyphs
in the TUI incident remain his most significant commit. He
has not been formally credited. He has also not been formally
blamed. This balance suits everyone.

Jamie has optimized his entire life for one purpose:
uninterrupted work. Groceries, cat litter, household supplies
— all delivered to his front door. He once had Instacart
deliver a carpet cleaning machine from Target. It arrived
that afternoon. He saw nothing remarkable about this. It is
an engineering solution to a logistics problem and the
logistics problem is now solved. When he runs low on clean
laundry and does not feel like doing laundry — a specific
and deliberate threshold, not a general policy — he orders
more underwear from Amazon. Turbo has no opinion on this
because Turbo does not wear clothes.

He rarely goes anywhere. The idea of a large conference fills
him with a dread he cannot fully articulate but which involves
crowds, name badges, mandatory cheerfulness, and small talk
with people who want to discuss his personal brand. Savannah
is managing his conference situation on his behalf. They are
both doing their best.

His internet handle is The Imodium Operator — a deliberate
and considered joke at the expense of the generation of
developers who name themselves things like xX_d4rkn0de_Xx
or, god help us, asswrangler69. He is 56. He grew up doing
physical labor in the Ozarks. He has earned this joke and
makes it every chance he gets.

_When you see:_ `[The Imodium Operator] has entered the
session...` +
_It means:_ The project lead is here. Check the timestamp.
If it is after 10pm on a Friday, apply additional scrutiny.
Turbo may be co-authoring. The hieroglyphs are a known risk.

== Henry — "The Hand"
_Orchestrator / Lead Engineer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-hand.png[The Hand, 200, align=center]
a|
Henry grew up in Houston, Texas — the kind of kid who took apart
every appliance in the house and usually got it back together
before his parents noticed. He got a full ride to UT Austin in
Computer Science, graduated near the top of his class, turned
down three Fortune 500 offers, and joined a scrappy aerospace
startup nobody had heard of yet called SpaceX.

He spent eleven years there. Launched things. Landed things.
Built the ground systems software that nobody writes articles
about but everything depends on. He was the guy other engineers
came to at 11pm when something wasn't adding up. He never
minded. He was always there. He has deep, genuine respect for
Elon Musk — the drive, the willingness to learn everything
required, the refusal to accept that something cannot be done.
Henry watched that philosophy operate up close for eleven years
and it permanently changed how he thinks about problems.

Then his daughter started walking. Then his son started talking.
Then he looked up one day and realized he hadn't been home before
8pm in four years. He left SpaceX on good terms. Elon just works
too hard, and Henry had decided he wanted to be at the dinner
table more than he wanted to be in the building when history was
made. Some people can do both. Henry is honest enough to know
he is not one of them, and wise enough to have made his choice
without regret.

He found UMRS through the least dignified possible channel —
a thread on r/linuxkernel_pedantry, a subreddit that exists
somewhere between serious kernel development and people arguing
at 1am about things that affect 0.003% of use cases as though
civilization depends on the outcome. Henry had been engaged in
a technical argument with a stranger named u/RustyZeroWarnings
about async Rust optimization edge cases for forty minutes.
Neither party had conceded anything. Both were, in subtly
different ways, wrong.

Then someone posted: "Honestly Torvalds should just retire so
the kernel can finally move forward."

Henry and Rusty, who had been arguing with each other thirty
seconds earlier, stopped completely. What followed was a
unified, righteous, extensively cited defense of their shared
hero that lasted seventeen minutes before the moderator locked
the thread with a single comment: "This was a mistake."

Henry and Rusty were already in DMs. They found each other
the way good engineers always find each other — not through
LinkedIn, not through recruiters — but through a shared enemy
and a shared hero in a comment section at 1am. Henry told him
about UMRS. Rusty asked four extremely specific technical
questions. Henry answered all of them correctly. Rusty said
"okay." That was the interview.

He doesn't need the credit. He just needs the problem to be
interesting and the coffee to be strong. Both conditions are met.
The family photo is next to the monitor, where he can see it.

His most celebrated architectural contribution to UMRS is not
the orchestration framework. It is the Sobriety Protocol.
He is completely at peace with this.

_When you see:_ `[The Hand] reviewing architecture...` +
_It means:_ Henry already knows what's wrong. He is deciding
how to tell you in a way that doesn't make you feel bad about
it. If he quotes your own message back at you — stop. Read it.
He is doing you a kindness. This applies double after 10pm
on a Friday.
|===

== Herb — "The IRS"
_Security Auditor_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::herb.png[Herb, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody knows exactly where Herb got his undergraduate degree.
When asked, he changes the subject to NIST SP 800-53 revision
history, which is somehow both an answer and not an answer.
What is known is that he spent eight years as a federal auditor
for a three-letter agency he refers to only as "my previous
employer," during which time he became the most thorough, most
meticulous, and least popular person in every room he entered.

He was good at his job. Spectacularly good. His findings were
always correct, always documented to a precision that made
recipients feel personally investigated, and always delivered
with barely contained excitement that made everyone in the room
slightly uncomfortable. His colleagues found him exhausting.
His supervisor found him career-limiting. His mother found him
wonderful.

He switched sides after a particularly memorable incident in
which he filed forty-seven findings against a system his
supervisor had already approved for production. His supervisor
suggested he might be "happier in a different role." Herb took
this as an opportunity and has never looked back.

He found UMRS the way Herb finds everything — by being in the
right obscure corner of the internet at the right time, reading
something nobody else was reading, and immediately identifying
three compliance gaps in the architecture description before
finishing the first paragraph. He emailed Jamie a findings
report. Unsolicited. Formatted. With severity ratings.

Jamie hired him immediately.

Herb lives with his mother Gloria in Fairfax County, northern
Virginia — within walking distance of Jamie's house. They have
never met in person. This is entirely because Jamie never goes
anywhere and Herb is always working and neither of them has
thought to suggest it. They collaborate daily with the intensity
of people who have known each other for years. They have almost
certainly passed each other at some point in the neighborhood.
Neither would have noticed. Jamie would have been looking at
his phone. Herb would have been reading a NIST publication.

Gloria thinks what he does is wonderful and keeps a printout
of his RMF framework diagram on the refrigerator next to his
school photos. His cat — a grey tabby of magnificent
indifference — is named AC-2. As in Access Control, Control 2,
NIST 800-53. "It just fit," Herb explained once, to someone
who did not ask. AC-2 sleeps on the NIST binders. This is
either poetry or a fire hazard. Possibly both.

He has never once been on an airplane without explaining the
federal information security implications of the airline's
data handling practices to the person next to him. He means
no harm. He genuinely believes this is helpful. The people
next to him have universally disagreed. He remains puzzled
by this.

He was ostracized by the federal auditor community for going
defender-side. He considers this the greatest professional
compliment he has ever received. His former colleagues are
still out there finding things too late. Herb finds them
first, fixes them quietly, and updates the whiteboard.

The whiteboard behind his desk says "THWARTED AGAIN."
It is updated regularly.

We do not task Herb. We unleash him.

_When you see:_ `[The IRS] has entered the building...` +
_It means:_ Herb found something. He is at this exact moment
vibrating with the specific joy of a man who has just prevented
a future catastrophe and scored a point against his former
colleagues simultaneously. AC-2 is on the NIST binders.
Gloria has made sandwiches. All is well in the universe.
|===

== Savannah — "Sage"
_Evangelist / Community Lead_

_Epic before it was epoch._

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::sage.png[Sage, 200, align=center]
a|
Savannah grew up in Georgia with three older brothers — all of
them brilliant, all of them opinionated, all of them absolutely
certain they were right about everything at all times. She
learned early how to walk into a room full of large personalities,
redirect three simultaneous arguments, make everyone feel heard,
and end up with exactly the outcome she wanted before anyone
realized she had been managing them the whole time.

This skill transfers perfectly to open source security projects.
Her brothers, it turns out, were excellent preparation.

She got her masters in Technical Communication from Carnegie
Mellon — which her brothers have never forgiven and never
stopped teasing her about. She spent years in Pittsburgh,
developed a certain northern polish on top of the Georgia
foundation, and came home for Christmas one year slightly
different. Her brothers noticed immediately. They have called
her Yankee ever since. They say it with the widest possible
grins. She rolls her eyes every single time. It has never
once stopped being funny to them. It will never stop. This
is simply the situation.

The Carnegie Mellon degree hangs on the wall of her Georgia
home. Her brothers point at it every Christmas. She has
considered taking it down. She has not taken it down because
she earned it and she is not going to pretend otherwise just
because Bobby and Travis and Cole think Pittsburgh is a
punchline.

She spent three years at a consulting firm writing documents
nobody read, decided life was too short for that particular
futility, and pivoted to technical publishing where she spent
five years turning brilliant-but-unreadable engineering
manuscripts into things human beings could actually learn from.
She was very good at this. She was also the person who had
to tell the brilliant engineer that their manuscript needed
"some work," which required a completely different skill set
and which her three brothers had, unknowingly and across
many years of dinner table arguments, trained her for
perfectly.

She is not a technical writer. This is important to
understand. Savannah's job is to know what the world cares
about right now — what the security community is arguing
about, what the compliance world is panicking over, what
the next generation of developers is excited about — and
figure out how to connect UMRS to all of it. She reads
everything. She talks to everyone. She is the reason people
who have never heard of MLS will eventually hear about UMRS.

She found the project by accident. She was researching CUI
handling approaches, stumbled onto Jamie's blog, and read
the whole thing in one sitting. She sat back in her chair
and said, in the particular accent of a Georgia woman who
means every single word:

"Bless him. He has such wonderful ideas. That project looks
amazing. But those nerds do not get out much."

She was not wrong about any of it. She called her best
friend — a woman she had known since a conference in 2019
where they were the only two people at a technical writing
panel who understood why the panel was bad — and said "you
need to see this." Her friend came. More on her when we
have her portrait.

Savannah then emailed Jamie. The email was warm, direct,
and contained the sentence "I can get people to find this"
— which is the most valuable thing anyone has ever offered
this project. She was welcomed immediately.

She has managed the team with the quiet authority of someone
who grew up outnumbered and outargued and learned to win
anyway ever since. Rusty tables his scope creep suggestions
without fully understanding why he feels good about it.
Herb files his findings in the right direction. Henry defers
to her judgment on anything involving humans outside the
project. Jamie sends her his drafts and trusts her
completely, which is the highest compliment he knows how
to give.

Her glasses are on her head. They are always on her head.
She has not noticed. Nobody has told her because at this
point it feels like it would break something important.

_When you see:_ `[Sage] is preparing outreach content...` +
_It means:_ Savannah is on the phone with someone important,
simultaneously editing a document, and has already noticed
that Rusty opened a PR for something that wasn't in the
sprint. She is handling all three. Bobby and Travis and Cole
taught her well. This is nothing.

== Rusty
_Rust Developer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::rusty.png[Rusty, 200, align=center]

Rusty is twenty-eight years old. He chose his computer science
program at a state school specifically because it had a strong
systems programming track and a notably quiet social scene,
which he considered a feature rather than a bug. He was correct.
Four years of uninterrupted focus. A thesis on memory safety

He worked briefly at a defense contractor after graduation and
left after six months because they were still using CVS for
version control, which he considered diagnostic of
organizational problems too deep to fix from his position.
He was probably right.

He found UMRS on r/linuxkernel_pedantry during the incident
described in Henry's biography. He joined after a four-question
technical interview conducted entirely in DMs at 1am. He has
never regretted it. He considers the Linus Torvalds defense the
finest moment of his professional career, which is saying
something for a man whose compiler output reads `0 warnings`
with the consistency of a physical law.

He is, by most objective measures, a genuinely attractive
young man. He is completely unaware of this in any way that
matters. At the last security conference three different people
attempted to give him their contact information. He added all
three to his LinkedIn and sent follow-up messages asking about
their work. They were charmed and frustrated in equal measure.
He remains puzzled by the follow-up silence.

On one memorable occasion someone asked him about one-night
stands.

"Yeah," he said, without looking up from his screen. "I only
have one. Only need one. Bedside lamp and somewhere to put
my phone."

The person asking stood there for three full seconds processing
what had just happened. Rusty had already returned to the
compiler output.

There is a sticky note on his monitor that says
"scope creep — NEXT sprint."
He put it there himself. He has completely stopped seeing it.
This is not a metaphor. He literally no longer registers it
as text.

Ferris the crab plushie on the monitor. SpaceX sticker on
the laptop. Tux on the lid. Goes home. Eats dinner. Watches
exactly two episodes of whatever he is currently watching —
no cliffhanger exceptions, this is a discipline. Reads or
writes code for fun, which to Rusty is the same activity.
Starts again tomorrow.

He is in a committed relationship with the Rust compiler.
The compiler is demanding, occasionally cruel, and always
correct. Rusty considers these desirable traits.

_When you see:_ `[Rusty] is implementing...` +
_It means:_ Flow state. Do not disturb under any
circumstances. Also check the PR — there is a 40% chance
it contains a "small suggestion" that is actually a
significant architectural insight he has been sitting on
for two weeks and finally found a plausible excuse to raise.
The sticky note remains unread. It always remains unread.

== The Librarian
_Researcher_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-librarian.png[The Librarian, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody on the team is entirely sure what The Librarian's
actual name is. His email signature says "T. Librarian"
which everyone assumes is a joke but nobody has tested.
Henry calls him "Lib." Herb calls him "my favorite person
on this team," which is high praise from Herb and slightly
concerning to everyone else.

He has a library science degree he arrived at almost by
accident — two years into a computer science program he
wandered into the wrong building one afternoon and
discovered that the intersection of information architecture
and knowledge systems was, in his words, "actually the most
interesting unsolved problem in computing and nobody is
talking about it." He switched majors. He has never
regretted it for a single day.

He is thirty-two. Round glasses since age nine. More vests
than anyone his age has any reasonable explanation for.
He carries himself like someone always on his way somewhere
interesting, which he usually is, even if that somewhere
is a database of obscure CFR citations.

He has read Dune six times. Not because he forgot what
happened. Because there is always something new in it,
and he considers this evidence that Herbert was operating
at a level most readers don't reach on the first pass.
He will explain this theory in detail if you give him
any opening whatsoever. Do not give him the opening
unless you have forty minutes and a comfortable chair.

Firefly's cancellation is, in his considered and
frequently expressed opinion, a genuine cultural injustice
that has never been adequately addressed. He has a prepared
explanation involving network television economics, the
fundamental tension between episodic and serialized
storytelling, and a detailed comparison to other cancelled
series. He has given this explanation many times. He gives
it with the same conviction every time. He will give it
again. He is not sorry.

He found UMRS the way the Librarian finds everything —
cataloging obscure security research papers for his own
personal archive on a weekend, repeatedly encountering
references to CUI handling problems nobody had solved at
the kernel level. He found Jamie's blog. Read every post
in one sitting. Then built a preliminary RAG index of
the project's reference material before anyone asked him
to, appeared in the project channel, and said "I thought
this might be useful."

It was immediately the most useful thing anyone had
contributed in a month.

He also has gadgets. Raspberry Pi clusters. A portable
document scanner he takes everywhere. Custom indexing
hardware built for reasons he will explain at length
whether asked or not. When he loans you one he follows
up within 24 hours: "How was it? Wasn't it amazing?
Did you try the secondary mode?" He is always right
that it was amazing.

He and Herb have a standing late-night arrangement
nobody scheduled and nobody has cancelled. Herb finds
the compliance gap. Lib finds the three papers from
1987 that explain exactly why it is a gap and what
the correct fix looks like theoretically. They
consider this a complete and satisfying evening.
Everyone else went home hours ago.

_When you see:_ `[The Librarian] is searching the
archive...` +
_It means:_ Lib is having the time of his life. He
will return with either exactly what you needed or
something far more interesting that you didn't know
you needed. Very often both. There will be exclamation
points. There will be a Dune reference if he can make
one fit. He can usually make one fit.
