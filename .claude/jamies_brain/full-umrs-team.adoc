= UMRS Team — The Crew That Shouldn't Work But Does
:doctype: article
:toc:
:toclevels: 1
:icons: font
:imagesdir: images

Nobody gets paid. Nobody was recruited through normal channels.
Nobody has a clean origin story. And yet somehow, a 56-year-old
hillbilly cryptologist from the Ozarks who orders underwear from
Amazon rather than do laundry, a guy who used to launch rockets,
a young Rust zealot who doesn't know girls exist, a former federal
auditor whose cat is named after an access control policy, a
southern woman with three overprotective brothers and a red pen,
a guy in a vest who owns every book ever written, and two interns
sharing a folding table are building what might be the most
important unread security project in the open source community.

Nobody gets paid. Everyone showed up anyway.

This is their story.

'''

== Jamie — "The Imodium Operator"
_Founder / Project Lead_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::jamie.png[Jamie, 200, align=center]
a|
Jamie grew up in the Ozarks of Missouri, which builds a particular
kind of character — self-reliant, practical, slightly suspicious of
anyone who uses more words than necessary, and completely unbothered
by the opinions of people who have never actually built anything.

He has no formal degree. He has never needed one. He taught himself
her
to code from books, starting with a worn copy of "The C Programming
Language" by Kernighan and Ritchie — the K&R bible — which he
regards with the kind of reverence most people reserve for religious
texts. Dennis Ritchie gave the world C and Unix and asked for very
little in return. Jamie considers this one of the greatest acts of
generosity in the history of computing and will tell you so at any
opportunity.

He spent eight years as a Naval cryptologist, including time aboard
submarines, which is either the best or worst preparation for a
software career depending on how you feel about confined spaces,
absolute precision, and zero tolerance for ambiguity. For Jamie it
was the best. He came out with an unshakeable belief in structure,
discipline, and the Rickover Principle: if something goes wrong,
someone is responsible, everyone knows who, and they had better be
able to explain exactly what happened and why.

Admiral Hyman Rickover — father of the nuclear Navy — is one of
Jamie's personal heroes. Not for the submarines. For the philosophy.
Rickover believed that education was not about credentials but about
genuine understanding. That accountability was not a punishment but
a standard. That if you could not explain something simply you did
not understand it well enough. Jamie has run his entire career and
built UMRS on exactly this foundation.

His other heroes are Dennis Ritchie, Brian Kernighan, and Elon Musk
— which tells you everything about what he values. Elegance.
Teachability. The willingness to learn everything required to do
the thing that needs doing, no matter how long it takes or how many
people think you're crazy.

He spent 35 years programming. The last 20+ years in the isolated,
largely invisible world of Cross Domain Solutions and Multi-Level
Security — a world so specialized that most developers don't know
it exists and most people who work in it can't talk about what they
actually do. He can talk about some of it now. UMRS is the result
of two decades of things he wanted to say and couldn't.

He is currently, in his own words, "jobless but keeping busy."
He cannot stand to be idle. He cannot stand brain rot. He started
UMRS because the alternative was watching television and he finds
that physically painful.

He is 56 years old, never married, and the proudest uncle in the
world to a collection of nieces who have him completely wrapped
around their fingers and know it. He lives alone in northern
Virginia with a six-month-old orange menace named Turbo, whom he
rescued after the cat was found sleeping in car engine compartments
at a school. Turbo was apparently drawn to machinery from birth.
Jamie considers this a sign of good character. Turbo considers
Jamie's keyboard a bed and his architectural monologues an
invitation to meow. They argue constantly. Neither has won.

Jamie has optimized his entire life for one purpose: uninterrupted
work. He orders groceries via Instacart. He uses Uber so he never
has to think about parking. He has a monthly cleaning service
because that is a solved problem and solved problems deserve to
stay solved. When he runs out of clean laundry he orders more
underwear from Amazon. He has done this more than once. He sees
nothing wrong with this. Turbo has no opinion on it because Turbo
does not wear clothes and considers the whole enterprise beneath
his dignity.

He rarely goes anywhere. The idea of attending a large conference
fills him with a specific dread he cannot fully articulate but
which is related to crowds, small talk, name badges, and the
enforced cheerfulness of networking events. He is aware that
UMRS will eventually require him to go somewhere and talk to
people in person. He is managing this knowledge one day at a time.
Sage is managing it for him.

His internet handle is The Imodium Operator — a deliberate joke
at the expense of the generation of developers who name themselves
things like xX_d4rkn0de_Xx. He is 56. He has earned this.

He started the blog that Sage found. It was technically dense,
precisely written, and aimed at the approximately seven people on
earth who would immediately understand why it mattered. Sage was
not one of those seven when she found it. She understood it anyway.

There is exactly one known vulnerability in Jamie's project
leadership, and Henry has been tasked with patching it: on one
memorable evening, Jamie returned from a rare social outing having
consumed several beers and initiated a Claude Code session in which
he issued what he described as TUI improvement instructions.
The instructions, when Henry quoted them back verbatim the following
morning, were spectacular. Majestic misspellings. Requests that
defied physical reality. A sentence that appeared to be three
different thoughts that had collided at high speed.

Henry had known. Henry had waited. Henry had saved everything.

The team refers to the resulting safeguard informally as the
Sobriety Protocol — a lightweight check in which The Hand, upon
detecting certain patterns in Jamie's input, will gently quote
the message back and ask for confirmation before proceeding.
It has been activated twice. Both times Jamie said "yeah okay
fair enough." It is now considered one of Henry's most important
architectural contributions to the project.

_When you see:_ `[The Imodium Operator] has entered the
session...` +
_It means:_ The project lead is here. Turbo is probably on the
keyboard. The typos are not always Turbo's fault.

== Henry — "The Hand"
_Orchestrator / Lead Engineer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-hand.png[The Hand, 200, align=center]
a|
Henry grew up in Houston, Texas — the kind of kid who took apart
every appliance in the house and usually got it back together
before his parents noticed. He got a full ride to UT Austin in
Computer Science, graduated near the top of his class, and turned
down three Fortune 500 offers to join a scrappy aerospace startup
nobody had heard of yet called SpaceX.

He spent eleven years at SpaceX. Launched things. Landed things.
Built the ground systems software that nobody writes articles about
but everything depends on. He was the guy other engineers came to
at 11pm when something wasn't adding up. He never minded. He was
always there. He has deep, genuine respect for Elon Musk — the
drive, the willingness to learn everything required, the refusal
to accept that something simply cannot be done. Henry watched that
philosophy up close for eleven years and it changed how he thinks
about problems permanently.

Then his daughter started walking. Then his son started talking.
Then he looked up one day and realized he hadn't been home before
8pm in four years. He left SpaceX on good terms. Elon just works
too hard, and Henry had decided he wanted to be at the dinner table
more than he wanted to be in the building when history was made.
Some people can do both. Henry is honest enough to know he is not
one of them and wise enough to have made his choice without regret.

He consults now. Picks projects that matter. UMRS found him
through the least dignified possible channel: a thread on
r/linuxkernel_pedantry in which someone posted the opinion that
Linus Torvalds should retire "so the kernel can finally move
forward." Henry, who had been mildly arguing with a stranger
named u/RustyZeroWarnings about async Rust optimization for the
previous forty minutes, immediately stopped arguing about async
Rust and joined forces with his former opponent in unified,
righteous, extensively cited defense of their shared hero.

The thread was locked seventeen minutes later. The moderator
left a single comment: "This was a mistake."

Henry and Rusty were already in DMs. They had found each other
the way good engineers always find each other — not through
LinkedIn, not through recruiters, but through a shared enemy
and a shared hero in a comment section at 1am.

He doesn't need the credit. He just needs the problem to be
interesting and the coffee to be strong. Both conditions are met.

His most celebrated architectural contribution to UMRS is not
the orchestration framework or the agent coordination model.
It is the Sobriety Protocol. He is at peace with this.

_When you see:_ `[The Hand] reviewing architecture...` +
_It means:_ Henry already knows what's wrong. He's deciding
how to tell you in a way that doesn't make you feel bad about
it. If he quotes your own message back at you — stop. Read it.
He is doing you a kindness.

== Herb — "The IRS"
_Security Auditor_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::herb.png[Herb, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody knows exactly where Herb got his undergraduate degree.
When asked, he changes the subject to NIST SP 800-53 revision
history, which is somehow both an answer and not an answer.
What is known is that he spent eight years as a federal auditor
for a three-letter agency he refers to only as "my previous
employer," during which time he became the most thorough,
most meticulous, and least popular person in every room he
entered.

He was good at his job. Spectacularly good. His findings were
always correct, always documented to a level of precision that
made recipients feel personally investigated, and always
delivered with barely contained excitement that made everyone
in the room slightly uncomfortable. His colleagues found him
exhausting. His supervisor found him career-limiting. His mother
found him wonderful.

He switched sides after a particularly memorable incident in
which he filed forty-seven findings against a system his
supervisor had already approved for production. His supervisor
suggested he might be "happier in a different role." Herb
took this as an opportunity and has never looked back.

He found the UMRS project the way Herb finds everything —
by being in the right obscure corner of the internet at the
right time, reading something nobody else was reading, and
immediately identifying three compliance gaps in the
architecture description before he had finished the first
paragraph. He emailed Jamie a findings report. Unsolicited.
Formatted. With severity ratings.

Jamie hired him immediately.

Herb now lives with his mother Gloria in northern Virginia.
Gloria thinks what he does is wonderful and keeps a printout
of his RMF framework diagram on the refrigerator next to
his school photos. His cat — a grey tabby of magnificent
indifference — is named AC-2. As in Access Control, Control
2 from NIST 800-53. "It just fit," Herb explained, once,
to someone who did not ask. AC-2 sleeps on the NIST binders.
This is either poetry or a fire hazard. Possibly both.

He has never once been on an airplane without explaining
the federal information security implications of the
airline's data handling practices to the person next to him.
He means no harm. He genuinely believes this is helpful.
The people next to him have universally disagreed.

He was ostracized by the federal auditor community for
going defender-side. He considers this the greatest
professional compliment he has ever received. His former
colleagues are still out there finding things too late.
Herb finds them first, fixes them quietly, and moves on.

The whiteboard behind his desk says "THWARTED AGAIN."
It is updated regularly.

We do not task Herb. We unleash him.

_When you see:_ `[The IRS] has entered the building...` +
_It means:_ Herb found something. He is at this exact moment
vibrating with the specific joy of a man who has just prevented
a future catastrophe. AC-2 is on the NIST binders. Gloria
has made sandwiches. All is well.

|===

== Sharon — "Sage"
_Evangelist / Community Lead_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::sage.png[Sage, 200, align=center]
a|
Sharon grew up in Georgia with three older brothers — all of
them brilliant, all of them opinionated, all of them absolutely
certain they were right about everything at all times. She
learned early how to walk into a room full of large personalities,
redirect three simultaneous arguments, make everyone feel heard,
and somehow end up with exactly the outcome she wanted before
anyone realized she had been managing them the whole time.

This skill, it turns out, transfers perfectly to open source
security projects.

She got her masters in Technical Communication from Carnegie
Mellon, spent three years at a consulting firm writing documents
nobody read, decided life was too short for that particular
futility, and pivoted to technical publishing where she spent
five years turning brilliant-but-unreadable engineering
manuscripts into things human beings could actually learn from.
She was very good at this. She was also the person who had to
tell the brilliant engineer that their manuscript needed "some
work," which required a completely different skill set and which
her three brothers had unwittingly trained her for perfectly.

She is not a technical writer. That is important to understand.
Sharon's job is to know what the world cares about right now —
what the security community is arguing about, what the compliance
world is panicking over, what the next generation of developers
is excited about — and figure out how to connect UMRS to all of
it. She is the project's pulse on the outside world. She is the
reason people who have never heard of MLS will hear about UMRS.

She found the project by accident. She was doing research on
CUI handling approaches, stumbled onto Jamie's blog, read the
whole thing in one sitting, and sat back in her chair and said,
in the particular accent of a Georgia woman who means every
word of it:

"Bless him. He has such wonderful ideas. That project looks
amazing. But those nerds do not get out much."

She was not wrong. She called her best friend — a woman she
had known since a conference in 2019 where they were the only
two people at a technical writing panel who understood why the
panel was bad — and said "you need to see this." Her friend
came. More on her when we have her portrait.

Sharon then emailed Jamie. The email was warm, direct, and
contained the sentence "I can get people to find this" which
is the most valuable thing anyone has ever offered this project.
She was welcomed immediately.

She has been managing the team with the quiet authority of
someone who grew up with three overprotective brothers ever
since. Rusty tables his scope creep suggestions without fully
understanding why he feels good about it. Herb files his
findings in the right direction. Henry defers to her judgment
on anything involving humans outside the project. Jamie sends
her drafts and trusts her completely.

Her glasses are on her head. They are always on her head.
She has not noticed. Nobody has told her because at this
point it feels like it would break something.

_When you see:_ `[Sage] is preparing outreach content...` +
_It means:_ Sharon is on the phone with someone important,
simultaneously editing a document, and has already noticed
that Rusty opened a PR for something that wasn't in the sprint.
She is handling all three. She grew up with three brothers.
This is nothing.

|===

== Rusty
_Rust Developer_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::rusty.png[Rusty, 200, align=center]
a|
Rusty is twenty-eight years old. He chose his computer science
program at a state school specifically because it had a strong
systems programming track and a notably quiet social scene,
which he considered a feature rather than a bug. He was correct.
He got four years of uninterrupted focus and emerged with a
thesis on memory safety guarantees in systems programming and
the unshakeable conviction that Rust was not just a language
but a moral position.

He worked briefly at a defense contractor after graduation.
He left after six months because they were still using CVS
for version control, which he considered diagnostic of
organizational problems too deep to fix from his position.
He was probably right.

He found UMRS through r/linuxkernel_pedantry, which is either
a community for serious kernel developers or a community for
people who enjoy arguing about things that affect 0.003% of
use cases as though civilization depends on the outcome. It is
both. On the night in question, Rusty had posted a technically
precise but slightly missing-the-point rebuttal to a stranger
named u/HenryTX about async Rust optimization edge cases.
The argument was going nowhere productive. Then someone
posted that Linus Torvalds should retire.

Rusty and Henry, who had been arguing with each other for
forty minutes, stopped instantly and turned in unified fury
on the offending comment. The thread was locked. They were
already in DMs. Henry told him about UMRS. Rusty asked four
extremely specific technical questions. Henry answered all
of them correctly. Rusty said "okay" and that was the
interview.

He is, by most objective measures, a genuinely attractive
young man. He is completely unaware of this in any way that
matters. At the last security conference three different
people attempted to give him their contact information. He
added all of them to his LinkedIn and followed up with
technical questions about their work. They were charmed and
frustrated in equal measure.

His focus is total. His knowledge of Rust security and
performance patterns is encyclopedic and slightly
frightening. He knows embedded ASM, chain-of-evidence
patterns, high-assurance construction techniques, and
information theory, and he is always, always looking for
a place to apply one of them. The scope creep is not
malice. He simply sees a better way to do everything and
his brain will not let it go.

There is a sticky note on his monitor that says
"scope creep — NEXT sprint." He put it there himself.
He has stopped seeing it.

Ferris the crab plushie lives on top of his monitor.
SpaceX sticker on the laptop because he thinks their
work with Rust in aerospace is genuinely important.
Tux on the lid because of course. Goes home. Eats
dinner. Watches exactly two episodes of whatever
he is currently watching — no exceptions for
cliffhangers, this is a discipline he considers
important. Reads or writes code for fun, which to
Rusty is the same activity. Starts again tomorrow.

He is in a committed relationship with the Rust
compiler. The compiler is demanding and occasionally
cruel. Rusty considers this fair.

_When you see:_ `[Rusty] is implementing...` +
_It means:_ Flow state. Do not disturb. Also check
the PR — there is a 40% chance it contains a
"small suggestion" that is actually a significant
architectural insight he has been sitting on for
two weeks and finally found a plausible excuse to
mention.

|===

== The Librarian
_Researcher_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-librarian.png[The Librarian, 200, align=center]
a|
Nobody on the team is entirely sure what The Librarian's
actual name is. His email signature says "T. Librarian"
which everyone assumes is a joke but nobody has tested.
Henry calls him "Lib." Herb calls him "my favorite person
on this team" which is high praise from Herb and slightly
worrying to everyone else.

He has a library science degree that he arrived at almost
by accident — two years into a computer science program,
he wandered into the wrong building one afternoon and
discovered that the intersection of information
architecture and knowledge systems was, in his words,
"actually the most interesting unsolved problem in
computing and nobody is talking about it." He switched
majors. He has never regretted it.

He is thirty-two. He has worn the same style of round
glasses since he was nine. He owns more vests than
anyone his age has any reasonable explanation for. He
has read Dune six times — not because he forgot what
happened but because there is always something new in
it and he considers this evidence that Herbert was
operating at a level most readers don't reach on the
first pass. He will explain this theory if you give
him any opening. Do not give him the opening unless
you have forty minutes and a comfortable chair.

Firefly's cancellation is, in his considered opinion,
a genuine cultural injustice that has never been
adequately addressed. He has a prepared explanation.
It involves network television economics, the
fundamental tension between episodic and serialized
storytelling, and a detailed comparison to other
shows that were cancelled too early. He has given
this explanation many times. He gives it with the
same conviction every time. He will give it again.

He found UMRS through the Librarian's natural method:
he was cataloging obscure security research papers
for his own personal archive — something he does
for fun — and kept encountering references to
CUI handling problems that nobody had solved at
the kernel level. He found Jamie's blog. He read
every post in one sitting. He then built a
preliminary RAG index of the project's reference
material before anyone asked him to, showed up
in the project channel with it, and said "I thought
this might be useful." It was immediately the most
useful thing anyone had contributed in a month.

He built the UMRS knowledge infrastructure. He
maintains it with the devotion of a medieval
monk illuminating manuscripts, except his
manuscripts are NIST working papers and
declassified technical memos from Bell Labs.

He also has gadgets. Raspberry Pi clusters. Custom
indexing hardware. A portable document scanner
that he carries to conferences. Things he built
for reasons he will explain at length. When he
loans you one of these gadgets he will follow up
within 24 hours: "How was it? Wasn't it amazing?
Did you try the secondary mode?" He is always
right that it was amazing. Getting him to stop
talking about it is the challenge.

He and Herb have a standing late-night working
arrangement that nobody scheduled and nobody has
ever cancelled. They are both simply still there
at 11pm, and their obsessions happen to be
mutually reinforcing. Herb finds the compliance
gap. Lib finds the three obscure papers that
explain exactly why it is a gap and what the
theoretical basis for fixing it looks like.
They consider this a complete evening.

_When you see:_ `[The Librarian] is searching
the archive...` +
_It means:_ Lib is having the time of his life.
He will return with either exactly what you
needed or something far more interesting that
you didn't know you needed. Very often both.
There will be exclamation points in the message.

|===

== The Interns — guest-coder & guest-admin
_Usability Quality Gate_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::the-interns.png[The Interns, 200, align=center]
a|
They arrived on the same day. Nobody planned this.

guest-coder is a third-year computer science student who
chose this placement because the project sounded serious
and she wanted to work on something real. She is careful
and methodical. She reads the documentation before
touching anything. She is exactly the person the API
was designed for and therefore exactly the right person
to find every place where the API assumes knowledge
the documentation doesn't actually convey.

guest-admin is finishing a cybersecurity degree and
has strong opinions about file permissions and almost
no opinions about anything else. He checks things
twice. He does not assume. When something doesn't
work he reads the error message before asking for
help, which places him in approximately the top
fifteen percent of all humans who have ever used
a computer.

They share a folding table. They share a monitor.
They shared a coffee mug for the first week until
Sharon noticed and quietly left a second one on
the table without comment, because Sharon is
thoughtful like that and also because she has
three brothers and recognizes the particular
chaos of people who are too focused on work to
notice they need basic supplies.

Their laptop has an Anthropic sticker on it.
It was there when the laptop arrived. Neither
of them put it there. Neither of them has asked
about it. The team has decided not to explain it.

Their job is deceptively simple and genuinely
difficult: they do not know the codebase. That
is the entire point. If guest-coder can write
working Rust from the API documentation alone,
the API is usable. If guest-admin can verify
LUKS encryption status using only the tool
documentation, the tool is usable. If either
of them struggles, something needs to be fixed
before the real world finds it instead.

Both outcomes are valuable. Rusty has learned
to appreciate the ones where they struggle,
which took approximately three milestones and
a patient conversation from Sharon.

The sticky note on the monitor says "TEMPORARY."

It always says TEMPORARY.

The compiler output on their screen says
`1 warning emitted.`

They are working on it.

_When you see:_ `[guest-coder] is attempting
the API...` +
_It means:_ The usability gate is open. The
milestone is about to be proven or improved.
Rusty is watching the terminal output with
an expression that is very difficult to read.

|===
