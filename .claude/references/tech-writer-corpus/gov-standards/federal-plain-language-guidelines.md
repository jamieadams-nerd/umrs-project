## What Is Plain Language?

Plain language is grammatically correct and universally understood language that includes
complete sentence structure and accurate word usage. Plain language is not unprofessional
writing or a method of "dumbing down" or "talking down" to the reader.

Writing that is clear and to the point helps improve all communication: it takes less time to
read and comprehend. Clear writing tells the reader exactly what the reader needs to know
without using unnecessary words or expressions.

Plain language qualities include:
- Use of personal pronouns
- Active voice
- Logical organization
- Easy-to-read design features such as bullets and tables

---

## Statutory Requirement

The Plain Writing Act of 2010 (H.R. 946/P.L. 111-274) requires federal agencies to communicate
with anticipated readers, recipients, and stakeholders in a clear, simple, meaningful, and
jargon-free manner.

Documents covered by the Act include:
- Documents necessary for obtaining any federal government benefit or service
- Documents that provide information about any federal government benefit or service
- Documents that explain to the public how to comply with a requirement that the federal
  government administers or enforces

## I. Think About Your Audience

### a. Identify and write for your audience

First, consider who your readers are. Consider what your readers need to know and want to know.
Organize content to answer their questions. Write at a reading level appropriate to your intended
audience.

### b. Address separate audiences separately

If you have more than one audience for your document, address each one separately.

---

## II. Organize

### a. Organize to meet your readers' needs

Think through the questions your audience is likely to ask and then organize your material in
the order they'd ask them. For regulations and other complex documents, create a comprehensive
table of contents. Your table of contents should be a reliable road map that users can follow
to quickly find the information they need.

Regulations frequently address processes. Chronological organization is best for process
information. Present the steps chronologically, in the order your user and your agency will
follow them.

Strong, logical organization includes an introduction followed by short sentences and paragraphs.
Organize messages to respond to your readers' interests and concerns.

### b. Address one person, not a group

Write as if you are speaking directly to one reader.

---

## III. Active Voice

Active voice makes it clear who is supposed to do what. It eliminates ambiguity about
responsibilities. Not "It must be done," but "You must do it."

Passive voice obscures who is responsible for what and is one of the biggest problems with
government documents. The active voice eliminates confusion by forcing you to name the actor
in a sentence. This construction makes clear to the reader who is to perform the duty.

**Examples:**
- Passive: "The form must be submitted."
- Active: "You must submit the form."

---

## IV. Short Sentences and Conciseness

Wordy, dense construction is one of the biggest problems in government writing. Nothing is more
confusing to the user than long, complex sentences containing multiple phrases and clauses.

**Target sentence length:** Average 15–20 words.

Sentences that are simple, active, affirmative, and declarative hold readers' interest.

Unnecessary words waste your audience's time. To address the problem, writers must become more
critical of their own writing. They must consider whether they need every word.

---

## V. Word Choice

Prefer the short word to the long. Prefer the familiar word to the far-fetched. Prefer the
concrete word to the abstraction. Prefer the Saxon word to the Romance word.

Use common, everyday words whenever possible. Avoid slang.

**Abbreviations:** One legal scholar calls abbreviations a "menace to prose." Abbreviations were
once intended to serve the audience by shortening long phrases. However, abbreviations have
proliferated so much in current government writing that they constantly require the reader to
look back to earlier pages, or to consult an appendix. The best solution is to find a simplified
name for the entity you want to abbreviate.

---

## VI. Tables and Design Features

Use design features to make documents easier to scan:
- Bullets
- Tables
- Headers and subheaders
- White space

Tables are especially useful for presenting complex regulatory information clearly. When
information has a consistent structure across multiple items, a table communicates the
relationships far more efficiently than prose.

## Key Principles Summary

| Principle | Guidance |
|-----------|----------|
| Audience | Identify your reader; write to their level |
| Organization | Use chronological order for processes; answer readers' questions in order |
| Active voice | Name the actor; say who does what |
| Sentence length | Average 15–20 words |
| Word choice | Short, familiar, concrete words; avoid jargon |
| Design features | Use bullets, tables, headers, and white space |
| Personal pronouns | Use *you* and *we* to be direct |
| Abbreviations | Minimize; use simplified names instead |
