# Tutorials — Diátaxis Framework

Source: https://diataxis.fr/tutorials/
Retrieved: 2026-03-12

## Definition

A tutorial is an **experience** that takes place under the guidance of a tutor, always **learning-oriented**. It is a practical activity in which students learn by doing something meaningful toward an achievable goal. The purpose is to serve the user's acquisition of skills and knowledge—their study—rather than helping them accomplish a task.

## The Tutorial as a Lesson

A tutorial functions as a lesson, representing a relationship between teacher and pupil. In this relationship, "learning takes place as the pupil applies themself to tasks under the instructor's guidance."

### Obligations of the Teacher

The teacher bears nearly all responsibility in this contract. A lesson must be:

- **Meaningful** — the student needs a sense of achievement
- **Successful** — the student must be able to complete it
- **Logical** — the path must make sense
- **Usefully complete** — the student encounters all necessary actions, concepts, and tools

### The Problem of Tutorials

Tutorials are rarely executed well because they are genuinely difficult and often misunderstood. In documentation, the teacher cannot interact and respond to students as in an ideal lesson. Tutorial creation demands considerable effort, and product evolution requires constant updating. Changes cascade throughout the entire narrative structure.

## Key Principles

### Show the Learner the Destination

Inform learners at the outset what they will achieve. As one principle states: "In this tutorial we will create and deploy a scalable web application."

### Deliver Visible Results Early and Often

Each step should produce a comprehensible result. "Every step the learner follows should produce a comprehensible result, however small."

### Maintain a Narrative of the Expected

Use phrases like "You will notice that…" and "After a few moments, the server responds with…" to provide feedback confirming the learner is on the right path.

### Point Out What the Learner Should Notice

Learning requires reflection. Prompt learners to observe environmental signs: "Notice that… Remember that… Let's check…"

### Target the Feeling of Doing

Accomplished practitioners experience a sense of purpose, action, thinking, and result flowing together. Tutorials should tie tasks together so learners discover this integrated feeling.

### Encourage and Permit Repetition

Learners naturally return to exercises that give successful results. "Repetition is not the best teacher - sometimes it's the only teacher." Create opportunities for students to repeat steps.

### Ruthlessly Minimise Explanation

"A tutorial is not the place for explanation." Learners focus on following directions and getting results. Explanation distracts from doing. Provide links to detailed explanations rather than inline discussion.

### Focus on the Concrete

Guide learners through concrete actions and results step by step. General patterns emerge naturally from concrete examples because "The one thing our minds do spectacularly well is to perceive general patterns from concrete examples."

### Ignore Options and Alternatives

Maintain focused guidance toward the conclusion. Ignore diversions and alternative approaches—save them for another time.

### Aspire to Perfect Reliability

Tutorials must inspire confidence. "A learner who follows your directions and doesn't get the expected results will quickly lose confidence." The tutorial should be so well-constructed that things cannot go wrong.

## The Language of Tutorials

Effective tutorials use specific linguistic patterns:

- **"In this tutorial, we will…"** — establishes shared journey
- **"First, do x. Now, do y."** — provides clear, unambiguous direction
- **"The output should look something like…"** — sets expectations
- **"Notice that…"** — prompts observation
- **"You have built a secure, three-layer [system]…"** — acknowledges accomplishment

## Applied to Food and Cooking

The cooking lesson analogy illustrates tutorial principles. When teaching a child to cook, success is not the culinary outcome but what the child gains: knowledge about food safety, knife handling, timing, and terminology. "The child learns all this by working alongside you in the kitchen; in its own time, at its own pace, through the activities you do together."

A short lesson where the child achieves something small and enjoys it provides value, laying foundation for future learning.
