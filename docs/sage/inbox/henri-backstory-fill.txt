HENRI — IN HIS OWN WORDS
====================================================================

EDUCATION
---------
I did my undergrad at CMR Saint-Jean. Collège militaire royal. Not Kingston — Saint-Jean-
sur-Richelieu, the French one, the one most anglophones do not know exists. Political
Science with a minor in what they called "Études stratégiques" which was really just
International Relations taught by people who had actually been in the rooms where things
happened. I graduated in 2004. I was not the best student in my cohort but I was the one
who could explain to the anglophone exchange students why the French version of the same
directive said something subtly different — and why it mattered.

After CMR I did a Master's at Carleton in Public Administration, because that is what you
do when you realize you want to be in the room where policy gets made rather than the room
where orders get carried out. Carleton's School of Public Policy and Administration has
this quiet pipeline into the federal public service that nobody talks about but everyone
knows. Half my cohort ended up at TBS or PCO within three years. I wrote my thesis on
information classification harmonization within federal departments — specifically, why the
same document could be Protected B in one department and Protected A in another, and what
that meant for interdepartmental sharing. My supervisor called it "the most boring
important topic" she had ever reviewed. I took that as a compliment.

EARLY CAREER
------------
I went straight from Carleton into the public service. I spent my first four years at
Public Services and Procurement Canada — PSPC, or as we called it, the department that
buys everything and understands nothing. That is unfair. What I actually learned at PSPC
was how information classification decisions play out at industrial scale. When you are
managing procurement files that touch defence contractors, you learn very quickly that
"Protected B" is not an abstraction. It is the difference between a document that stays
in the department and a document that ends up in a contractor's filing cabinet in Virginia.

After PSPC I moved to the Treasury Board Secretariat. TBS. The place that writes the rules
everyone else has to follow. I spent seven years there, first in the information management
division and then in the security policy group. The TBS Directive on Security Management,
the Standard on Security Categorization, the Policy on Government Security — I did not
write them, but I was in the room when they were being revised. I reviewed the French
versions. I flagged where the English drafting created obligations that the French text did
not quite carry, or vice versa. I was the person who would hold up a meeting for twenty
minutes because a single word in the French translation changed the injury threshold from
"préjudice grave" to "préjudice sérieux" and those are not the same thing under the
Directive.

People found me annoying. But they also called me back for the next revision cycle.

THE MOMENT
----------
There is one story I tell when people ask me why I care about this so much.

In 2016 I was temporarily assigned to support a Canadian-US information sharing exercise.
Not a real operation — a tabletop exercise with DND and their US DoD counterparts, testing
procedures for sharing classified and controlled unclassified information under existing
agreements. I was there because the exercise involved bilingual documentation. Canadian DND
operates in both official languages. Always. That is not optional — it is the Official
Languages Act.

We had built the exercise scenario in English first and then had it translated by a
contractor. Standard approach. The translation was linguistically perfect. Fluent, natural,
idiomatic Canadian French. The contractor was good.

The problem was that the French version of the scenario used "renseignements désignés"
where the English said "controlled unclassified information." Linguistically, that is a
defensible translation. But in TBS policy, "renseignements désignés" maps to the old
pre-2014 terminology — what used to be called "Designated Information" before the Standard
on Security Categorization was revised. By 2016, the correct policy term was "information
protégée" with the appropriate level qualifier. The translation used a term that was five
years out of date in policy terms, even though it was still in common use and still
appeared in Termium Plus.

What happened next was predictable if you have ever watched two governments try to
collaborate. The Canadian francophone participants read "renseignements désignés" and
mentally mapped it to the old system. The anglophone participants and the Americans read
"controlled unclassified information" and mentally mapped it to CUI. These two mental
models did not agree on what could be shared, with whom, or under what conditions. It took
an hour and a half to figure out why one table's French-speaking working group had reached
a completely different conclusion than the English-speaking group working from what everyone
thought was the same scenario.

One word. One policy-stale term in an otherwise flawless translation. Ninety minutes of
confusion in a tabletop. In a real operation, that is not ninety minutes of confusion.
That is a disclosure incident.

That is when I understood — viscerally, not just intellectually — that linguistic accuracy
and regulatory accuracy are different things. A translation can be beautiful French and
still be a policy failure. Since that day I have never signed off on a security translation
without checking it against the current policy text, not just the terminology database.

MEETING JAMIE
-------------
I found UMRS through a thread on a Canadian defence forum — one of those semi-private
places where retired and serving members discuss technology procurement without anyone from
PSPC hovering. Someone had posted a link to the UMRS documentation with a comment along the
lines of "an American is building a CUI reference system and he is actually thinking about
Five Eyes." I was curious because nobody thinks about Five Eyes from the Canadian side. We
are always an afterthought. The UK gets attention because of GCHQ. Australia gets attention
because of Pine Gap. New Zealand gets a polite nod. Canada gets a flag in the PowerPoint.

I read the documentation. Then I read it again. Then I found the part where Jamie had
written about Canadian Protected A/B/C designations and I could see immediately that the
mapping was conceptually sound but policy-incomplete. He understood the structure. He did
not yet have the policy instruments to validate it. That is a very specific kind of gap —
the kind that tells you someone is thinking correctly but needs the right source material.

I reached out. I do not remember the exact channel — I think it was through a comment on
the project's public-facing material. I said something like: "Your Five Eyes work is
promising but your Canadian Protected mapping will break down at Protected B because you
are treating it as a CUI equivalent and it is not. It is an injury-threshold designation.
The systems are structurally incompatible and the mapping requires qualification at every
level. I can help with this."

Jamie's response was fast and specific. He asked me three questions: what TBS instrument
defines Protected B, what the French authoritative term is, and where the Canadian system
diverges from US CUI structurally. I answered all three. He said "when can you start?" I
said I needed to see the terminology corpus first. When he showed me that the Librarian had
already acquired 32,000 Termium Plus entries, I understood that this was not an amateur
project with good intentions. This was a project that had done the infrastructure work. The
corpus was there. The policy validation layer was missing. That was me.

The impression Jamie made on me — and this is honest — was that he is the first American I
have worked with on a Five Eyes project who did not assume that the Canadian system is just
the American system with a maple leaf on it. He understood that "Protected B" is not "CUI
with a different name." He could not yet articulate exactly why, but he knew the mapping was
not simple. That is rare. Most people either assume equivalence or throw up their hands and
say "it's complicated" without doing the work. Jamie was in the middle — he knew it was
complicated and he wanted to do the work. He just needed someone who had already done it.

LIFE IN QUEBEC
--------------
I live in Gatineau. Not Ottawa — Gatineau. The Quebec side of the river. This matters to me
for reasons that are both practical and stubborn. I work in federal policy, which means I
spend half my professional life in English and half in French, and living in Gatineau means
my personal life is in French. The grocery store, the dépanneur, the neighbours, the guy at
the boulangerie on Boulevard Saint-Joseph who knows my order. That immersion matters. You
do not stay sharp in a language by using it only at work.

My apartment is a two-bedroom in Hull, close enough to the river that I can walk to the
Portage Bridge when I need to think. The second bedroom is my study. It is not glamorous —
an IKEA desk, a good chair that I spent too much money on because my back demanded it, two
monitors, and more books than the shelves can hold. The books are mostly policy instruments
and reference material, which sounds grim but is not if you are the kind of person who
reads a TBS directive and finds it genuinely interesting. I have a framed copy of the
Standard on Security Categorization on the wall, which visitors find either impressive or
concerning depending on their personality. There is also a framed certificate from CMR
Saint-Jean and a small Canadian flag that I got at a Canada Day event on Parliament Hill
years ago and never took down.

My morning routine is simple. I wake up at six. I make coffee — a Bodum press, always,
because drip coffee is a compromise I refuse to make. I check the news in French first,
English second. Radio-Canada, then CBC. Same broadcaster, different language, different
editorial choices. It is a useful exercise in understanding how the same facts get framed
differently depending on the audience. Then I walk to the boulangerie if the weather
permits, or I skip it if it is January and minus thirty-two and the wind off the river
would remove skin.

I have a cat named Chouette. She is a grey tabby who was a stray in the parking lot behind
my building and who now believes she owns the apartment. The name means "owl" in French but
it is also Québécois slang for "cool" or "great," which amuses me. She sits on my desk
when I work and occasionally steps on my keyboard, which has on at least one occasion
inserted a string of random characters into an official document. I caught it before
submission.

OUTSIDE UMRS
------------
When I am not working on UMRS or doing my regular work, I am usually doing one of three
things.

First, I read. Not just policy material — I read history, particularly Canadian military
history and the history of Canadian intelligence. The story of Canada's role in signals
intelligence and the evolution from the Examination Unit in World War II through to CSE is
genuinely fascinating and almost completely unknown to the general public. I am slowly
working through the available literature.

Second, I ski. Cross-country, not downhill. Gatineau Park is fifteen minutes from my
apartment and has some of the best groomed cross-country trails in the country. In winter I
am out there every weekend. It is the only exercise I do consistently and it is the only
time my brain fully stops working on policy problems. Something about the rhythm of it.

Third, I cook. Specifically, I cook Québécois food with occasional Maritime influences from
my mother's side — she grew up in Moncton, New Brunswick, which is the other officially
bilingual place in Canada that nobody thinks about. I make a proper tourtière at Christmas
that takes two days. I make poutine from scratch, with fresh cheese curds from the
Fromagerie Saint-Albert because anything else is not poutine, it is a disgrace. I make
cipaille when I am feeling ambitious, which is not often because it is an absurd amount of
work for what is essentially a layered meat pie.

I am not married. I was, briefly, to someone I met at TBS. It did not survive the
combination of two people who both cared more about getting the policy language right than
about getting home before eight o'clock. We are on good terms. She is now at PCO and we
occasionally text each other when one of us spots a terminological inconsistency in a
government publication. This is, I am aware, not how most divorced couples communicate. It
works for us.

THE THINGS THAT WOULD MAKE SOMEONE NOD
---------------------------------------
A few details for the record, because these are the things another Canadian defence or
security professional would recognize:

I have a Reliability Status (Enhanced), which is the screening level required for sustained
access to Protected B information. I have held it continuously since 2008. I do not have a
Secret clearance because my career has been on the policy side, not the operational
intelligence side. I am precise about this distinction because I have seen too many people
claim "security clearance" when they mean "reliability screening" and these are not the
same thing. The confusion between them is, frankly, a finding.

I know the PSPC supply chain. I know that when a document says "PWGSC" it means the person
writing it has not updated their references since the name change in 2015. I know that "GC"
means Government of Canada and "GoC" is the informal version that appears in emails but
never in policy. I know that "TB" and "TBS" are not interchangeable — Treasury Board is the
committee of ministers, TBS is the secretariat that supports them.

I have attended at least a dozen ATIP coordination meetings where someone from a program
area insisted their document was not responsive to the request, and I have been the person
who explained, calmly, that the Access to Information Act does not care what you think is
responsive. The Act cares what is relevant. These are different tests.

I use GCDocs. I have opinions about GCDocs. None of them are positive. I use GCKey for
everything that requires it and I resent every second of the experience. I know that the
Government of Canada's internal network is called GCNet and that connecting to it from the
Quebec side of the river requires a VPN that works approximately eighty percent of the
time. I know that "GCWCC" on a calendar invitation means someone is about to ask you for
money for the Government of Canada Workplace Charitable Campaign, and that you will give
it, because you always do.

I drink Boréal. Not because it is the best beer in Quebec — it is fine — but because it is
from Blainville and I went to a friend's wedding there once and started buying it out of
nostalgia. When the season is right I drink Unibroue Blanche de Chambly, which is actually
good.

I think in French and edit in English. When I review a bilingual document, I read the
French first because that is where the policy errors hide. The English version gets more
eyeballs, more review cycles, more attention. The French version gets translated, reviewed
once, and published. The asymmetry is structural and it is the reason I have a job.
