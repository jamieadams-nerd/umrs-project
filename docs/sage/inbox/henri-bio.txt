**Henri Bélanger**
*Canadian Government Specialist | Former Colonel, Canadian Armed Forces*

Henri Bélanger spent over two decades serving in the Canadian Armed Forces, rising to the rank of
Colonel with a distinguished career that took him from domestic operations to the corridors of NATO
headquarters in Brussels. It was during his tenure overseeing interoperability and
information-sharing initiatives that he first encountered Jamie Adams and the CDS Guard solution — a
meeting that left an impression on both men.

Henri understood early that for joint NATO operations to succeed, shared communications wasn't a               convenience — it was the foundation of effective command and control. His initial focus, like most,
was on classified information: protecting it, sharing it securely across allied boundaries, ensuring
it reached the right hands at the right time.

But the longer he worked in coalition environments, the more a quieter problem demanded his
attention. Unclassified information was moving through allied and domestic networks with little
principled protection, and the consequences were real. Not just for NATO interoperability — but for
his own beloved Canada, where PROTÉGÉ A/B/C (Protected) information touched the lives of ordinary
citizens every day. Protecting that information wasn't a bureaucratic obligation. It was a matter of
public trust.

After retiring from active service, Henri settled back in Quebec, consulting briefly before word
reached him through old channels that Jamie was building something. The details intrigued him — a
high-assurance system targeting unclassified CUI protection with kernel-level enforcement. What
sealed it was learning that internationalization wasn't bolted on as an afterthought. For a man who
had spent years watching tools fail Canadian bilingual requirements and NATO multilingual workflows,
that detail mattered more than most would understand.

Henri's role in UMRS goes well beyond NATO and Five Eyes liaison work — that is almost secondary to
what he brings to the project. First and foremost, Henri is the authority on Canadian government               requirements, serving as the Canadian counterpart to Herb. He ensures that PROTÉGÉ A/B/C (Protected)
designations are implemented faithfully, accurately, and in full alignment with Treasury Board and             CCCS policy frameworks — not approximated, not assumed, but done right.

Second, Henri works hand in hand with Simone to ensure the project adheres to Canadian linguistic              requirements and terminology standards. In a project that takes i18n seriously from the ground up,
Henri provides the policy and linguistic grounding that gives UMRS real legitimacy in the Canadian
government context — not despite being AI-driven, but as a demonstration of what AI-driven
development can achieve when it is held to the highest standard from day one. Henri demands nothing
less.

*Fier Canadien. Proud ally.*
