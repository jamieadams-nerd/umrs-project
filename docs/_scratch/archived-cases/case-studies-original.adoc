= High-Assurance Failure Case Studies
:toc: left
:description: Real-world failure examples that demonstrate the cost of missing integrity, provenance, and access control in unclassified systems — and how high-assurance design would have changed outcomes.

// NIST SP 800-53 AC-4, AC-6, AU-9, SI-7
// NSA RTB RAIN

[IMPORTANT]
====
The case summaries below are based on publicly available reporting,
government press releases, and court documents. Numerical figures (dollar amounts,
record counts, durations) are estimates or ranges from public sources and may not
reflect final adjudicated facts. They are presented to illustrate the pattern of
failure, not as verified statistics.
====

The dominant failure in unclassified systems is not sophisticated external attack.
It is _too much legitimate access_, _informal integrity controls_, and _no enforceable
provenance_ — conditions where access controls are social rather than technical, and
where data can be modified, copied, or used outside its intended scope without detection.

This page presents representative cases by CUI category and maps each failure to the
UMRS design property that would have changed the outcome.

== The Recurring Pattern

Across sectors, the same failure structure appears:

. Data is decision-critical — it drives safety, policy, or legal outcomes.
. Access is broad for convenience — "anyone in operations can query everything."
. Integrity controls are social, not technical — rely on policy compliance, not enforcement.
. Provenance is informal — records can be created, backdated, or modified without attribution.
. Audit exists, but enforcement does not — logs are present but cannot prove what happened.

When something goes wrong, the organization cannot answer: who saw what, when, and
why? Was this the original data? Was it modified? Was it used outside its intended
scope? Decisions lose legitimacy as a result.

MLS-style enforcement is not primarily about secrecy. It is about constraining
legitimate access, proving provenance, preserving evidentiary value, and allowing
systems to be trusted by design.

== CUI//PRIVACY — Over-Access and Data Segmentation Failures

=== OPM Breach (2015)

The U.S. Office of Personnel Management stored background investigation records
for federal employees in a centralized, minimally segmented system. Attackers
gained access and exfiltrated records including SSNs, financial history, and personal
data on millions of individuals. The data was not classified, but was highly sensitive.

*Failure:* "One big database" architecture. Once inside, lateral movement was
unconstrained. Sensitivity subsets (SSN vs employment history vs foreign contacts)
were not separated by label or access tier.

*UMRS control pattern:* MLS labeling of PII subsets by sensitivity. Purpose-based
access: "support lookup" and "bulk export" require different security contexts.
Mandatory high-signal audit events for record access and export.

=== Equifax Breach (2017)

Equifax stored credit data on approximately 147 million individuals. An unpatched
vulnerability allowed access to a broad swath of stored records. Settlement with
regulators exceeded $700 million.

*Failure:* Insufficient segmentation. Exploiting one vulnerability provided access
to large amounts of data that had no additional integrity or access boundaries.

*UMRS control pattern:* Data labeling by sensitivity class. Anomaly detection on
access volume and time patterns. Cryptographic, append-only logging for privileged
queries and exports.

=== Twitter Internal Misuse (2019)

Two Twitter employees were charged with using their legitimate access to user account
data to identify users for foreign intelligence purposes. This was not a hacking failure.

*Failure:* "Too much legitimate access." Access was role-based but not compartmentalized
by data sensitivity, purpose, or explicit handling rules. Access was not treated as a
privileged, audited action.

*UMRS control pattern:* Sensitivity labels on personal account records. Purpose
separation: operational access and export-level access require different security
contexts. High-signal audit trails on privileged queries.

== CUI//WATER and CUI//ENV — Environmental Data Integrity Failures

=== Flint Water Crisis

Internal EPA memos documented concerns about lead contamination in Flint's water
supply. Sampling procedures were altered, internal reports were not escalated or
enforced, and external stakeholders were given incomplete information. The public health
impact was severe and long-term.

*Failure:* Data integrity and custody. Water quality measurements were not treated as
controlled objects with enforced review boundaries. Informal handling allowed concerns
to be suppressed.

*UMRS control pattern:* IMA/EVM appraisal on measurement records — a "Water Quality
Memo" becomes a signed, measured artifact. Unauthorized modification is detected by
the kernel. Append-only audit log creates a forensic record of what was seen and when.

=== Falsified Drinking Water Reports (DOJ Cases)

Multiple federal cases have involved falsification of drinking water sampling results
and laboratory reports submitted for Clean Water Act compliance. In one case, a
Mississippi lab director was sentenced for falsifying over 1,000 water-related
laboratory reports.

*Failure:* No tamper-evident provenance linking sample collection to reported results.
Corrections could be made without attribution. Raw instrument data was not cryptographically
bound to submitted compliance reports.

*UMRS control pattern:* Chain of custody for samples — who collected, where, when,
and handling steps — signed. Immutable lab results with corrections as append-only
attributed events. Automated raw-to-reported pipeline with cryptographic binding so
a regulator-facing report traces to raw instrument output.

== CUI//TRAN and CUI//CRIT — Inspection Record Falsification

=== Rail Transit Inspection Falsification (WMATA, MBTA, LIRR)

Federal investigations and prosecutions have alleged that rail transit inspectors
at multiple agencies falsified bridge, tunnel, and track inspection records —
reporting inspections as completed when they were not performed, or altering records
after the fact.

*Failure:* Inspection records were mutable, not tamper-evident. No cryptographic
binding between the inspection event, the evidence (photos, sensor data), and the
written record. Corrections could be made silently.

*UMRS control pattern:* "Inspection record" is a controlled object — write-once,
with amendments as append-only attributed events. Separation of duties: inspector
cannot be sole approver. Evidence binding: photos and sensor proofs are hashed and
tied to the record at the time of collection. Mandatory access control around who
can close an inspection item.

=== Boeing 737 MAX and FAA Disclosures

The DOJ documented that Boeing employees made misleading statements and omissions
to the FAA regarding the 737 MAX MCAS system, impeding safety oversight.

*Failure:* Provenance and accountability gaps in safety disclosures. No immutable
record of what was communicated to regulators and when. Engineering, safety, and
program management approvals were not structurally separated.

*UMRS control pattern:* Regulator disclosure packet treated as a controlled artifact
with change control. Mandatory internal attestations. Immutable record of what was
disclosed when. Separation between engineering and safety approval authorities.

== CUI//CRIT — Pipeline Safety Record Falsification

Federal cases have involved safety inspectors fabricating hundreds of gas pipeline
test results and covering the falsification with paperwork. State-level investigations
have alleged similar falsification at regulated utilities.

*Failure:* Field test results were not bound to the device, user, time, or location
of the actual test. Paperwork could be created retrospectively. No independent
attestation separated the inspector from the result.

*UMRS control pattern:* Field test results as immutable, signed events (device identity,
user identity, timestamp, location). Strong separation: contractor generates results,
independent verifier attests. Append-only corrections with mandatory reason codes.
Auditable chain from raw measurement to reported compliance summary.

== Engineering and Safety Systems

=== Mars Climate Orbiter (1999)

A $125M spacecraft was lost because one engineering team used metric units while
another used English units in navigation software. The mismatch was not detected until
the spacecraft entered the Martian atmosphere on the wrong trajectory.

*Failure:* Data was reused outside its original context without verified provenance
or unit-type enforcement. There was no mechanism to prevent a "metric-labeled"
calculation from being consumed by a "English-units-expecting" process.

*UMRS control pattern:* MLS labels can be used to tag data by processing context.
A process expecting data in one labeled context cannot ingest a file labeled for
a different context — the kernel denies the flow. Type-safe access controls prevent
context mismatches at the data layer, not just at the application layer.

=== Deepwater Horizon Alarm Bypass

Testimony in post-incident proceedings revealed that the Deepwater Horizon general
alarm system had been placed in "bypass" mode to prevent false alarms from waking
workers. When methane gas reached the drill floor, the alarm did not trigger. Workers
had no warning before the explosion.

*Failure:* A critical safety configuration was modified for operational convenience
without enforced integrity controls. The bypass state was not treated as a controlled
object with audit trail and change authorization requirements.

*UMRS control pattern:* IMA/EVM Appraisal would treat the alarm configuration file as
a controlled object. Any unauthorized modification to the "bypass" state would be
detected or blocked by the kernel — the safety-critical configuration cannot be silently
altered.

== Digital Evidence and Legal Proceedings

=== United States v. Vayner

A federal conviction was vacated on appeal because the prosecution could not establish
provenance for social-media content offered as evidence — specifically, it could not
prove the content was created by the defendant and had not been altered.

*Failure:* No provenance controls. There was no cryptographic binding of origin,
integrity, and custody metadata to the artifact.

=== Lorraine v. Markel

A frequently cited e-discovery case in which a court excluded emails and digital
records because the parties failed to properly authenticate them. The records existed;
their authenticity could not be established.

*UMRS lesson:* These failures are not about secrecy. They demonstrate that when
systems do not treat digital artifacts as controlled objects with enforced lifecycle
rules, those artifacts lose evidentiary weight. Custody trust and content trust must
be structurally enforced, not assumed.

== Summary: UMRS Properties and the Failures They Address

[cols="2,3"]
|===
| Failure class | UMRS design property

| "Too much legitimate access" — lateral movement, bulk export
| MLS sensitivity labels and category-based compartments constrain access by type of
  data, not just job title

| Falsified records — inspection reports, sampling results, safety configurations
| IMA/EVM Appraisal — files are measured at the kernel level; unauthorized modifications
  are detected or blocked before the file is used

| Provenance gaps — cannot prove origin, chain of custody, or what was seen when
| Kernel-level audit (SELinux AVC, IMA measurement log) creates tamper-visible
  provenance records not writable by userland processes

| Context mismatches — data reused outside its intended purpose or processing context
| MLS labels as a machine-enforced tag on the processing context; the kernel prevents
  flows between mismatched labeled domains

| Accountability gaps — cannot attribute modifications to a specific identity and time
| Signed audit log chains; EVM-protected xattrs; append-only kernel measurement log
|===
