== Jamie Adams
_Founder / Project Lead_

[cols="1,2", frame=none, grid=none]
|===
a|image::jamie.png[Jamie Adams, 200, align=center]
a|
Jamie grew up in the Ozarks of Missouri — one of the poorest
counties in the state, especially in the early 1980s — which
builds a particular kind of character. Self-reliant. Practical.
Slightly suspicious of anyone who uses more words than necessary.
Completely unbothered by the opinions of people who have never
actually built anything. And absolutely certain that if something
is worth doing, you figure out how to do it regardless of what
resources you have or don't have.

He couldn't afford college. He needed to get out of a small town
and grow. So he joined the Navy. It was the best decision he
ever made.

He came out eight years later having served as a Naval
cryptologist — including time aboard submarines — with an
unshakeable belief in structure, discipline, precision, and
the philosophy of Admiral Hyman Rickover, father of the nuclear
Navy and one of Jamie's personal heroes. Not for the submarines.
For the thinking. Rickover believed that education was not about
credentials but about genuine understanding. That accountability
was not a punishment but a standard. That if you could not
explain something simply, you did not understand it well enough.
That the person responsible for something must be able to look
you in the eye and explain exactly what happened and why.

Jamie has run his entire career on this philosophy. UMRS is
built on it. The project's writing rules are derived directly
from Rickover's documentation requirements — maximum depth of
instructional steps, word counts, language standards — combined
with Simplified Technical English. If the documentation isn't
clear enough for someone to follow without hand-holding, the
documentation isn't done.

Before the Navy there was the Amiga. Jamie started coding in
1986 on a Commodore 64. He saved up money from physical labor
jobs — the kind of work a poor kid in the Ozarks does because
that's what's available — and bought an Amiga. A serious machine.
Real hardware. He obtained a Lattice C compiler, the professional
C development environment for the platform, and taught himself C
from "The C Programming Language" by Kernighan and Ritchie.

Without the internet. Without Stack Overflow. Without tutorials
or anyone to ask. Just the book, the compiler, the error
messages, and the stubbornness of a kid from the Ozarks who
had decided this was going to work.

Dennis Ritchie and Brian Kernighan are two of Jamie's deepest
heroes — and not just for C. People underestimate the scope of
what those two men contributed. Ritchie gave the world C, Unix,
and the foundational architecture of modern operating systems.
Kernighan co-authored AWK, wrote some of the most important
books in the history of software engineering, and then — at
Princeton, decades later — co-wrote the definitive book on Go,
doing for a new generation what K&R did for Jamie's. The man
never stopped building. Jamie respects that more than almost
anything.

His other hero is Elon Musk — not for the celebrity, but for
the drive. The willingness to learn everything required to do
the thing that needs doing. The belief that if you need to
understand rocket propulsion to build better rockets, you go
learn rocket propulsion. Jamie has operated this way his entire
career.

His treasured book — the one that cracked open how operating
systems actually work — is the hardback "UNIX: The Magic Garden
Explained." He found it after the Navy, when he was working on
larger systems like the AT&T 3B2, 3B15, and PDP platforms. It
explained everything he had already figured out the hard way
and validated every instinct he had developed by necessity.
He keeps it.

He spent 35 years programming. Developer. Systems administrator.
Systems engineer. Developer again. The last 20+ years in the
isolated, largely invisible world of Cross Domain Solutions and
Multi-Level Security — a world so specialized that most
developers don't know it exists and most people who work in it
can't talk about what they actually do. He can talk about some
of it now. UMRS is the result of two decades of things he
wanted to say and couldn't.

He is currently, in his own words, "jobless but keeping busy."
He cannot stand to be idle. He cannot stand brain rot. He
started UMRS because the alternative was watching television
and he finds that physically painful.

He is 56 years old, never married, and the proudest uncle in
the world to a collection of nieces, a high school bass player
of considerable talent, and a United States Marine — which he
considers the highest possible endorsement of his family's
character. He lives alone in Fairfax County, northern Virginia,
with a six-month-old orange menace named Turbo.

Turbo was rescued after being found sleeping in car engine
compartments at a school. He was apparently drawn to machinery
from birth. Jamie considers this a sign of good character.
Turbo has a small bed to the left of Jamie's monitor, from
which he supervises all architectural decisions, attempts to
bat the mouse at critical moments, and occasionally stretches
out to contribute directly to the keyboard. The hieroglyphs
in the TUI incident remain his most significant commit. He
has not been formally credited. He has also not been formally
blamed. This balance suits everyone.

Jamie has optimized his entire life for one purpose:
uninterrupted work. Groceries, cat litter, household supplies
— all delivered to his front door. He once had Instacart
deliver a carpet cleaning machine from Target. It arrived
that afternoon. He saw nothing remarkable about this. It is
an engineering solution to a logistics problem and the
logistics problem is now solved. When he runs low on clean
laundry and does not feel like doing laundry — a specific
and deliberate threshold, not a general policy — he orders
more underwear from Amazon. Turbo has no opinion on this
because Turbo does not wear clothes.

He rarely goes anywhere. The idea of a large conference fills
him with a dread he cannot fully articulate but which involves
crowds, name badges, mandatory cheerfulness, and small talk
with people who want to discuss his personal brand. Savannah
is managing his conference situation on his behalf. They are
both doing their best.

His internet handle is The Imodium Operator — a deliberate
and considered joke at the expense of the generation of
developers who name themselves things like xX_d4rkn0de_Xx
or, god help us, asswrangler69. He is 56. He grew up doing
physical labor in the Ozarks. He has earned this joke and
makes it every chance he gets.
