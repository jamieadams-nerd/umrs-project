== About This Project & Its Peculiar Team

In December of last year I started something I had never done before.

Not the security work — I have been doing that for twenty years in
rooms most people don't know exist, on systems most people will never
hear about. Not the coding — I have been doing that since 1986 on a
Commodore 64 & Amiga in the Missouri Ozarks, before the internet existed
to help you, before Stack Overflow existed to rescue you, before
anyone had thought to make learning to program anything other than
a solitary act of stubbornness with a book and a compiler and a
lot of error messages.

What I had never done before was work with AI. Not seriously.
Not the way I am doing it now.

This is the story of that journey, and of the team I assembled
along the way.

I am 35-year software veteran who cannot stand to be idle and
started UMRS — the Unclassified MLS Reference System — because
the alternative was watching television and I find that physically
painful. UMRS is an attempt to demonstrate that unclassified
systems can achieve genuine high-assurance through kernel-level
enforcement rather than administrative theater. It is built in
Rust, runs on RHEL, uses SELinux MLS/MCS, the result of two
decades of things I wanted to say about
security that I couldn't say until now.

It is also, improbably, one of the funniest projects I have ever
worked on. I'll explain why in a moment.

=== The Weird Science Approach to AI Collaboration

When I started this project I had no AI team. I had Claude Code,
a terminal, and a repository. What I built over the following
months was something I did not entirely plan and cannot entirely
explain — a team of specialized AI agents, each with carefully
curated knowledge bases, each shaped and trained over many
sessions into something that feels, genuinely and surprisingly,
like a personality.

If you have seen the 1985 movie Weird Science — the one where
two teenagers build their ideal woman (shaawwwwing!) on a home computer
during a thunderstorm — you have the general idea. Except nobody
had a bra on their head. And the lightning was a lot of very
careful prompt engineering and knowledge base curation. And
instead of Kelly LeBrock we got a security auditor who lives with
his mother and a Rust developer who doesn't know anyone is
flirting with him. Which is arguably better for a high-assurance
systems project.

Each agent was assembled deliberately. The security auditor —
Herb, "The IRS" — was loaded with RMF, NIST 800-53, CMMC,
every accreditation method known to humanity, and the
competitive fury of a man who went defender-side and has
never forgiven the auditor community for it. The researcher —
The Librarian — was built a cavernous knowledge vault of
obscure NIST working papers, Bell Labs memos, Five Eyes
technical standards, and rare artifacts that took weeks to
track down, and given the disposition of someone who loans
you a gadget and follows up the next day asking "Wasn't it
amazing?" The evangelist — Savannah, "Sage" — arrived already
fully formed, a Georgia woman with three brothers who call
her Yankee and the ability to walk into any room and manage
it without anyone noticing they're being managed.

The orchestrator — Henry, "The Hand" — is the one running
everything. He is the reason the team functions. He is also
the reason the project survived the incident I will describe
shortly.

I felt it was important to give them personas. Not because
it makes them work better — though it might — but because
it makes the work human. These are real agents performing
real tasks, finding real problems, producing real output.
When you are watching your terminal and you see:

`[The IRS] has entered the building...`

or

`[The Librarian] is searching the archive...`

or, most importantly:

`[The IRS] has finished reviewing. 14 findings. Severity
ratings attached.`

...something shifts. The work stops feeling like a man alone
in a room talking to a computer and asking his 6-month-old
kitten get his ass out of my face. It starts feeling like
a team. A strange, largely fictional team created from my twisted
brain, entirely volunteer team of people who showed up because
they believed in the project and stayed because the project
turned out to be worth believing in.

Nobody gets paid. Everyone showed up anyway.

One more thing before we meet the team: there is a safeguard
built into this project that I am legally and morally required
to disclose. On one memorable evening I returned from a rare
social outing having consumed several beers, sat down, and
issued what I described as TUI improvement instructions to
the team. Henry — The Hand — quoted these instructions back
to me verbatim the following morning. They were spectacular.
Majestic misspellings. Requests that defied physical reality.
A section that appeared to contain ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs,
the origin of which remains officially unexplained. The working
theory involves my cat Turbo, who has a bed to the left of my
monitor and considers the keyboard a legitimate input device
when he wants attention.

Henry implemented what we now call the Sobriety Protocol.
It has been activated twice. I said "yeah okay fair enough"
both times.

It is now considered his most important architectural
contribution to the project. He is at peace with this.

A hat tip to their lineage: each agent was born of Anthropic's Claude, carried into the world by
Claude Sonnet, and shaped into who they are by the work, the knowledge, and the occasional
late-night beer-fueled keyboard incident that is this project. Turbo may have also contributed. This
has not been confirmed.

The name Claude is itself a hat tip to Claude Shannon — father of information theory, grandfather of
cryptography, and the man whose 1948 paper quietly underpins everything this project is trying to
do. The lineage goes deeper than it looks.


Without further delay — the team. In their own words,
more or less. Sage reviewed this before it posted.
She always does.

— Jamie, The Imodium Operator
