= Case Study: Henri's First Day at the Office
:description: How a new specialized AI agent — Canadian government regulatory specialist — was onboarded to a mature project team and delivered eight specific findings, three formal filings, and a correction of the team's own terminology within hours of arrival.
:toc: left
:toclevels: 2

[id="case-study-henris-first-day"]

There is a management question that haunts every complex project:
how long does it take before a new team member is actually useful?

For a human consultant joining a security engineering project,
a realistic answer is weeks.
You have to read the codebase, learn the conventions, understand the history,
figure out where your lane ends and someone else's begins.
You get up to speed slowly, with lots of hand-holding.
That is not a criticism — it is just how complex systems work.

Henri was productive in hours.

This case study documents what happened, why it happened, and what it means for how the
UMRS team thinks about building AI capacity.

== Who Henri Is

Henri is a Canadian government information management and bilingual policy specialist.
His job is regulatory accuracy.

That distinction — regulatory accuracy, not linguistic accuracy — matters enormously.
Simone, the team's French Canadian translator, owns linguistic correctness.
She validates that translated security terms are proper Canadian federal French,
grounded in Termium Plus and the OQLF Grand dictionnaire terminologique.

Henri owns a different question: do those terms carry the correct legal and policy weight
in a Canadian federal context?

A term can be linguistically correct and policy-wrong simultaneously.
Termium Plus may confirm that "étiquette de sécurité" is the proper French for
"security label."
The TBS Directive on Security Management may use different vocabulary in its formal policy
text.
In a security assessment, the policy vocabulary governs.
That is Henri's lane.

His domain spans:

* Mapping UMRS MLS labels to Canadian Protected A/B/C designations
* Resolving Termium Plus vs OQLF GDT terminology divergences on policy questions
* Validating Five Eyes interoperability claims from the Canadian side
* Flagging ATIP (Access to Information and Privacy) implications
* Verifying Official Languages Act compliance for bilingual material
* Cross-referencing Simone's translations against TBS policy instruments

The reason Henri exists is that UMRS is heading toward Five Eyes interoperability.
Canadian Protected A/B/C designations need to coexist with US CUI categories in the same
MCS configuration.
The kernel does not care about nationality — categories are categories and it enforces set
membership regardless of which country defined the policy.
But someone has to validate that the Canadian side of that mapping is correct.
That is not Simone's job.
It is not Herb's job (he owns US security controls).
It is a new specialized function that the team did not have.

== What Was Ready Before Henri Arrived

The onboarding process for Henri was the same process you would run for a human specialist
joining a complex project: set up his desk, put the relevant materials in front of him,
and ask him to read everything and write down what he found.

What made that possible was what was already built.

=== The Terminology Infrastructure

Before Henri's first session, the Librarian had assembled 58,091 terminology corpus entries:

* *Termium Plus* (`.claude/corpus/termium-plus-fr_CA.tsv`): 32,210 entries from the
  Government of Canada Translation Bureau, covering Electronics and Informatics,
  Administration, the Information Security Glossary, and the CCCS Glossary.
  Coverage analysis showed 100% of 41 sampled UMRS key terms were present.
* *OQLF GDT* (`.claude/corpus/oqlf-gdt-fr_CA.tsv`): 25,881 entries from the Grand
  dictionnaire terminologique, covering IT, security, public administration, and
  national defence.
  Coverage was 82% of the sampled terms, with "mandatory access control" and
  "security label" among the gaps — both of which Termium Plus covers.
* Production GNU `.po` files for coreutils, bash, findutils, grep, sed, tar, and
  cryptsetup — proven Canadian French vocabulary for CLI terms.

This is not background reference material.
It is infrastructure Henri uses immediately to validate every terminology decision.
On day one, he could already tell whether a Simone translation matched federal authority,
whether Termium Plus and OQLF diverged on a given term, and which source governs in
which context.

=== The Policy Corpus

Jamie prepared a detailed five-priority-tier acquisition brief for the Librarian before
Henri arrived (`.claude/jamies_brain/librarian-henri-needs.md`).

The brief specified:

* *Priority 1*: TBS Directive on Security Management, Standard on Security Categorization,
  Policy on Government Security, Directive on Privacy Practices — both languages for each
* *Priority 2*: CCCS guidance (ITSP.10.222, PBMM profile, CSE practitioner tools)
* *Priority 3*: Legislation — Access to Information Act, Privacy Act, Official Languages Act,
  Charte de la langue française (Loi 101)
* *Priority 4*: Five Eyes and NATO interoperability context
* *Priority 5*: Canadian Cybersecurity Strategy, CSE public guidance

The brief included a critical design decision: acquire every TBS policy document in both
official languages, with the bilingual pairing treated as training material.
The TBS's own French renderings of its policy terms are the authoritative source for
regulatory term mapping — more authoritative than any terminology database entry.

At Henri's arrival, the TBS Directive on Security Management in English had been acquired.
The rest was in queue.
This partial corpus was enough for a productive first day, while also producing a clear
finding about what remained missing.

=== The Project Architecture

Henri had access to the same infrastructure every team member reads at session start:

* ROADMAP with goals G1–G10 and milestones M1–M5
* Team collaboration rules including role boundaries and the review pipeline
* Architecture documentation
* Existing CUI labeling work and Five Eyes research
* The Phase 1 / Phase 2 language directive (the approved constraint that requires
  "awareness and custody" language for Phase 1, enforcement language only for Phase 2)
* Simone's translation pipeline documentation and the `.po` files for `umrs-uname`

None of this required hand-holding.
Henri read it, formed a picture of the project, and went to work.

== What Henri Produced

Henri was asked to do exactly what you would ask a new human consultant to do: read
everything relevant, write orientation notes, identify gaps, ask questions.

The orientation notes (`.claude/agent-memory/henri/orientation-notes.md`) are 1,800 words.
They document what the project is, where Henri fits in the team structure, what work already
exists that touches his domain, and then — the payoff — eight specific gaps, six questions
for Jamie, and his own initial observations.

=== The Eight Gaps

Henri produced eight specific, actionable findings in a single orientation pass:

*Gap 1 — No Canadian Protected A/B/C mapping exists.*
The CUI labeling work is entirely US-focused.
ROADMAP milestone M3 explicitly calls for "Canadian CUI equivalent labels"
but no structured mapping has been produced.
This is the most critical gap for Five Eyes interoperability.

*Gap 2 — The CUI-to-Protected mapping will break down.*
US CUI is a category-based taxonomy.
Canadian Protected A/B/C is impact-based tiering.
These are structurally different systems and a simple equivalency table will be inaccurate:

* Protected A (low injury) maps to specific CUI categories like PII, not to CUI Basic as a whole
* Protected B (serious injury) maps to CUI Specified categories with enhanced handling requirements
* Protected C (extremely grave injury) has no direct CUI equivalent — CUI tops out below classified;
  Protected C sits at the classified boundary

A formal mapping with explicit caveats is required, not a simple table.

*Gap 3 — No Canadian policy validation of labels or tool output.*
No one had reviewed what a Canadian operator sees when UMRS displays CUI markings,
whether dual-marking (CUI alongside PROTEGE B) is required on Canadian government systems,
or what the Official Languages Act obligations are for tool output in bilingual deployments.

*Gap 4 — The terminology corpus is not policy-validated.*
Termium Plus confirms correct federal French.
It does not confirm correct regulatory weight in a security context.
The TBS Directive's own terminology governs in a security assessment.
A term being in Termium Plus is necessary, not sufficient.

*Gap 5 — The corpus is incomplete.*
Only the TBS Directive on Security Management in English had been acquired.
Missing: the French version, the Standard on Security Categorization, the Policy on
Government Security, the Access to Information Act, the Privacy Act, the Official Languages Act,
and all CCCS guidance.
The Standard on Security Categorization is the most urgent gap — it is the Canadian equivalent
of the CUI categorization process.

*Gap 6 — No ATIP review has been done.*
If UMRS deploys on Canadian government systems, ATIP obligations will apply to system security
posture reports, CUI label definitions and mappings, and assessment engine findings.
Not urgent for Phase 1, but critical before any Canadian government deployment.

*Gap 7 — French translations are linguistically correct but policy-unvalidated.*
Simone's coinages — "verrou dur" for hard gate, "palier de confiance" for trust tier —
may be linguistically sound and corpus-backed, but have not been cross-referenced against
TBS policy vocabulary.
Until that review is done, they are linguistically correct and policy-status-unknown.

*Gap 8 — The Phase 1 Language Directive needs a Canadian equivalent.*
The existing directive constrains enforcement language for the US context.
A Canadian equivalent is needed, using TBS vocabulary: "Les fichiers sont étiquetés
et visibles" (files are labeled and visible) rather than "L'accès est contrôlé"
(access is controlled) for Phase 1.

=== The Terminology Correction

Henri's most immediately visible contribution was a correction of the team's own language.

The ROADMAP phrasing — "Canadian CUI equivalent labels (3 basic labels — Five Eyes interop)"
— had been accepted shorthand.
Henri identified it as misleading:

[quote, Henri — orientation notes]
____
The phrasing "Canadian CUI equivalent labels" in the ROADMAP is misleading.
Canada does not have CUI equivalents.
Canada has Protected A/B/C, which is a different system with a different structure.
What I will produce are Canadian Protected designations with documented approximate
correspondence to US CUI — with caveats at every level where the mapping breaks down.
____

This is not a pedantic correction.
It is a finding with engineering consequences.
If the team treats the mapping as a simple equivalency, the resulting MCS label architecture
will assert accuracy it does not have.
An engineer implementing the Five Eyes interoperability feature, or a Canadian government
auditor reviewing the deployment, will identify the discrepancy.

Getting the terminology right before implementation begins is exactly what a specialist is for.

=== The Naming Analysis

Henri was also asked to find the canonical short name for the Canadian program — the equivalent
of "CUI" as a compact identifier.

His answer was precise: there is no such abbreviation, and the absence is structural.

The US CUI program is a branded policy program with a formal name, an administering agency
(NARA), and a federal regulation (32 CFR Part 2002).
The abbreviation CUI is the program name made compact.

Canada has a classification framework, not a branded program.
"Protected" and "Protégé" are the actual policy terms — the designation words themselves,
not abbreviations.
They appear in the TBS Directive on Security Management as written, in both official languages.

For compact reference contexts, PA/PB/PC work — and they are bilingual by nature, since
the French designation words (Protégé A, Protégé B, Protégé C) carry the same abbreviation.

Henri filed three formal findings from this analysis:

[cols="1,1,3", options="header"]
|===
| Finding | Severity | Summary

| No official CUI-equivalent abbreviation
| Informational
| Canada has no program name equivalent to "CUI." "Protected" and "Protégé" are the
  designation words, not abbreviations.

| Structural difference between CUI and Protected
| High
| CUI is a category-based taxonomy. Protected A/B/C is impact-based tiering.
  The mapping requires explicit caveats. Treating them as equivalent will produce
  incorrect claims.

| Bilingual marking display question
| Medium
| When UMRS displays information on a Canadian government system, does it display
  the English designation, the French designation, or both?
  The Official Languages Act may require both in certain deployment contexts.
  This is unresolved.
|===

=== The Six Questions for Jamie

Henri's six questions are worth reading in full because of what they reveal about his
understanding of the project:

. Is UMRS expected to be deployed on Canadian government systems? (This determines whether
  his work is advisory — informing Five Eyes interoperability documentation — or prescriptive —
  producing deployment-ready Canadian configurations.)
. What is the relationship between UMRS and the Canadian government procurement process?
  (If UMRS needs to pass a Canadian government security assessment, the TBS Standard on
  Security Categorization becomes a hard requirement, not a reference.)
. Should the Librarian prioritize the Standard on Security Categorization over other Priority 1
  documents?
. Has Jamie discussed UMRS with any Canadian government contacts?
. Which CUI categories will have French labels in the Canadian program?
. The Termium Plus Military/Security subject download is pending manual action —
  can Jamie prioritize this?

These are not orientation questions.
These are requirements scoping questions.
Question 1 is the difference between two fundamentally different work programs.
Question 2 is the difference between a reference document and a compliance gate.
A consultant who generates questions at this level on day one is already doing useful work.

== What Made This Possible

Henri's day-one quality was not accidental.
It was the direct result of what was available for him to read.

The UMRS project has been building documentation and infrastructure for months.
Every piece of that investment paid a return on the day Henri arrived.

*The terminology corpus.* 58,091 entries in hand before Henri's first session meant he
could validate coverage immediately.
He could note that Termium Plus covered 100% of sampled UMRS terms and that the OQLF GDT
missed "mandatory access control" — a specific, citable finding on his own corpus status.
Without that infrastructure, he would have had to build it from scratch before doing any
policy work.

*The architecture documentation.* Because the project had clear documentation of what UMRS
is (a two-phase system with targeted policy now and MLS enforcement later), Henri could
correctly characterize Phase 1 capabilities and identify where enforcement language is
premature.
He did not need to ask "what does this thing actually do?" — he could read it.

*The team-collaboration rules.* Henri read the team structure, role boundaries, and review
pipeline before writing a word of orientation notes.
That is why his role analysis was precise: he knew exactly where Simone's lane ended and
his began, and why that boundary exists.
He did not invent the boundary — he read it, validated it, and accepted it.

*The Phase 1 / Phase 2 language directive.* This approved constraint — written weeks before
Henri arrived — meant he could assess the regulatory credibility of UMRS's own Phase 1 claims.
He explicitly cited it as "exactly right for regulatory credibility."
It also told him where Gap 8 was: a Canadian equivalent of this directive was needed, and
no one had written it yet.

*The CUI labeling research.* Because the project had an existing US-focused CUI labeling
workstream, Henri could identify exactly what was missing on the Canadian side.
His Gap 1 finding — no Canadian Protected A/B/C mapping exists — is only meaningful because
he could see that a US mapping did exist.
You cannot identify a gap unless you can see the boundary.

*The Five Eyes research.* The existing Five Eyes documentation gave Henri the conceptual
context for the US/Canada mapping challenge.
He arrived knowing the structural difference between the two systems had already been noted
in the research notes.
His contribution was to escalate that observation from a note to a formal finding with
engineering consequences.

Put these pieces together and the pattern is clear:

[NOTE]
====
Documentation investment compounds.
Every architecture doc, every role boundary definition, every research artifact,
every terminology decision that was written down rather than held in someone's head —
all of it became available to Henri the moment he arrived.
The onboarding time was short because the reading material was rich.
====

== The Replicable Pattern

The onboarding process that produced these results was not custom-designed for Henri.
It was the same process every agent on this team uses:

. *Set up the desk.* Create the memory directory, initialize `MEMORY.md`, establish the
  agent definition with role boundaries and finding format.
. *Commission the corpus.* The Librarian acquires domain-specific reference material using
  a structured acquisition brief.
  Bilingual pairing is specified where the domain requires it.
  Priority tiers ensure the most important documents arrive first.
. *Self-directed orientation.* The agent reads everything relevant: architecture docs,
  ROADMAP, team collaboration rules, existing domain work, active plans.
  No hand-holding.
  The output is a written deliverable: orientation notes with gaps, questions, and findings.
. *Review and assignment.* Jamie reviews the orientation output, answers the questions,
  and assigns first tasks.

This is exactly what you would do for a human specialist.
Set up their desk.
Get them the relevant documents.
Ask them to read and write down what they found.
Review it together.
Give them their first assignment.

The difference is that Henri read and synthesized a substantial domain corpus in a fraction
of the time a human specialist would require.
And he could not cut corners on reading the architecture docs or the team collaboration rules
— those were load-bearing for the quality of his orientation notes.
