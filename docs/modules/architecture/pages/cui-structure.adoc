== What CUI Is

_Controlled Unclassified Information_ (CUI) is unclassified information that the U.S.
government designates as requiring controlled handling under law, regulation, or
government-wide policy.

CUI was created by Executive Order 13556 (2010) to standardize a patchwork of prior
labels that federal agencies had used inconsistently for decades. Before EO 13556,
the same type of sensitive-but-unclassified information might be labeled FOUO
(For Official Use Only), SBU (Sensitive But Unclassified), LES (Law Enforcement
Sensitive), or any of dozens of agency-specific variants. CUI replaced that
inconsistency with a single framework governed by the National Archives (NARA).

Authoritative sources:

* Executive Order 13556 (2010)
* 32 CFR Part 2002 (implementing regulation)
* NARA CUI Registry: https://www.archives.gov/cui
* NIST SP 800-171 (CUI protection requirements for non-federal systems)

CUI applies to information, not to people, systems, or clearance levels. It is not
a classification level. A document marked CUI does not require a security clearance
to handle — it requires compliance with the applicable handling rules.

== CUI Categories

The NARA CUI Registry defines more than 100 CUI categories organized into index groups.
Each category identifies a specific type of information with specific handling requirements.

Representative categories relevant to high-assurance system deployments:

[cols="1,2,3"]
|===
| Category | Abbreviation | Description

| Controlled Technical Information | CTI | Technical information with military or space application
| Information Systems Vulnerability Information | ISVI | Weakness, vulnerability, or exploitation path details
| Operations Security | OPSEC | Information revealing operational patterns or capabilities
| Protected Critical Infrastructure Information | PCII | Voluntarily submitted critical infrastructure data
| Critical Energy Infrastructure Information | CEII | Energy sector infrastructure vulnerability data
| General Privacy | PRVCY | Personally identifiable information (general)
| General Law Enforcement | LEI | Law enforcement information with handling restrictions
| Investigation | INV | Ongoing or completed investigation records
| Export Controlled | EXPT | ITAR / EAR regulated information
| Export Controlled Research | EXPTR | R&D data subject to export control
|===

See xref:reference:cui/cui-category-abbreviations.adoc[CUI category abbreviations] for the
complete list.

== CUI vs Classification

CUI is not a classification level. The distinction is structural:

[cols="1,2,2"]
|===
| Property | Classified information (C/S/TS) | CUI

| Authority | Executive Order 13526 | Executive Order 13556
| Who designates | Original classifying authorities | Any U.S. government agency
| Who handles | Cleared individuals with need-to-know | Authorized individuals with lawful purpose
| System requirement | Accredited classified system | System meeting NIST 800-171 requirements
| Lattice / dominance | Yes (formal MLS hierarchy) | No inherent lattice
| Enforcement model | Mandatory access control | Regulatory / contractual compliance
|===

People sometimes describe CUI as "a new security level." That framing is incorrect.
CUI is a handling framework for unclassified data that still requires protection. It sits
entirely below the classification boundary. The information is not secret — but it is
controlled.

== CUI vs Proprietary

CUI and proprietary information have different authorities and must not be conflated.

*CUI* is created by government designation. EO 13556 governs it. Government agencies
designate information as CUI; contractors and other non-federal entities are required
to protect it when they receive it.

*Proprietary information* is owned and designated by a company. It covers trade secrets,
intellectual property, and internal business data. It is not governed by EO 13556.
Proprietary data is not automatically CUI. CUI data is not automatically proprietary.
They overlap only when a contract explicitly says so.

From an MLS standpoint, CUI and Proprietary require separate sensitivity labels.
Collapsing them into a single label obscures which regulatory authority applies
and complicates compliance reporting.

== CUI vs ITAR / Export Controlled

Export-controlled information (governed by ITAR — the International Traffic in Arms
Regulations — or EAR — the Export Administration Regulations) is regulated by the
Department of State and the Department of Commerce respectively. It is a separate legal
authority from NARA and EO 13556.

Some ITAR information may also be marked CUI in practice, but the legal authority is
distinct. An MLS label model should represent these as separate sensitivity levels:
a CUI sensitivity level and an EXPORT_CONTROLLED sensitivity level, with the latter
carrying additional citizenship and dissemination constraints.

See xref:mls-label-model.adoc[MLS label model] for the UMRS sensitivity level set.

== What CUI Requires from a System

Systems processing CUI must comply with NIST SP 800-171. The key requirements
relevant to UMRS:

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| 800-171 Requirement | UMRS Implementation

| 3.1.3 — Control CUI flow
| MLS sensitivity labels enforce information flow at the kernel level. Data labeled
  CUI cannot flow to a domain with lower clearance.

| 3.1.1 — Limit access to authorized users
| SELinux user clearance ranges bound which sensitivity levels each user can access.

| 3.1.2 — Limit access to authorized functions
| SELinux type enforcement restricts which system calls and capabilities are available
  to each process domain.

| 3.13.1 — Monitor, control, and protect CUI
| IMA/EVM ensures CUI objects are measured and integrity-protected at the kernel level.

| 3.3.1 / 3.3.2 — Audit system events
| SELinux AVC audit events and the IMA measurement log provide tamper-visible records
  of access decisions.

| 3.13.10 — Establish and manage cryptographic keys
| Key management is addressed by the UMRS key management infrastructure.
  See xref:reference:key-recommendation-list.adoc[Cryptographic key recommendations].
|===

== CUI in the UMRS Context

UMRS implements CUI handling through MLS enforcement rather than application-layer
controls. This means:

* A process cleared for CUI sensitivity (s4) cannot read EXPORT_CONTROLLED (s5) data
  regardless of POSIX permissions.
* A process at CUI sensitivity cannot write to CONTROLLED (s2) storage — the no-write-down
  rule prevents intentional or accidental downgrade.
* CUI objects carry a security label (`s4` with applicable categories) that is stored in
  a kernel-protected xattr (EVM-protected) and cannot be stripped or altered without
  breaking the integrity chain.

The goal is to replace reliance on correct application behavior with kernel-enforced
policy. "Even if the application is compromised, it cannot read CUI data above its
clearance or write CUI data to a lower-labeled object."
