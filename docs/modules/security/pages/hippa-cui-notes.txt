HIPAA security as CUI Health (context first)

HIPAA-covered information aligns with the CUI category “Controlled Unclassified Information – Health” (often abbreviated CUI-H). This includes Protected Health Information (PHI) as defined by HIPAA when it is handled by non-federal or federal systems under contracts, grants, or shared missions.

Important distinction:
HIPAA is a law and regulatory framework.
CUI is a federal information handling framework.

When PHI exists in a federal or federally related context, HIPAA and CUI-H overlap. CUI does not replace HIPAA; it layers handling requirements and marking discipline on top of it.

HIPAA security rule – what it actually governs

HIPAA has three major rule sets:
	•	Privacy Rule (who may access and disclose PHI)
	•	Security Rule (how electronic PHI is protected)
	•	Breach Notification Rule

For our purposes, the Security Rule is the most relevant.

HIPAA Security Rule scope:
	•	Applies only to electronic PHI (ePHI)
	•	Focuses on confidentiality, integrity, and availability
	•	Is technology-neutral and risk-based

This is a key philosophical difference from many federal frameworks: HIPAA intentionally avoids prescribing specific technologies.

HIPAA Security Rule safeguard categories

HIPAA security controls are grouped into three safeguard classes:

Administrative safeguards
These are governance and process controls.
Examples include:
	•	Risk analysis and risk management
	•	Workforce training
	•	Incident response procedures
	•	Contingency planning
	•	Assigned security responsibility

Technical safeguards
These are system-enforced controls.
Examples include:
	•	Access control (unique user identification, emergency access)
	•	Audit controls
	•	Integrity controls (mechanisms to prevent improper alteration)
	•	Person or entity authentication
	•	Transmission security (encryption in transit)

Physical safeguards
These cover facilities and devices.
Examples include:
	•	Facility access controls
	•	Workstation use and security
	•	Device and media controls

Required vs addressable controls (critical nuance)

HIPAA controls are classified as either:
	•	Required
	•	Addressable

Addressable does not mean optional.

It means:
	•	You must implement the control OR
	•	Implement a documented equivalent OR
	•	Formally justify why it is not reasonable and appropriate

This is one of the most misunderstood parts of HIPAA and is where many organizations get into trouble.

HIPAA as a risk-based framework (why this matters)

HIPAA does not say:
“You must use encryption X”
or
“You must log event Y”

HIPAA says:
“You must assess risk and implement reasonable and appropriate protections.”

This gives flexibility, but it also shifts the burden of proof onto the system owner.

In high-assurance terms:
HIPAA is outcome-driven, not mechanism-driven.

This contrasts with frameworks like:
	•	NIST 800-53
	•	STIGs
	•	NSA Raise the Bar

Which are far more prescriptive.

Where HIPAA fits relative to NIST and CUI

HIPAA maps very cleanly to NIST frameworks, even though it predates many of them.

Common mappings:
	•	HIPAA Security Rule ↔ NIST SP 800-53 Moderate baseline
	•	HIPAA risk analysis ↔ NIST SP 800-30
	•	HIPAA audit controls ↔ NIST AU family
	•	HIPAA integrity controls ↔ NIST SI family
	•	HIPAA access control ↔ NIST AC family

CUI overlays typically expect NIST-style controls, which means HIPAA-covered systems handling CUI-H often end up implementing NIST 800-53 anyway, even if HIPAA alone would not strictly require it.

Common failure patterns in HIPAA environments

From a security engineering perspective, HIPAA failures usually stem from:
	•	Treating HIPAA as a checklist instead of a risk framework
	•	Weak or nonexistent audit logging
	•	Overreliance on perimeter controls
	•	Poor key management
	•	Inadequate separation of duties
	•	Informal access provisioning and deprovisioning
	•	Lack of integrity protection for logs and records

HIPAA violations are rarely caused by exotic attacks. They are usually operational.

Why HIPAA and CUI-H deserve MLS-style thinking

Although HIPAA does not mandate MLS, the data characteristics strongly resemble MLS concerns:
	•	Different sensitivity levels (billing vs diagnosis vs mental health)
	•	Need-to-know access
	•	Compartmentalization
	•	Strong auditing requirements
	•	Insider threat considerations

This is why applying SELinux, xattrs, strong audit trails, and explicit labeling (even unclassified) makes architectural sense for HIPAA/CUI-H systems.

How CUI categories are actually defined
CUI categories and subcategories are formally defined in the CUI Registry maintained by NARA. Each category has a formal name and description, but NARA does not standardize short abbreviations for most of them.

The only consistently abbreviated form is:
CUI- (full or nearly full word)

Examples that are commonly accepted in practice
CUI-H or CUI-Health
This one is widely used and understood. Health is the strongest exception because of its tight coupling to HIPAA and PHI.

CUI-PRVCY or CUI-Privacy
Privacy is often written out. You will sometimes see shortened forms internally, but there is no single accepted letter like “CUI-P” that auditors will universally recognize.

CUI-PROC or CUI-Procurement
Procurement is almost always spelled out or lightly shortened. “CUI-P” would be ambiguous and is not standard.

CUI-FIN or CUI-Financial
Sometimes shortened, but again not standardized.

CUI-LAW or CUI-Legal
Used occasionally, but not formally codified.

CUI-IT
Sometimes used informally, but technically vague because many CUI categories involve IT systems.

Why single-letter abbreviations do not exist
There are three reasons this differs from classified markings.

First, CUI was designed to be readable by non-cleared personnel. Clarity was favored over brevity.

Second, many categories would collide if reduced to a single letter. Procurement, Privacy, Proprietary, and Personnel would all become “P”.

Third, CUI is not a lattice like classified MLS. It is a handling framework layered on civil systems, so human readability was prioritized.

What is actually safe to use in documentation
Safe and accepted:
CUI-Health
CUI-Privacy
CUI-Procurement
CUI-Financial
CUI-Legal
CUI-Export-Controlled
CUI-Critical-Infrastructure

Risky or nonstandard:
CUI-P
CUI-L
CUI-F
Any single-letter abbreviation unless you define it explicitly and locally

What high-assurance and audit teams typically do
Most mature programs do one of the following:

Option 1
Use full category names everywhere and accept the verbosity.

Option 2
Define a local abbreviation table once, for example:
CUI-PROC = Procurement
CUI-PRIV = Privacy
CUI-FIN = Financial
CUI-H = Health

And then consistently use those within the system and documentation, making it clear they are local conventions, not federal standards.

First: the basic CUI marking structure

A full CUI marking has three conceptual parts:
	1.	The CUI banner
	2.	The category and optional subcategory
	3.	Optional dissemination controls

The general syntax looks like this:

CUI//[/]//

The double slashes are significant and intentional. They mirror classified markings but remain human-readable.

Category vs subcategory (important distinction)

Category
This is the broad CUI category as defined in the CUI Registry.

Examples:
	•	HEALTH
	•	PRIVACY
	•	PROCUREMENT
	•	FINANCIAL
	•	EXPORT CONTROLLED
	•	CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE

Subcategory
This is only used when the CUI Registry explicitly defines subcategories under that category.

Not all categories have subcategories. When they do, the subcategory name is also taken verbatim (or near-verbatim) from the registry.

Example: Health

Health is a category. It has recognized subcategories that map to statutory authorities.

Common real-world examples:

CUI//HEALTH
This is valid when you are not narrowing further.

CUI//HEALTH/PHI
Used when explicitly marking Protected Health Information.

CUI//HEALTH/CLINICAL
Used in some environments for clinical records.

CUI//HEALTH/RESEARCH
Used where health-related research data is the driver.

You do not invent these — you only use subcategories that are actually defined or commonly accepted by the owning authority.

Example: Privacy

Privacy is its own category.

Typical markings:

CUI//PRIVACY
General privacy-controlled information.

CUI//PRIVACY/PII
Very common. This is one of the most widely used subcategories.

You will often see this exact form on federal systems.

Example: Procurement

Procurement generally does not have deep subcategories in day-to-day use.

Typical marking:

CUI//PROCUREMENT

If subcategories exist, they tend to be very specific and authority-driven (e.g., source selection information), and many organizations simply stay at the category level.

Example: Financial

Typical:

CUI//FINANCIAL

Sometimes further refined internally, but most programs avoid unnecessary subdivision unless required.

Dissemination controls (optional but common)

After the category (and optional subcategory), you may see dissemination controls.

Examples:

CUI//HEALTH/PHI//NOFORN
CUI//PRIVACY/PII//FEDONLY
CUI//PROCUREMENT//REL TO USA, CAN

These controls are orthogonal to the category. They do not change what the data is — they change who may receive it.

You are not trying to reproduce the entire banner in the SELinux label. You are mapping semantics.

A very clean, modern approach is:

	•	Categories (MCS) for the CUI categories

c2 = CUI_PRIVACY

s0:c2,c11  → CUI//PRIVACY/PII

Using a JSON-based extension/mediator to expand abbreviated internal labels into authoritative, human-readable CUI markings is exactly the right architectural move. It gives you all the benefits you want without violating registry intent.

Why a JSON mediator is the right abstraction

You are cleanly separating three concerns:
	1.	Enforcement
SELinux MCS labels remain compact, stable, and operable.
	2.	Semantics
The JSON file becomes the authoritative mapping from internal labels to NARA-aligned CUI meanings.
	3.	Presentation / tooling
Anything human-facing (GUIs, reports, banners, audits, Cockpit views, etc.) consumes the JSON, not the raw MCS label.

That separation is exactly what was missing with things like ADS — and you are deliberately fixing it here.

What the JSON should represent (conceptually)

The JSON should be declarative, not procedural.

Each entry should answer:
	•	What category is this?
	•	What subcategory (if any)?
	•	What is the exact CUI marking string?
	•	What authority or basis does it map to?
	•	Is it primary or optional?

For example, conceptually (not prescribing format):
	•	Internal label: CUI_PRIVACY_PII
	•	Expands to: CUI//PRIVACY/PII
	•	Authority: Privacy Act / NARA CUI Registry
	•	Notes: PII subcategory

This gives you:
	•	Traceability
	•	Audit friendliness
	•	Zero ambiguity
	•	Future extensibility
