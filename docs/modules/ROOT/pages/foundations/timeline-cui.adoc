= Controlled Unclassified Information: A Timeline of Good Intentions and Slow Enforcement
:description: From ad-hoc markings to kernel-level enforcement — the CUI program's long road to accountability

Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) occupies one of the most frustrating positions
in the security landscape. It is not classified. It does not carry the weight of Top
Secret compartments or the institutional infrastructure of the classified world.
But it is sensitive. It can damage national security, expose acquisition strategies,
reveal intelligence methods, compromise personnel, and undermine the defense industrial
base — when it leaks. And it leaks. Repeatedly. Avoidably.

The CUI program was established in 2010 to solve a problem the government had created
for itself over decades: a chaotic patchwork of inconsistent markings — FOUO, SBU, LES,
SENSITIVE, and dozens of others — that meant different things to different agencies and
provided no consistent protection requirements. Fifteen years later, the enforcement
mechanism that was always supposed to follow is finally arriving.

UMRS was created in December 2025 to demonstrate what CUI protection looks like when
it is implemented at the kernel level rather than the policy level — applying the same
formal enforcement discipline the classified world has used for decades to unclassified
information that deserves the same care.

== The Pre-CUI Era — Marking Chaos

Before 2010 there was no unified framework for unclassified sensitive information.
Agencies invented their own markings. Contractors received information marked in
ways that carried no consistent legal or technical protection requirement. The result
was predictable: sensitive information flowed freely across boundaries it should never
have crossed, protected by nothing more than informal convention and good intentions.

The intelligence community, defense agencies, and law enforcement each maintained their
own category systems. A contractor working across multiple agencies faced a maze of
incompatible requirements with no authoritative reference. The information was
sensitive. The protection was theater.

== Establishment — The Program with No Teeth

Executive Order 13556 <<eo13556>> in November 2010 established the CUI program under
the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) as Executive Agent. It
mandated a single, government-wide framework to replace the chaotic marking zoo.
It was a genuine step forward — but it contained a critical gap.

The EO established the *framework* but not the *enforcement*. Agencies were directed
to implement CUI requirements but given no deadline and no penalty for non-compliance.
Contractors continued operating under inconsistent agency-specific requirements for
years after the EO was signed. As one legal analysis noted, <citetitle>"the EO did not
actually implement the CUI program so contractors continued to be uncertain what their
obligations were"</citetitle> <<far-cui>>.

The program existed on paper. In practice, little changed.

== Self-Attestation Era — Compliance Theater

DFARS 252.204-7012 <<dfars7012>> required DoD contractors to self-attest compliance
with NIST SP 800-171. There was no independent verification. No third-party assessment.
No audit mechanism. Contractors submitted scores to the Supplier Performance Risk System
(SPRS) that were entirely self-reported.

The results were precisely what you would expect. Organizations with poor security
postures submitted optimistic scores. The incentive was to pass, not to comply.
Sensitive defense information continued to flow through contractor systems protected by
little more than a checkmark on a form and an optimistic SPRS score.

Meanwhile, adversaries — particularly nation-state actors — were systematically
targeting the Defense Industrial Base precisely because CUI in contractor systems was
a soft target. The F-35 technical specifications. Submarine propulsion data. Acquisition
strategies. Personnel files. The damage from CUI breaches in this era is documented,
ongoing, and deeply underreported.

== Enforcement Era — Teeth Finally Added

CMMC 2.0 <<cmmc2024>> transformed CUI compliance from a self-reported checkbox into a
legally binding, third-party assessed condition of contract award. The Final Rule,
published October 15, 2024 and codified in 32 CFR Part 170, established the regulatory
foundation. The companion DFARS acquisition rule <<dfars7021>>, which cleared regulatory
review in August 2025, integrates CMMC into the contracting process itself — making
certification a condition of contract eligibility.

NIST SP 800-171 Revision 3 <<nist171r3>>, published May 2024, updated the control
framework while DoD's class deviation maintained Rev 2 as the operative standard for
existing contracts during the transition. The FAR CUI Rule <<far-cui>>, advancing
through rulemaking in 2025, extends CUI protection requirements beyond the Defense
Industrial Base to all federal contractors — the first time CUI enforcement reaches
the full federal supply chain.

The program established in 2010 is finally acquiring the enforcement infrastructure
it always needed. Fourteen years later.

== Kernel Enforcement Era — What CUI Protection Should Have Always Been

The CUI program's trajectory — from ad-hoc markings, to policy framework, to
self-attestation, to third-party audit — has consistently treated CUI protection
as an administrative and procedural problem. Policies, plans, attestations, audits.

The classified world solved this problem differently. It solved it at the kernel level.
Mandatory access controls. Security labels enforced by the reference monitor.
Bell-LaPadula no-read-up, no-write-down implemented in hardware and operating system
policy — not in a training slide or a contractor's self-assessment form.

UMRS applies that same discipline to CUI. Not because the regulatory framework requires
it yet — but because it is the correct engineering answer to the problem, and because
the regulatory framework is clearly moving in that direction.

=== UMRS CUI Implementation Status

**Phase 1 — Complete (December 2025)**

CUI category labels are loaded into the Multi-Category Security (MCS) translation
layer under targeted SELinux policy. Users see human-readable CUI handling requirement
labels on files and processes. The system makes CUI visible — operators know what they
are handling and what the requirements are.

This is awareness enforcement. The label is present. The policy is present.
The human is informed. But the kernel is not yet enforcing the boundary.

**Phase 2 — Planned**

MLS (Multi-Level Security) policy replaces targeted policy. The SELinux reference
monitor enforces CUI boundaries at the kernel level — no read-up, no write-down,
no process can bypass the label. dm-crypt encrypts CUI data at rest. IMA measures
integrity of CUI-handling binaries before execution. The system enforces the boundary
whether or not the human remembers to.

This is the difference between a sign that says "authorized personnel only" and a
locked door. Phase 1 installs the sign. Phase 2 installs the lock.

== Why This Matters

The CUI breach case studies accumulate year after year. Contractor laptops. Unencrypted
file shares. Misconfigured cloud storage. Email to the wrong recipient. In almost every
case, the information was marked. The policy existed. The training had been delivered.
And none of it was enough because none of it was enforced at the level where enforcement
actually works — the kernel.

The classified world learned this lesson in the 1970s and 1980s. The Orange Book, the
reference monitor concept, Bell-LaPadula — all of these emerged from the recognition
that administrative controls fail and technical controls are necessary. Mandatory access
control at the kernel level is not optional paranoia. It is the correct engineering
response to the problem of protecting sensitive information on systems that humans
operate under time pressure with imperfect attention.

CUI deserves the same treatment. The regulatory framework is now demanding it.
UMRS delivers it.
