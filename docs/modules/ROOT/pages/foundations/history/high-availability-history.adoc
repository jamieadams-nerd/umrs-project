= High Availability vs High Assurance: A Historical Record
:toc: left
:toclevels: 2
:description: The history of redundancy engineering in technology — and why high availability is not the same as high assurance.

// This page exists to establish the distinction between high availability (HA) and
// high assurance in technical documentation. The two terms are frequently confused.
// High availability addresses uptime and fault tolerance. High assurance addresses
// correctness, integrity, and security trustworthiness. These are different engineering
// disciplines with different goals, different failure modes, and different design methods.

The abbreviation HA is used throughout the computing industry to mean *high availability* —
systems engineered for continuous uptime, fault tolerance, and service continuity.
In the UMRS project, HA does not appear as an abbreviation because it would be confused
with *high assurance*, which is an entirely different engineering discipline.

This page traces the history of redundancy and availability engineering to show where
high availability came from and why it is distinct from high assurance.

== Why This Distinction Matters for UMRS

High availability and high assurance address different problems:

[cols="1,2,2"]
|===
| Dimension | High Availability | High Assurance

| *Goal*
| Keep the service running
| Prove that the result is correct and trustworthy

| *Threat*
| Hardware failure, network outage, software crash
| Incorrect behavior, policy violation, security compromise

| *Success measure*
| Uptime percentage (nines)
| Correctness of enforcement; absence of bypass conditions

| *Primary technique*
| Redundant components, failover, replication
| Independent verification, provenance checking, non-bypassable enforcement

| *Failure mode*
| Service unavailable
| Service available but wrong or compromised

| *Correctness assumed?*
| Yes — replicas assumed identical and correct
| No — independent mechanisms must agree
|===

A system can be highly available but insecure.
A system can be high assurance but have planned maintenance windows.
Neither property implies the other.

UMRS is designed for high assurance, not high availability.
When UMRS detects a security enforcement failure, the correct response may be to halt
the affected service — which is the opposite of an availability-optimized system's response.

== Part 1 — The Origins of Redundancy Engineering

=== Telecommunications (Early–Mid 20th Century)

The earliest large-scale redundancy concepts originated in telecommunications networks,
long before digital computing required high availability.

Telephone systems built in the early and mid-20th century faced a fundamental reliability
problem: a single switching office or trunk line failure could disconnect entire regions.

Bell Labs and other telecom engineering organizations developed techniques to prevent this:

* *Alternate routing* — networks designed so traffic could be rerouted through alternate
  switching centers if a direct path failed.
* *Trunk diversity* — cities connected by multiple trunk circuits, physically separated to
  avoid common-mode failures.
* *Protection switching* — automatic circuit failover when a failure occurred.

These techniques established the earliest form of path redundancy and introduced
terminology still used today:

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Term | Meaning

| Alternate routing | Multiple network routes between points
| Protection switching | Automatic circuit failover
| Trunk diversity | Multiple physical connections
| Carrier diversity | Using different telecom providers
|===

=== Military Survivability Engineering (Cold War Era)

During the Cold War, communications reliability became a national security concern.
DARPA, the RAND Corporation, and military communications laboratories studied survivable
communication systems — networks that could continue functioning even if parts were destroyed.

Key techniques developed in this era:

* *Decentralized network topology* — many distributed nodes instead of central switching,
  reducing single points of failure.
* *Packet switching* — messages divided into packets that could travel independently
  on different routes.
* *Dynamic routing* — routing decisions changed at runtime based on network conditions.

These ideas formed the basis of ARPANET, the precursor to the Internet.
The goal was availability after partial destruction — not correctness of content.

=== Aerospace Reliability Engineering

A major advance in redundancy engineering came from spacecraft and aircraft design.
Unlike terrestrial systems, spacecraft cannot be repaired after launch.
Failures must be tolerated by design.

*Triple Modular Redundancy (TMR)*

Three processors perform the same computation simultaneously.
A voter circuit determines the correct output.
If one processor produces an incorrect result, the other two override it.

[source]
----
CPU A
CPU B  → Majority Voter → Output
CPU C
----

*Fly-by-wire aircraft*

Modern aircraft replaced mechanical flight controls with electronic systems.
The F-16, F-18, and Airbus flight control systems use three or four redundant flight
computers that continuously compare results.

Terminology from aerospace reliability engineering:

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Term | Meaning

| TMR | Triple Modular Redundancy
| Voter circuit | Majority decision mechanism
| Fault containment | Prevents propagation of faults
| Graceful degradation | Reduced functionality instead of total failure
|===

NOTE: Aerospace TMR is primarily an *availability* mechanism — it keeps the aircraft
flying. The voter does not verify that the computation is *correct in a security sense*,
only that a majority of processors agreed. This is distinct from the assurance redundancy
used in high-assurance security systems.

== Part 2 — Computing Hardware Redundancy

=== Early Computers: Repairability (1940s–1960s)

Early computers (ENIAC, UNIVAC, IBM mainframes) did not incorporate redundancy.
Failures were frequent due to vacuum tube failures and thermal stress.
The strategy was rapid repair:

* Spare parts inventories of replacement components
* Modular construction for quick replacement
* Metrics introduced: *MTBF* (Mean Time Between Failures) and *MTTR* (Mean Time To Repair)

=== Field Replaceable Units (1960s–1980s)

Vendors began designing systems specifically for field servicing.
A *Field Replaceable Unit (FRU)* is a component designed to be replaced on site without
factory repair. Examples: power supplies, CPU boards, memory modules, disk controllers.

FRUs reduced repair time significantly but systems still relied on single active components.
Availability improved through faster repair, not through eliminating downtime.

=== Embedded Controllers and Intelligent Hardware

A turning point occurred when hardware components began including embedded processors
and firmware. Earlier components were passive devices — disk drives, power supplies, and
interface boards could not monitor their own health or coordinate failover.

Embedded microcontrollers changed this by enabling hardware to:

* Detect failures and isolate faulty components
* Coordinate failover operations
* Support live component replacement

This development made modern hot-swap, RAID, and redundant controller architectures
possible.

=== RAID and Storage Redundancy

RAID technology was introduced in the late 1980s. Common RAID levels:

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Level | Purpose

| RAID 1 | Mirroring — exact duplicate of all data
| RAID 5 | Parity protection — one disk failure tolerated
| RAID 6 | Dual parity — two disk failures tolerated
| RAID 10 | Mirrored striping — performance and redundancy combined
|===

=== Hot-Swap Hardware

Hot-swap technology allowed components to be replaced while systems remained operational.
This required embedded hardware logic to detect device insertion, manage power sequencing,
and initialize hardware safely.

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Term | Meaning

| Hot swap | Replace component while system is running
| Hot plug | Device insertion detected automatically
| Cold spare | Replacement not yet installed
| Warm spare | Installed but inactive
|===

=== Redundant Controllers and Multipath

Enterprise systems introduced dual controllers and multiple data paths.

[source]
----
Server
  |        |
HBA A    HBA B
  |        |
SAN A    SAN B
  |        |
Controller A
Controller B
----

This architecture survives controller failures, cable failures, and switch failures.

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Term | Meaning

| Active-Active | Both controllers active simultaneously
| Active-Passive | Secondary controller in standby
| Multipathing | Multiple I/O routes to the same storage
| Dual fabric | Two independent SAN networks
|===

== Part 3 — Facility and Geographic Redundancy

=== Data Center Infrastructure

Redundancy expanded to the facility level.

*Power systems*

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Model | Meaning

| N | Minimum required capacity
| N+1 | One redundant component
| 2N | Fully duplicated systems
| 2N+1 | Dual systems plus spare
|===

Data centers deployed multiple utility feeds, UPS battery systems, and backup generators.
Cooling infrastructure incorporated redundant chillers, cooling towers, and air handlers.

=== Geographic Redundancy

Even fully redundant data centers remain vulnerable to regional disasters.
Organizations developed geographically separated disaster recovery sites with constraints:

* Different power grids
* Different flood and earthquake zones
* Independent telecommunications providers

=== Storage Replication Technologies

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Model | Behavior

| Synchronous replication | Data written to both sites simultaneously — zero data loss,
latency-sensitive, limited by distance.

| Asynchronous replication | Write occurs at primary site first; replication follows —
supports long distances, small window of possible data loss.
|===

== Part 4 — Cluster Software and Failover Orchestration

=== High-Availability Clusters (Late 1990s–Early 2000s)

Replication alone was not sufficient — software had to coordinate failover between servers.
Cluster software emerged to perform this function.

A cluster typically included:

* Node A (Primary) and Node B (Standby)
* Shared storage
* Heartbeat network

Cluster software monitored system health and initiated failover when necessary.

=== Heartbeat and Automatic Failover

Nodes periodically exchange heartbeat messages. If heartbeats stop, the cluster assumes
a node failure and performs automatic failover:

. Detect failure
. Stop services on failed node
. Activate services on standby node
. Mount shared storage
. Redirect network traffic

Major cluster platforms included Veritas Cluster Server, Sun Cluster, Oracle RAC, and
Microsoft Cluster Server.

=== Split Brain and Quorum

Clusters face the *split brain* problem: if nodes lose communication but both continue
operating, they may believe they are each the active system, causing conflicting writes
and data corruption.

Solutions:

* *Quorum* — only the node group with quorum (majority agreement) remains active.
* *Fencing* — failed nodes are forcibly isolated via power fencing, storage fencing, or
  network isolation.

=== Availability Metrics

[cols="1,2"]
|===
| Availability | Downtime per year

| 99% | ~3.6 days
| 99.9% | ~8.7 hours
| 99.99% | ~52 minutes
| 99.999% | ~5 minutes
|===

These "nines" became standard contractual guarantees in service level agreements.
Failure to meet them could result in financial penalties and service credits.

== Part 5 — Assurance Redundancy vs Availability Redundancy

The cluster and replication era represented the maturation of availability-oriented
redundancy. A separate engineering tradition — assurance-oriented redundancy — developed
in parallel in aerospace, nuclear control, and high-assurance security systems.

=== The Distinction

*Availability redundancy* keeps services running when components fail.
Correctness is assumed. If one server fails, the backup serves identical data.
The focus is uptime.

*Assurance redundancy* detects incorrect or compromised results.
Correctness is verified, not assumed. If two independent implementations disagree,
the system rejects the result and fails closed.
The focus is trustworthiness.

=== Assurance Redundancy Patterns

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Pattern | Description

| N-Version Programming
| Multiple independently developed implementations of the same function run in parallel.
Results are compared or voted. Reduces common-mode failures. Used in avionics, safety
controllers, and cross-domain security systems.

| Self-Checking Pair (SCP)
| Two independent channels compute the same result. A comparator checks agreement.
If outputs differ, the system fails closed. Used extensively in fly-by-wire and
safety-critical controllers.

| Diverse Self-Checking Pair (DSCP)
| A self-checking pair where the two channels are intentionally different implementations
(different languages, algorithms, or teams) to reduce correlated failure. UMRS uses this
pattern: the `nom`-based declarative parser and the `FromStr`-based imperative parser
must agree on every security label before access is granted.

| Recovery Blocks
| Primary algorithm runs; an acceptance test validates the result; if validation fails,
an alternate implementation runs. Formalized by Randell (1975). Redundancy in time
rather than in space.

| Byzantine Fault Tolerance
| Multiple replicas cross-validate each other. Designed for environments where some
components may behave maliciously rather than just failing silently. Requires 3f+1 nodes
to tolerate f Byzantine faults.

| End-to-End Integrity Verification
| Multiple layers independently validate data integrity. Even if an intermediate layer
checks integrity, endpoints verify independently. Principle from Saltzer, Reed, and Clark.
|===

=== Why Common-Mode Failure Matters

The central challenge in assurance redundancy is *common-mode failure*: two implementations
may share the same specification error, algorithmic mistake, or library vulnerability,
causing them to fail in the same way.

High-assurance systems mitigate this by introducing diversity:

* Different programming languages
* Different algorithms
* Different development teams
* Different compilers
* Different operating environments

The goal is to reduce correlated failure — to make it unlikely that the same bug
compromises both independent paths.

=== The Assurance Principle

____
Trust is replaced by independent verification.

Instead of believing a single component is correct, the system asks:
do multiple independent mechanisms agree on the result?
____

This principle appears in safety engineering, nuclear control systems, avionics,
cryptographic protocols, and cross-domain security systems.
It is the foundation of the Two-Path Integrity (TPI) pattern in UMRS.

== How These Traditions Connect to UMRS

UMRS draws from the assurance redundancy tradition, not the availability redundancy
tradition. The specific patterns:

[cols="2,3"]
|===
| UMRS mechanism | Assurance pattern

| TPI (Two-Path Integrity) — `nom` + `FromStr` parsers must agree
| Diverse Self-Checking Pair

| Fail-closed on TPI disagreement
| SCP mismatch → reject result

| `CategorySet` bitmask — fixed-size, deterministic layout
| Deterministic execution; no allocator influence on timing

| Inode-anchored xattr reads via `rustix`
| Non-bypassable — no path-based bypass possible

| KATTRS statfs magic verification
| Provenance verification — confirms kernel filesystem identity before trusting the read
|===

UMRS does use availability concepts in infrastructure design, but they are secondary.
When a security check fails, UMRS does not attempt to keep the service running.
It fails closed and reports the condition.
