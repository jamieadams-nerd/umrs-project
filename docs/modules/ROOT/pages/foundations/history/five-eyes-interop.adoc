= Five Eyes Classification Interoperability
:description: UMRS label support for the national unclassified information handling schemes of Five Eyes partner nations alongside the US CUI baseline.
:toc: left
:toclevels: 2

// NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2 — CUI protection requirements
// CMMC 2.0 — operationalized CUI protection assessment

NOTE: This page covers Five Eyes nations (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) plus France.
France is not a Five Eyes member, but it is included here because UMRS is designed
to support coalition and partner environments that include French-origin information.

The Five Eyes alliance is an intelligence-sharing arrangement among
the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand.
Each member nation handles its own national-origin sensitive-but-unclassified
information under a distinct marking and handling scheme. When UMRS-enabled
systems operate in coalition or partner environments, the platform must be
able to represent, enforce, and translate labels from each national scheme
alongside the US CUI baseline.

This page describes the interoperability requirement, each partner nation's
scheme, and the UMRS design goal for supporting them.

== Why Interoperability Is an Architectural Requirement

The US CUI program — governed by NIST SP 800-171 and CMMC 2.0 — defines
categories and handling requirements for unclassified federal information.
CUI is the primary labeling baseline for UMRS.

Partner nations' schemes are structurally similar to CUI but differ in
naming, tiering, and specific handling rules. When a document marked
OFFICIAL:Sensitive (Australian scheme) is received on a UMRS-enabled system,
the system must know how to represent that marking in a security label,
apply the appropriate handling controls, and produce audit evidence that the
label was respected.

Without a common label abstraction, each national scheme would require
custom enforcement logic. With a common abstraction, the UMRS label model
maps to any scheme by configuring the mapping layer — the enforcement
mechanism stays the same.

== The Four-Axis Normalization Model

To understand how different national schemes compare and how UMRS maps to them, it
helps to separate unclassified information controls into four independent axes:

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Axis | Meaning

| *Protection tier*
| The impact level within unclassified — routine, sensitive, higher-but-not-classified.
Determines baseline handling requirements.

| *Subject taxonomy*
| A domain category system (e.g., Law Enforcement, Agriculture, Health).
Specifies what kind of information is involved, independent of its sensitivity tier.

| *Handling requirements*
| Specific rules for storage, processing, transmission, and disposal.
May be defined per tier, per category, or both.

| *Dissemination restrictions*
| Who the information may be shared with — citizenship limits, need-to-know,
compartment membership, "no foreign" controls.
|===

The US CUI model is unusual in formalizing all four axes explicitly. Most other nations
formalize only the protection tier and leave the other three to policy guidance or
organizational judgment.

=== The Three Structural Philosophies

Five Eyes nations use three distinct structural approaches to unclassified information control:

[cols="1,2,3"]
|===
| Model | Countries | Structure

| Hierarchical ladder
| Canada, New Zealand
| Multiple distinct tiers within non-classified information (Protected A/B/C; In-Confidence/Sensitive).
Tiers represent severity of injury if compromised.

| Flat tier + caveats
| United States
| Single unclassified tier (CUI) with orthogonal subject taxonomy and explicit dissemination controls.

| Flat tier + handling caveat
| United Kingdom, Australia, France
| Single official tier (OFFICIAL, Diffusion Restreinte) with a sensitivity marker
that triggers stricter access and handling rules.
|===

Understanding which model each partner nation uses is essential for designing label mappings
that represent intent accurately without forcing one nation's philosophy onto another.

== Per-Country Profiles

=== United States — CUI

*Program name*: Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) Program +
*Primary authority*: Executive Order 13556; 32 CFR Part 2002; NIST SP 800-171; NARA CUI Registry

The US CUI model formalizes all four axes:

* *Protection tier*: CUI is the single controlled tier within unclassified.
* *Subject taxonomy*: A national registry of categories and subcategories
  (e.g., CUI//SP-PRVCY for privacy data, CUI//CTI for controlled technical information).
* *Handling requirements*: Defined per category — some categories require encryption at rest,
  others carry specific transmission controls.
* *Dissemination restrictions*: Explicit Limited Dissemination Controls (LDCs) such as
  NOFORN, FED ONLY, and REL TO partner nations.

A fully qualified US CUI marking:

----
CUI//LAW-ENF//NOFORN
----

Meaning: CUI tier, Law Enforcement category, no foreign nationals.

In UMRS, CUI categories map to MCS category bits. This is the implemented baseline.

=== United Kingdom — GSCP

*Program name*: Government Security Classifications Policy (GSCP) +
*Primary authority*: Cabinet Office / GOV.UK

The UK scheme has three tiers (OFFICIAL, SECRET, TOP SECRET). Within the unclassified
range relevant to UMRS:

* *Protection tier*: OFFICIAL is the baseline for the vast majority of government business.
* *Sensitivity caveat*: OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE is not a separate classification level — it is
  a handling caveat applied to OFFICIAL information that needs tighter controls.
  It signals stricter access (need-to-know) and special handling.
* *Subject taxonomy*: No central national registry equivalent to the CUI Registry.
  Subject breakdown is organizational and mission-defined.
* *Dissemination restrictions*: Primarily need-to-know. Specific caveats (e.g., LOCSEN
  for location-sensitive material) exist but are not standardized to the same degree as US LDCs.

Structurally, UK unclassified is one tier (OFFICIAL) plus an optional sensitivity flag
(OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE) that triggers stricter dissemination and handling.

=== Canada — Protected A/B/C

*Program name*: Government of Canada security classification and designation system +
*Primary authority*: Canada.ca "Levels of security" (official Government of Canada guidance)

Canada has a clear vertical ladder within non-classified information:

* *UNCLASSIFIED* — routine government information.
* *PROTECTED A* — low injury if compromised.
* *PROTECTED B* — serious injury if compromised. The most common tier in coalition
  information-sharing contexts.
* *PROTECTED C* — extremely grave injury if compromised. Access tightly restricted.

Access is determined by security screening level plus need-to-know:

* Reliability Status → access to Protected A and B
* Enhanced screening → access to Protected A, B, and C

This is a vertical risk ladder, not a formal MLS dominance lattice. Someone cleared for
Protected C can access B and A with need-to-know, but this is administrative policy
rather than mathematically enforced lattice semantics.

Canada's Protected tiers mirror injury severity, not national-security status, which
is why they map cleanly to UMRS sensitivity levels:

----
Protected C ≈ serious injury → S2
Protected B ≈ moderate injury → S1
Protected A ≈ low injury → S1 (sub-category)
----

=== New Zealand — PSR

*Program name*: New Zealand Government Security Classification System / Protective Security Requirements (PSR) +
*Primary authority*: NZ Protective Security "Classification System"

New Zealand uses multiple tiers that include unclassified-style levels:

* *UNCLASSIFIED*
* *IN-CONFIDENCE* — moderate sensitivity; release could damage NZ interests or create
  legal, commercial, or reputational issues.
* *SENSITIVE* — greater harm if disclosed. Often expressed as SENSITIVE [handling
  instruction] (e.g., SENSITIVE BUDGET).

Higher levels (RESTRICTED, CONFIDENTIAL, SECRET, TOP SECRET) enter the national-security
classification range.

Access rules are role, need-to-know, and agency policy — not strict MLS dominance.
NZ is similar to Canada in spirit: a ladder within unclassified, with IN-CONFIDENCE and
SENSITIVE as meaningful intermediate tiers.

=== Australia — PSPF

*Program name*: Protective Security Policy Framework (PSPF) +
*Primary authority*: Official PSPF site ("PSPF Release 2025")

Australia follows the UK structural model within unclassified:

* *OFFICIAL* — routine government information.
* *OFFICIAL: Sensitive* — OFFICIAL information containing sensitive material needing
  extra protection, with access limited to need-to-know.

Handling requirements come from PSPF policy, not from a subject taxonomy registry.
Structurally, Australia looks like UK: baseline tier plus a sensitivity marker that
triggers stricter dissemination rules.

=== France — SGDSN / IGI 1300

*Program name*: Protection du secret de la défense nationale +
*Primary authority*: SGDSN IGI 1300

France focuses on distribution restriction rather than a subject taxonomy:

* *Unclassified baseline* — routine information.
* *Diffusion Restreinte (DR)* — not a classification level in France.
  It is explicitly described as restricting dissemination of non-classified information,
  without carrying the penal protections of classified defense secret.
* *Spécial France* — an additional caveat used in some contexts for direct
  citizenship-based distribution restrictions.

DR is best modeled as an unclassified baseline plus a restriction marker, with optional
extra caveats for specific dissemination constraints.

== The Key Architectural Insight

The US system is unique because it formalizes three layers that other countries leave informal:

. Subject taxonomy
. Standard dissemination caveats
. Category-specific handling requirements

Most other countries implement only:

* Protection tier
* Need-to-know
* Policy guidance

This means a system that works well for US CUI must be deliberately designed to avoid
imposing US taxonomy assumptions on partner-nation data.

== UMRS Label Model Mapping

The UMRS label model separates the four axes, which means it can represent all national
schemes without forcing one country's philosophy onto another.

The proposed mapping uses a sensitivity level as the tier axis and orthogonal attributes
for the remaining axes:

----
UMRS Label
 ├─ Sensitivity level (tier axis)
 ├─ Category set (domain/subject taxonomy — optional per country)
 ├─ Membership / dissemination restrictions
 └─ Handling controls
----

Conceptual label examples across partner nations:

----
S1:US:CUI:LAW_ENF:REL_TO_FBI
S1:NZ:SENSITIVE:POLICY
S1:CA:PROTECTED_B:PROCUREMENT
S1:UK:OFFICIAL_SENSITIVE
S1:AU:OFFICIAL_SENSITIVE
S1:FR:DR
----

S0 means the tier is essentially a single unclassified band — the subject taxonomy and
dissemination axes carry the protection semantics.

=== Important Consequence

Canada and New Zealand have vertical tiers that are administratively real — but they
do not enforce MLS dominance. Access is clearance plus need-to-know, not read-down/write-up
lattice semantics. Modeling them with a sensitivity level plus category sets and membership
controls is correct and technically sound:

* No dominance relationship is implied or enforced
* No read-down / read-up semantics
* Access decisions come from membership plus policy, not MLS level comparison

This keeps the SELinux MLS machinery simple while letting UMRS represent all national
models through categories and attributes.

== Current Status

CUI (US baseline) is the only implemented scheme. Partner nation support
is a planned architectural objective. The label abstraction layer is
designed to accommodate additional schemes without changes to the
enforcement core.

When partner scheme support is added, it will appear as:

* Configuration mappings in the `setrans.conf` equivalent for each scheme
* Reference documentation for each scheme's marking categories
* Integration tests demonstrating correct label translation

Compliance note: NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2 (3.1.3, 3.13.1) governs
information flow enforcement for CUI. CMMC 2.0 Level 2 (AC.L2-3.1.3)
maps this control to the maturity assessment model. Partner-nation
scheme support will be governed by the applicable bilateral agreement
or information sharing arrangement in effect for the deployment
environment.
