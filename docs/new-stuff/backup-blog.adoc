I have been in this industry long enough to remember when FOUO meant whatever
the person who printed the document wanted it to mean. Before 2010 there was
no unified framework for sensitive unclassified information — just a zoo of
incompatible agency markings, none of them backed by consistent legal or technical
protection requirements. FOUO, SBU, LES, SENSITIVE. Dozens of variations, none of
them enforceable in any mechanically verifiable sense.

Then Executive Order 13556 established the Controlled Unclassified Information
program in 2010 and unified all of that under one framework. It was a real step
forward. NARA became the executive agent. The markings got standardized.
NIST SP 800-171 published 110 controls across 14 families. Contractors started
putting CUI policies in their SSPs.

And then almost nothing changed for the next several years. The policy existed.
The training got delivered. The information kept leaking.

== The Sign That Replaced the Lock

Here is the thing about the CUI self-attestation era: it was not dishonest
because contractors were bad actors. It was dishonest because the incentive
structure made honesty expensive and irrelevant. DFARS 252.204-7012 required
contractors to attest compliance with NIST SP 800-171 and submit a score to
the Supplier Performance Risk System. There was no independent verification.
No third-party assessment. No mechanism to distinguish a contractor who had
actually implemented the controls from one who had checked the boxes and
moved on.

The result was predictable. Sensitive defense information — F-35 technical
specifications, submarine propulsion data, acquisition strategies — flowed
through contractor systems protected by little more than an optimistic
SPRS score and a sign on the metaphorical door that read "CUI: authorized
personnel only."

The classified world solved this problem differently. It solved it in the 1970s.
The reference monitor concept — introduced in the Anderson Report in 1972 and
formalized through the Orange Book TCSEC, Bell-LaPadula, and eventually
SELinux — is built on the recognition that administrative controls fail.
Humans forget. Humans get tired. Humans make mistakes under time pressure.
The kernel does not.

Bell-LaPadula's "no read up, no write down" is not a policy you post on the
wall. It is a formal model implemented in the operating system. When SELinux
enforces it, no process can read a file above its clearance level regardless
of what the human at the keyboard wants to do. That is the difference between
a sign and a lock.

CUI has had fifteen years of signs. CMMC 2.0 is finally demanding locks.

== The Assurance Pendulum Reaches Its Limit

If you have been in security long enough you recognize the pattern. The
software assurance pendulum has been swinging for fifty years between two
competing forces: engineering discipline on one side, market velocity on the
other. The formal methods era of the 1960s and 1970s gave us Ada, SPARK,
the Orange Book. Then the desktop revolution swung the pendulum hard toward
"ship fast, patch later." The fast era produced the broken era — the Morris
Worm, Code Red, SQL Slammer, Solar Sunrise. The broken era produced regulation.
Regulation without enforcement produced compliance theater.

What feels different about right now — and I do not say this lightly after
watching the cycle repeat — is that the pendulum may be losing its swing. The
enforcement mechanisms are becoming structural rather than advisory. CMMC 2.0
Final Rule effective December 2024 makes CUI compliance a contractual condition,
not a checkbox. The DFARS acquisition rule that cleared review in August 2025
integrates it into contract eligibility itself. The FAR CUI rule advancing in
2025 extends these requirements beyond the defense industrial base to all federal
contractors.

Simultaneously, the NSA, CISA, and the White House Office of the National Cyber
Director have declared memory-safe languages a national priority. Not a best
practice. A priority, with guidance explicitly deprecating C and C++ for new
security-critical development. The fast-era shortcuts are being encoded out of
existence by toolchains, contracts, and law at the same time.

== What UMRS Actually Is

I built UMRS in December 2025 as a direct response to this arc. Let me be
precise about what it is and what it is not, because the difference matters.

UMRS is not a reference monitor. The Linux kernel is the reference monitor.
The LSM framework and SELinux are the enforcement mechanism. UMRS is the
system that verifies the reference monitor is correctly configured, active,
and actually enforcing policy — not just configured to enforce it.

That distinction is not semantic hair-splitting. It is the core of why the
tool is useful. Configuration files describe intent. The kernel enforces
reality. Your `/etc/selinux/config` saying `SELINUX=enforcing` does not mean
SELinux is enforcing. If `/sys/fs/selinux/enforce` reads 0, you are running
permissive. Your policy exists. Your controls exist on paper. The enforcement
mechanism is bypassed.

UMRS queries the kernel. Always the kernel. Runtime state over configuration
artifacts. This is the Runtime Source of Truth principle, and it is why the
tool can tell you things that your configuration management tooling will not.

The codebase enforces this in Rust with `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]` in every
crate root. That annotation is not a policy statement. It is a compile-time
proof. `#![forbid]` cannot be overridden by any inner `#[allow]` — a fact
mechanically verifiable by any auditor who opens the source. Every memory
safety guarantee the tool makes is structurally enforced, not aspirationally
stated.

== Phase 1: The Sign Is Actually Good

Here is where I need to be honest about what Phase 1 delivers versus what
Phase 2 will deliver — because integrity matters more than marketing.

Phase 1, which is complete, loads CUI category labels into the Multi-Category
Security translation layer under targeted SELinux policy. Operators see
human-readable CUI handling requirement labels on files and processes. The
system makes CUI visible. You know what you are handling. The policy is present.
The human is informed.

This is awareness enforcement. It is genuinely valuable. Making CUI visible is
not a trivial problem — most organizations have no operator-facing view of what
handling requirements apply to the files their processes are touching.

But it is still a sign. The kernel is not yet enforcing the boundary in the
sense that Bell-LaPadula defines enforcement. A determined or inattentive user
can still do things the policy says they should not.

Phase 2 is the lock. MLS policy replaces targeted policy. The SELinux reference
monitor enforces CUI boundaries at the kernel level — no read up, no write down,
no process bypasses the label regardless of human intent. dm-crypt encrypts CUI
data at rest. IMA integrity measurement verifies CUI-handling binaries before
execution. Phase 2 is planned. It is not complete.

I will not tell you Phase 2 is done when it is not. This is a project built on
fifty years of engineering honesty, and that starts here.

== The Lineage That Justifies the Confidence

Why does a RHEL 10 Rust project have the standing to claim it is doing what
classified systems do? Because it is implementing the same formal model those
systems use, on the same kernel infrastructure, with the same enforcement
philosophy.

James Anderson's 1972 report defined the reference monitor: tamper-proof,
always invoked, small enough to verify. Bell and LaPadula formalized the
information flow model in 1973. The Orange Book codified these into evaluation
criteria. The work that became SELinux at the NSA in the 1990s implemented
them in a production Linux kernel. RHEL 4 shipped the first commercial SELinux
in 2005.

UMRS sits at the end of that lineage. It does not invent new ideas about
mandatory access control. It applies fifty years of proven ideas to the
specific, current, legally mandated problem of CUI protection on unclassified
Linux systems — implemented in a memory-safe language with no unsafe code,
no FFI shortcuts, and every design decision traceable to a requirement.

Every fast-era shortcut UMRS refuses to make has a CVE behind it. Every
assurance pattern it implements has a regulatory requirement driving it.
The standards are not arbitrary. The constraints are not bureaucratic
obstruction. They are the accumulated lessons of engineers who watched what
happens when software ships without them.

== What You Can Do Right Now

UMRS is open source. The Phase 1 implementation is on GitHub. If you are a
security engineer dealing with CMMC compliance or CUI handling on Linux, there
is concrete material to look at:

* The SELinux posture evaluation in the `umrs-selinux` crate — query the
  kernel runtime state, detect drift between configured and effective SELinux
  mode, and surface enforcement gaps as typed findings rather than log strings
* The MCS label integration showing CUI category mapping into the SELinux
  translation layer
* The high-assurance pattern library documenting the specific engineering
  patterns (Two-Path Independence, Fail-Closed, Non-Bypassability) and the
  NIST SP 800-53 controls they satisfy

If you are a Rust engineer who wants to see what `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]` in
a security-critical codebase actually looks like in practice — the parsing
patterns, the typed security contexts, the dual-parser validation — the code
is readable and the documentation explains the why, not just the what.

If you have been living with compliance theater and want to understand what
the kernel-enforcement alternative looks like: that is exactly what this
project is built to show you.

The lock is under construction. The blueprints are open.
