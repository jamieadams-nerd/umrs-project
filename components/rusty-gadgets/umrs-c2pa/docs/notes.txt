 What C2PA can detect                                                                                         
                                                                                                               
  The c2pa.hash.data assertion stores a SHA-256 hash of specific byte ranges of the asset. If those bytes      
  change after signing, verification fails with assertion.dataHash.mismatch. This covers:                      
                  
  - Pixel edits (Photoshop, etc.)                                                                              
  - Metadata changes (EXIF stripping/modification)
  - Re-compression or re-encoding                                                                              
  - Any byte-level modification to the protected regions                                                       
                                                                                                               
  This is deterministic and reliable — it's just a hash comparison.                                            
                                                                                                               
  ---             
  What C2PA cannot detect
                                                                                                               
  - Manifest stripping — someone can remove the entire manifest box. The image looks clean/unsigned, not
  tampered. C2PA has no way to re-attach a stripped manifest or prove one existed. The absence of credentials  
  is not proof of tampering; it might just be an unsigned image.
  - Re-capture attacks — take a screenshot of the image, photograph your monitor, or print-and-scan it. The new
   file has no manifest at all. The content is identical visually but the provenance chain is broken.          
  - Content outside the hash exclusions — the spec intentionally excludes certain regions (like the manifest
  box itself) from the hash. A carefully crafted attack targeting those exclusion regions could theoretically  
  modify bytes that aren't hashed.
                                                                                                               
  ---             
  The honest summary
                    
  C2PA is provenance infrastructure, not copy protection or tamper detection in the general sense. What it
  actually gives you is:                                                                                       
   
  "This specific file, as it exists right now, was signed by this identity, at this time, and has not been     
  byte-modified since."
                                                                                                               
  If the manifest is present and valid → you can trust the chain of custody.
  If the manifest is absent or invalid → you know nothing, either way.                                         
                                                                      
  It's much more like a chain of custody seal on evidence than a DRM system. The value is in the positive case,
   not in catching bad actors who simply strip the metadata.    

  signature_info.issuer — What It Actually Means
                                                                                                               
  The issuer is the CA that issued the signing certificate, not the entity that signed. There are two distinct
  fields:                                                                                                      
                  
  "signature_info": {
    "issuer": "Adobe Inc.",           ← the CA that issued the cert
    "cert_serial_number": "03:a1:",   ← which cert from that CA                                                
    "time": "2025-11-14T10:23:00Z"    ← when it was signed                                                     
  }                                                                                                            
                                                                                                               
  The actual signer identity is in the end-entity certificate itself (the CN= field). So issuer: "Adobe Inc."  
  means Adobe's CA vouches for whoever signed — it could be Lightroom, Firefly, or any Adobe product with a
  cert from that CA. It tells you who vouched for the signer, not who the signer is directly. The full cert is 
  embedded in the manifest and you can inspect the subject CN for the actual tool/entity name.

  ---
  The First Manifest in the Chain — Can You Assume Origin/Ownership?
                                                                                                               
  Cautiously yes, but with important caveats.
                                                                                                               
  The first manifest (the deepest ingredient in the chain, with no further ingredients of its own) represents  
  the original creation event. If it has:                                                                      
                                                                                                               
  - c2pa.created action                                                                                        
  - No ingredients
  - A signing cert from a camera manufacturer CA (Sony, Leica, Nikon, etc.)                                    
                                                                                                               
  ...then yes, you can reasonably assert: "this pixel data originated from this device, at this time, without  
  prior editing." That is as close to "owner at capture" as C2PA can express.                                  
                                                                                                               
  However:        
  - The first manifest proves creation identity, not ownership. The photographer who pressed the shutter owns
  the image legally — C2PA doesn't encode that distinction. It encodes who signed, which is the camera.        
  - If the first manifest was signed by a software tool (c2pa.created from Photoshop or Midjourney), that's the
   origin — AI-generated or synthetically created.                                                             
  - If there is no first manifest with c2pa.created — only c2pa.edited — the true origin is unknown or was     
  stripped.

  ---
  The Two Cases You Described                                                                                  
                             
  Case 1 — No existing manifest:
  - File arrives unsigned                                                                                      
  - You sign it with c2pa.acquired or c2pa.published action (not c2pa.created — you're not claiming you made
  it)                                                                                                          
  - Your manifest says: "we received this file, we make no claim about its origin, here is its hash as it      
  arrived on our system, signed by us"                                                                    
  - Downstream recipients can verify the file hasn't changed since you touched it                              
                  
  Case 2 — File already has a manifest:                                                                        
  - You read and display the existing chain
  - You add a new manifest on top, embedding the existing store as an ingredient                               
  - Your action: c2pa.published or a custom label                               
  - Your assertion: "we received this, made no modifications, here is the hash"                                
  - The c2pa.hash.data in your manifest covers the exact same bytes as the previous signer's hash — so if      
  someone later verifies your manifest, the hash comparison proves the pixels are identical to what the        
  previous signer certified                                                                                    
                                                                                                               
  ---                                                                                                          
  What the Downstream Recipient Sees

  [Original signer or camera]       ← ingredient (if present)
    action: c2pa.created / unknown                                                                             
    hash: 0xABCD...                                                                                            
          ↓                                                                                                    
  [Your system]                                                                                                
    action: c2pa.published                                                                                     
    claim_generator: "your-system/1.0"                                                                         
    hash: 0xABCD...                 ← same hash, proving you changed nothing
    signed by: your cert                                                                                       
                                                                                                               
  They verify your manifest → hash matches → they know the pixels are identical to what you received. They     
  verify the ingredient → they see whatever provenance existed before you. The chain is intact and auditable.  
                                                                                                               
  ---             
  One Important Detail
                      
  For Case 1, you should use something like:
                                                                                                               
  {
    "action": "c2pa.published",                                                                                
    "reason": "Accepted as-is; origin unknown; no modifications made."
  }                                                                                                            
   
  Do not use c2pa.created — that asserts you are the originator of the content, which is a false claim and     
  would be misleading to downstream verifiers.

  ---                                                                                                          
  Trust Levels — How They Actually Work
                                       
  You used the term "trust anchor" — that is the correct PKI term. Here's the full picture:
                                                                                                               
  Each hop in the chain gets one of these trust evaluations independently:                                     
                                                                                                               
  ┌───────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐  
  │  Status   │                                           Meaning                                           │
  ├───────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ TRUSTED   │ Cert chain leads to a root CA in the C2PA Trust List. Cryptographically valid.              │
  ├───────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ UNTRUSTED │ Signature is cryptographically valid, but the CA is not on the Trust List (self-signed,     │  
  │           │ private CA, test cert). Valid chain, unknown identity.                                      │  
  ├───────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤  
  │ INVALID   │ Signature verification failed, or asset hash doesn't match. Something broke.                │  
  ├───────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤  
  │ REVOKED   │ Cert was revoked by the issuing CA (OCSP/CRL check)                                         │
  └───────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘  
                  
  The trust anchor is the root CA certificate. The C2PA Trust List is a curated set of root CAs maintained by  
  the C2PA organization — camera makers, Adobe, etc. apply to get their CA added.
                                                                                                               
  For UMRS in test mode: your self-signed cert will show UNTRUSTED — which is correct and honest. It means     
  "valid signature, unknown identity." In production, customers get a cert from a recognized CA and their
  entries show TRUSTED.                                                                                        
                  
  The cryptographic strength (key size, algorithm) is separate from trust level — it's about whether the math  
  is sound, not whether you trust who did the math.

  FIPS Algorithms — C2PA's Allowed Set vs. FIPS
                                                                                                               
  C2PA spec allows exactly these algorithms:
                                                                                                               
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┬─────────────────────┬───────────────────────┐
  │            Algorithm            │ FIPS 140-3 Approved │      Safe Choice      │                            
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤
  │ es256 — ECDSA P-256 + SHA-256   │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Recommended        │
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤
  │ es384 — ECDSA P-384 + SHA-384   │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Recommended        │                            
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤                            
  │ es512 — ECDSA P-521 + SHA-512   │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Recommended        │                            
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤                            
  │ ps256 — RSA-PSS 2048+ + SHA-256 │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Acceptable         │
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤                            
  │ ps384 — RSA-PSS + SHA-384       │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Acceptable         │
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤                            
  │ ps512 — RSA-PSS + SHA-512       │ ✅ Yes              │ ✅ Acceptable         │
  ├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┤                            
  │ ed25519 — EdDSA Curve25519      │ ⚠️  Conditional      │ ❌ Avoid on FIPS RHEL │
  └─────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────┴───────────────────────┘                            
   
  ed25519 is in FIPS 186-5 (2023) on paper, but RHEL's OpenSSL FIPS provider often doesn't expose it — it      
  varies by RHEL version and provider build. Stick with es256 or es384. They're the most widely supported in
  the C2PA ecosystem anyway (Adobe, Sony, etc. all use ECDSA).                                                 
                  
  Does your FIPS choice impact others? No. The algorithm is recorded in your manifest and used to verify your  
  signature only. Each signer in the chain used whatever algorithm they chose. Verifiers handle all the allowed
   algorithms. Your choice of es256 doesn't constrain what Sony used upstream.

  Vendored OpenSSL + FIPS on RHEL — The Tension
                                                                                                               
  This is the one real conflict to flag. Vendored and FIPS on RHEL are in tension:
                                                                                                               
  ┌───────────────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────┐   
  │             Approach              │        Portability         │            FIPS Validated             │   
  ├───────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤   
  │ openssl crate, features =         │ ✅ Builds anywhere,        │ ❌ NOT the NIST-validated module —    │
  │ ["vendored"]                      │ statically linked          │ it's a source build                   │
  ├───────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤   
  │ openssl crate, no vendored (links │ ❌ Requires system OpenSSL │ ✅ Uses RHEL's validated FIPS module  │
  │  system OpenSSL)                  │  present                   │                                       │   
  └───────────────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────┘
                                                                                                               
  The right solution: make it a compile-time feature flag.                                                     
   
  # Cargo.toml                                                                                                 
  [features]      
  default         = ["vendored-openssl"]
  vendored-openssl = ["openssl/vendored"]   # portable, hermetic, not FIPS-validated
  system-openssl  = []                      # links system OpenSSL — use on FIPS RHEL                          
                                                                                                               
  [dependencies]                                                                                               
  openssl = { version = "0.10", optional = true }                                                              
  c2pa    = { version = "0.78.6", default-features = false, features = ["openssl", "file_io"] }                
                                                                                                               
  Build for FIPS RHEL:                                                                                         
  cargo build --no-default-features --features system-openssl                                                  
                                                             
  Build for portable/test:
  cargo build  # uses vendored by default                                                                      
                                         
  This way the prototype works anywhere out of the box, and customers deploying on FIPS RHEL compile with      
  system-openssl to use the validated module. Document it clearly — this is a sysadmin-level decision.

What "remote" means in C2PA context

  1. Trust List checking (online, remote)
  The C2PA org maintains a live list of approved root CAs. If you check against it, a Sony or Adobe cert shows
  TRUSTED. Without it, the same signature still verifies cryptographically — it just shows UNTRUSTED (valid
  signature, unknown CA). For your ingest-dropbox use case, the distinction doesn't matter much — you're recording
  chain of custody, not gatekeeping on public trust.

  2. OCSP / CRL revocation (online, remote)
  Real-time check whether a signing cert has been revoked. Not in scope for the prototype per the plan, and the c2pa
  crate doesn't do it by default either.

  3. TSA (Time Stamp Authority) (online, optional)
  The tsa_url config. This is the only "remote" thing currently wired in. It's optional — if omitted, timestamps come
   from the local system clock. Useful but not required.

  ---
  Do people need root CAs?

  No — not to sign. You just need any cert + private key. Self-signed works fine. The difference is only what
  downstream validators display:

  ┌───────────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────┬─────────────────┐
  │          Cert source          │ Trust shown to verifier │ Crypto validity │
  ├───────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Self-signed (test mode)       │ UNTRUSTED               │ ✅ valid        │
  ├───────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ Private CA (internal PKI)     │ UNTRUSTED               │ ✅ valid        │
  ├───────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ DigiCert / recognized C2PA CA │ TRUSTED                 │ ✅ valid        │
  └───────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────┴─────────────────┘

  For UMRS ingest — where you're stamping "we received this file, hash X, at time T" — UNTRUSTED is honest and
  sufficient. It means "signature is mathematically valid, we just don't vouch for who issued the cert." Downstream
  recipients can still verify the file hasn't changed since UMRS signed it.

  You only need a cert from a recognized CA if you want external tools (like Adobe's online verifier) to show a green
   TRUSTED badge. That's a deployment decision, not a code one — the config path is already there, you just point
  cert_chain and private_key at a proper cert when/if you get one.

● Great questions. Let me untangle this.

  "Ephemeral" just means the key is thrown away after signing. The output file is a perfectly standard C2PA-signed
  JPEG. Any C2PA validator (Adobe's, the c2pa-rs tool, whatever) can read and verify the signature. The only thing
  "test mode" about it is that the cert is self-signed — no chain of trust to a real CA. The cryptography is identical
  either way.

  Production signing uses your real cert + key from the config file. Same code path, same embedding format, just a
  persistent identity backed by a CA.

  On Ed25519 and FIPS: Your concern is valid.

  - FIPS 186-5 (2023) added EdDSA/Ed25519 as approved
  - FIPS 140-3 is the module validation standard — what matters is whether the specific validated module on your RHEL
  box includes Ed25519 in its validated boundary
  - Most RHEL OpenSSL FIPS modules were validated under 186-4, which does not include EdDSA

  update in action — Red Hat's newer module includes EdDSA.

  ┌───────────────┬──────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────┬────────────────┬───────────┐

  ├───────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────┤
  │ ES256 (P-256) │ Yes              │ Yes                                │ Yes — required │ Universal │
  ├───────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────┤
  │ ES384 (P-384) │ Yes              │ Yes                                │ Yes — required │ Universal │
  ├───────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────┤
