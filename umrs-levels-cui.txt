Cui

Yes. CUI is a real and formally defined data classification, but it is not a new “security level” in the MLS / classification sense.

Here are the facts, clean and evidence-based.

What CUI is
CUI = Controlled Unclassified Information

It was created to standardize how the U.S. government handles sensitive but unclassified information that previously went by many inconsistent labels (FOUO, SBU, LES, etc.).

CUI applies to information, not to people, systems, or clearance levels.

Authoritative source
CUI is defined by Executive Order 13556 (2010).

The official public authority is the National Archives (NARA) CUI program.

Primary public references (these are real, citable):

• Executive Order 13556 – “Controlled Unclassified Information”
• 32 CFR Part 2002 – Controlled Unclassified Information
• NARA CUI Registry:
https://www.archives.gov/cui

Those are the canonical sources.

What CUI is NOT
CUI is not:
	•	a classification level like Confidential / Secret / Top Secret
	•	an MLS label in the traditional sense
	•	a clearance
	•	a replacement for classified handling rules

Instead, it is a handling framework for unclassified data that still requires protection.

How CUI fits into security engineering
CUI mainly matters because it drives system requirements, not classification logic.

Key impacts:

• Systems processing CUI must comply with NIST SP 800-171
• Federal contracts often require CUI protection clauses
• CUI handling shows up heavily in DFARS and civilian agency contracts

If you see CUI in technical contexts, it is usually shorthand for:
“This system must meet 800-171 requirements.”

Difference between CUI and MLS
This matters given your background.

MLS world:
	•	Formal security levels (U, C, S, TS)
	•	Mandatory labels
	•	Lattice enforcement
	•	Strong information flow controls

CUI world:
	•	Policy-driven handling rules
	•	Access control + auditing
	•	Encryption at rest and in transit
	•	No lattice, no dominance relation

CUI does not introduce a new MLS label.

Common misunderstanding
People sometimes say:
“We have a new security level called CUI”

That is incorrect language, even if it’s common in meetings.

Correct phrasing:
	•	“The system processes CUI”
	•	“The data is designated as CUI”
	•	“CUI handling requirements apply”

Why it feels like a “new level”
Because:
	•	It comes with mandatory protections
	•	It is contractually enforced
	•	It often sits between Public and Classified in risk discussions

But legally and technically, it is still unclassified data.

Short, defensible summary

It is a standardized category of unclassified information with legally enforceable handling requirements, defined by EO 13556 and implemented via NIST SP 800-171.

1. Can you have MLS levels in an unclassified system?

In unclassified MLS, labels usually represent:

• data ownership boundaries

⸻

2. Does CUI include proprietary?

No.

CUI and Proprietary are different authorities.

CUI:
• Created by Executive Order 13556
• Applies only when the government designates information as CUI
• Governed by NARA and implemented via NIST SP 800-171
• Is unclassified but controlled government information

Proprietary:
• Owned and designated by a company
• Covers trade secrets, IP, internal business data
• Not governed by EO 13556
• May exist entirely outside government data flows

Important consequences:

• Proprietary data is not automatically CUI
• CUI data is not automatically proprietary
• They overlap only when a contract says so

So from an MLS standpoint: CUI and Proprietary must be separate labels.

⸻

3. ITAR / EAR: they also do NOT fall under CUI

Export-controlled data (ITAR / EAR):

• Is regulated by State / Commerce, not NARA
• Has citizenship and dissemination constraints
• Often stricter than generic CUI
• Is legally distinct from CUI

Some ITAR data may also be marked as CUI in practice, but the legal authority is different, so the MLS label should remain distinct.

• Public or releasable

Level 1: CONTROLLED
• Internal-only
• No statutory regime
• Corporate sharing allowed

Level 2: PROPRIETARY
• Company IP / trade secrets
• Need-to-know
• Owner-restricted

Level 3: CUI
• Government-designated CUI
• NIST 800-171 applies
• Contract-bound handling

Level 4: EXPORT_CONTROLLED
• ITAR / EAR
• Citizenship restrictions
• Strict dissemination limits

Edit the SELinux config:

vi /etc/selinux/config

SELINUX=enforcing
SELINUXTYPE=mls

grep SELINUX /boot/grub2/grubenv

grub2-mkconfig -o /boot/grub2/grub.cfg

Should say:
Enforcing

sestatus | grep “Policy from”
Should say:
Policy from config file: mls

Check MLS support:

cat /sys/fs/selinux/mls
Should return:
1

If that file says 0, MLS is not active.

	1.	What “system low” and “system high” really mean

In an MLS system, “system low” and “system high” are conceptual:
	•	System low = the lowest sensitivity label that any subject or object in your system can validly have.
	•	System high = the highest sensitivity label that any subject or object in your system can validly have.

On an SELinux MLS box, that’s implemented as:
	•	The minimum and maximum MLS ranges you configure for:
	•	the kernel / policy
	•	user roles
	•	system services (daemons)
	•	login users

So if you decide:
	•	System low  = s0
	•	System high = s15

then in practice you’re saying, “nothing in this system ever goes below s0 or above s15.”
	2.	Using s0 as system low and s15 as system high

Using s0 for system low is normal and intuitive:
	•	s0 feels like “public / unrestricted / bottom of the world”.
	•	Everything else is “above” that.

Using s15 for system high is also fine:
	•	MLS levels are just ordered labels to SELinux; s15 could be your top.
	•	The only requirement is that your “high” really is the max that anything ever uses.

Then you hang your business levels in between:

Example (roughly matching what we’ve been sketching)
	•	s0  = UNRES (Unrestricted / Public)
	•	s1  = PRIVACT (Privacy Act / generic PII)
	•	s2  = CUI-GEN (General CUI)
	•	s3  = CUI-EXPORT (Export-controlled / ITAR-lite, EAR-sensitive)
	•	s4  = CUI-FOCI (Foreign-controlled info, stricter CUI category)
	•	…
	•	s15 = SYS-HIGH (the “top” for this system – maybe the most sensitive combo of CUI and PII you intend to host)

You do NOT have to fill all levels. You could easily skip some to allow future expansion:
	•	s0  = UNRES
	•	s2  = PRIVACT
	•	s4  = CUI-GEN
	•	s6  = CUI-EXPORT
	•	s8  = CUI-SPECIAL-1
	•	s10 = CUI-SPECIAL-2
	•	s15 = SYS-HIGH

The kernel doesn’t care if you leave “gaps”; it only cares about ordering.
	3.	Where system low / system high get enforced

Practically, you make your choice “real” by:
	•	Labeling the core system files and daemons with ranges that sit within [s0 – s15].
	•	Setting each SELinux user’s MLS range appropriately.
	•	Making sure no file or process is ever created outside that range (or if it is, it’s relabeled back in-range by policy / admin action).

Common pattern you and I both know from TS/SCI worlds:
	•	“System high” is basically the max label any process or file can legitimately take.
	•	Most daemons run in a range that includes what they need, but not necessarily full [low–high] unless they truly must see everything (e.g., auditd or a trusted guard).

So, in your unclassified CUI system:
	•	You could choose to have:
	•	auditd, logging, and the guard processes run with a range s0–s15 (because they need to see events from everywhere).
	•	ordinary user shells: something like s0–s2 (UNRES through PRIVACT/CUI-GEN).
	•	special “export control officer” account: s0–s3 or s0–s4.
	•	The important part is that no user or daemon casually gets s0–s15 unless it is truly a system-wide TCB component.

	4.	Do we “need” s0 and s15, or could we keep using just s0–s4?

You could absolutely stick with:
	•	s0 = UNRES
	•	s1 = PRIVACT / PII
	•	s2 = CUI-GEN
	•	s3 = CUI-EXPORT
	•	s4 = SYSTEM-HIGH

and treat s4 as your “high.” Technically it’s simpler.

Why I like your “s0 and s15” idea anyway:
	•	It mirrors how a lot of RTB / MLS policies are mentally structured (big top and bottom).
	•	It gives you “slack space” for future categories and business changes without relabeling everything.
	•	It lets you conceptually separate “business high” (say s4) from “system high” (s15) if you ever want a reserved level that only TCB components can touch.

	5.	One subtle thing: “system high” as a concept vs a concrete level

Some people treat “system high” not as a single level, but as:
	•	“whatever the current highest label actually used in the deployed configuration is”.

You’re leaning toward a concrete label (s15 = system high). That’s fine and often easier to document:
	•	Policy doc: “System high for this MLS system is s15. No user account is assigned a range that includes s15. Only specific trusted components (X, Y, Z) may have ranges that touch s15 if required by design.”

	6.	My recommendation given everything we’ve discussed

Given your goal (real-world, unclassified, CUI+/PII-oriented MLS system):
	•	Yes: use s0 as system low.
	•	Yes: reserve a top end, like s15, as system high.
	•	Put your active business levels low in the range, e.g.:
s0  = UNRES
s1  = PRIVACT (Privacy Act / generic PII; no HIPAA)
s2  = CUI-GEN
s3  = CUI-EXPORT / ITAR-ish
s4  = CUI-SPECIAL-1 (if you ever need it)
…
s15 = SYS-HIGH (reserved / TCB only)
	•	Make sure your SELinux user ranges, daemon contexts, and labeling rules all stay inside s0–s15.
